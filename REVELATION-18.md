## Revelation 18

---

### [Rev 18:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-1.htm) — "And after these things I saw another angel..."

**Text**: "And after these things I saw another angel come down from heaven, having great power; and the earth was lightened with his glory."

**Exegesis**: The phrase "after these things" (Gr. *meta tauta*) marks a literary seam, transitioning from the prostitute-Babylon vision of ch. 17 (which explained the political mechanics of Babylon's fall via the ten kings) to the ch. 18 proclamation of that fall in fuller detail. This angel is distinct from the interpreting angel of ch. 17 — he arrives independently from God with judicial-evangelistic authority (*exousian megalēn*, "great authority/power"). His luminous advent — the entire earth illuminated with his glory — is no mere theatrical detail: it is the precursor glory that directly dims and outshines Babylon's own glamorous splendor. The commentary (Stefanovic) reads this as an eschatological parallel to the latter-rain outpouring: the worldwide illumination anticipates the final proclamation of the everlasting gospel (cf. [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)–12; 18:1–4). His purpose is not to gloat over the fallen but to announce God's triumph and issue the final appeal to exit Babylon. His proclamation brings to its culmination the warning sequence of the three angels of Rev. 14.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)–12; 14:8; 16:19; 17:16–17; [2 Tim. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/3-5.htm).

### [Rev 18:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-2.htm) — "Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen..."

**Text**: "And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird."

**Exegesis**: The double cry "Fallen, fallen" (Gr. *epesen epesen*) is a direct quotation of [Isaiah 21:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/21-9.htm): "Fallen, fallen is Babylon; and all the images of her gods are shattered on the ground." It also repeats the second angel's warning of [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm). The double aorist (past tense used for a future event) is a Hebrew prophetic perfect — the fall of end-time Babylon is so certain that it is announced as already accomplished. The repetition intensifies the sense of inevitability and provides assurance to God's people that the judgment is imminent. The desolation imagery — Babylon becoming the dwelling place of *daimoniōn* (demons), every unclean spirit, every unclean and hateful bird — is drawn directly from OT oracles against Edom ([Isa. 34:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-11.htm)–15), Nineveh ([Zeph. 2:13](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/2-13.htm)–15), and especially ancient Babylon. Isaiah prophesied: "Desert creatures will lie down there ... ostriches also will live there, and shaggy goats will frolic there. Hyenas will howl in their fortified towers" ([Isa. 13:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-21.htm)–22). Jeremiah echoed: "The desert creatures will live there along with jackals" ([Jer. 50:39](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-39.htm)); "Babylon will become a heap of ruins, a haunt of jackals" ([Jer. 51:37](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-37.htm)). This imagery of wild, unclean habitation signals total, irreversible desolation — no human being will ever live there again. The historical pattern of ancient Babylon's fall becomes the typological template guaranteeing end-time Babylon's fall: as God acted then, so he will act again.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 13:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-19.htm)–22; 21:9; 34:11–15; [Jer. 50:39](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-39.htm); 51:37; [Zeph. 2:13](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/2-13.htm)–15; [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 16:19.

### [Rev 18:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-3.htm) — "For all nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication..."

**Text**: "For all nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication, and the kings of the earth have committed fornication with her, and the merchants of the earth are waxed rich through the abundance of her delicacies."

**Exegesis**: Verse 3 gives the threefold cause of Babylon's fall. First, the nations have been made drunk (*pepōken*) with the wine of the wrath of her fornication — an image of religious seduction and spiritual intoxication (cf. [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 17:2). Drunkenness in this metaphor means the nations can no longer reason clearly; they are blind to Babylon's ultimate insecurity and to God as the only source of real security. Second, the kings of the earth (end-time governing political powers) have *eporneusaan* (committed fornication) with her — an illicit partnership between institutional religion and state power (developed fully in ch. 17). Third — and this is a new dimension not present in ch. 17 — the merchants of the earth have grown wealthy (*eploutēsan*) through the power (*ek tēs dynameōs*) of her luxuries (*strēnous*). This third element introduces the economic dimension of Babylon's global influence: material wealth and commercial prosperity are the motivation that blinds the world's economic leaders to Babylon's true nature. Babylon promises security and wealth; she delivers seduction and ruin. The merchants here are best understood figuratively as the commercial distributors of Babylon's spiritual merchandise — her doctrines and policies sold to kings and peoples.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 17:2; 18:9, 15, 23; [Jer. 51:7](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-7.htm).

### [Rev 18:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-4.htm) — "Come out of her, my people..."

**Text**: "And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues."

**Exegesis**: A second heavenly voice (not the angel of vv. 1–3 but another divine voice — possibly Christ himself, since God calls them "my people") interrupts the proclamation of judgment with an urgent pastoral appeal. This is the pivot of the entire chapter; Leon Morris calls it "the key to the whole chapter." John is not gloating; he is appealing. The call echoes Jeremiah's appeal to Jews in literal Babylon: "Flee from the midst of Babylon, and each of you save his life! Do not be destroyed in her punishment, for this is the Lord's time of vengeance" ([Jer. 51:6](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-6.htm); cf. 50:8; 51:45). The parallel also invokes the story of Lot in Sodom (Gen. 19): Lot was not a participant in Sodom's sins, yet his identification with the city would have destroyed him had he not been urged to leave before the fire fell. The call presupposes that sincere, God-fearing people are still within Babylon, wavering, perhaps unaware of their identification with the apostate system. This is the last opportunity to escape Babylon's fate. The warning is twofold: (a) to avoid being *sygkoinōnēsēte* (sharing in, being joint-partakers of) Babylon's sins, and (b) to avoid receiving her plagues. Identity with Babylon means complicity in her guilt and solidarity in her punishment. This verse stands as the climactic appeal that consummates the three-angel warning messages of Rev. 14.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 50:8](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-8.htm); 51:6, 45; Gen. 19; [Isa. 48:20](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/48-20.htm); 52:11; [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)–12; [2 Cor. 6:17](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/6-17.htm).

### [Rev 18:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-5.htm) — "For her sins have reached unto heaven..."

**Text**: "For her sins have reached unto heaven, and God hath remembered her iniquities."

**Exegesis**: The language echoes [Jeremiah 51:9](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-9.htm): "For her judgment has reached to heaven and towers up to the very skies." The image of sins "reaching" or "accumulating" (*ekolouthēsan*; lit. "have followed, have piled up") unto heaven employs a spatial metaphor of moral weight: the accumulation of Babylon's unrighteousness has reached the very throne of God, demanding divine response. This is the same conceptual framework as [Gen. 18:20](https://biblehub.com/genesis/18-20.htm)–21 where the sin of Sodom "cries out" to heaven and God comes down to investigate, and as the tower of Babel ([Gen. 11:4](https://biblehub.com/genesis/11-4.htm)) where human presumption reaches upward and is met by divine judgment. The phrase "God hath remembered her iniquities" (*emnēsthē ho theos ta adikēmata autēs*) is covenantal and judicial language — God's "remembering" is not a mere cognitive act but the triggering of judicial action (cf. [Rev. 16:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-19.htm): "great Babylon came in remembrance before God"). Paul's parallel: the stubborn and unrepentant are "storing up wrath for themselves in the day of wrath and revelation of the righteous judgment of God" ([Rom. 2:5](https://biblehub.com/romans/2-5.htm)–6). Babylon has exhausted divine patience; the investigation is complete.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:9](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-9.htm); [Gen. 11:4](https://biblehub.com/genesis/11-4.htm); 18:20–21; [Rev. 16:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-19.htm); [Rom. 2:5](https://biblehub.com/romans/2-5.htm)–6; [Ps. 109:14](https://biblehub.com/psalms/109-14.htm)–15.

### [Rev 18:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-6.htm) — "Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double..."

**Text**: "Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works: in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double."

**Exegesis**: The divine address now shifts to the agents of judgment, commanding *retributive* justice. The Greek is forceful: *diplosate autē ta dipla* — "double unto her the double," a superlative expression of full, complete recompense. The double-recompense (*diplosmos*) is a well-established OT legal concept: the law of Moses required anyone responsible for stolen property to repay it twice over ([Exod. 22:4](https://biblehub.com/exodus/22-4.htm), 7, 9). The prophets used "double" as an idiom for punishment or reward in full measure — not necessarily literally twice, but equivalent in weight on God's scales of justice (cf. Meredith Kline: "Babylon's iniquities were to be balanced by their equal weight of punishment in God's scales of justice"). Isaiah declared Jerusalem "received of the Lord's hand double for all her sins" ([Isa. 40:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/40-2.htm)). Jeremiah: "Bring on them a day of disaster, and crush them with twofold destruction" ([Jer. 17:18](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/17-18.htm)). The cup metaphor — "in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double" — reverses Babylon's own weapon against her: she used the cup of the wine of fornication to intoxicate the nations (v. 3); now God mixes the full measure of wrath into her own cup. The principle is Jesus' own: "The measure you give will be the measure you get" ([Matt. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/7-2.htm)). The judgment fits the crime precisely.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 137:8](https://biblehub.com/psalms/137-8.htm); [Jer. 16:18](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/16-18.htm); 17:18; 50:29; 51:24; [Isa. 40:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/40-2.htm); 61:7; [Exod. 22:4](https://biblehub.com/exodus/22-4.htm), 7, 9; [Zech. 9:12](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/9-12.htm); [Matt. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/7-2.htm); [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm); 17:4.

### [Rev 18:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-7.htm) — "How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously..."

**Text**: "How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow."

**Exegesis**: This verse articulates the *lex talionis* (law of retaliation) principle in its theological depth: the measure of her self-glorification (*edoxasen heautēn*) and luxury (*estrēniasen*) becomes the precise measure of her torment and mourning. The arrogant boast — "I sit as a queen, and am not a widow, and I will never see mourning" — is a near-verbatim echo of Isaiah's oracle against ancient Babylon: "You said, 'I shall be a queen forever'... who says in your heart, 'I am, and there is no one besides me. I shall not sit as a widow, nor shall I know loss of children'" ([Isa. 47:7](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-7.htm)–9). The claim "I sit as a queen" (*kathēmai basilissa*) is Babylon's arrogation of divine prerogatives: in Revelation, glory (*doxa*) belongs exclusively to God ([Rev. 14:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-7.htm); 15:4; 19:1). Self-glorification is the ultimate act of idolatry — claiming for oneself what is God's alone. The term "widow" (*chēra*) is significant: in antiquity, a widow was one bereft of protector and provision, utterly vulnerable. Babylon's boast that she is no widow is a claim of total self-sufficiency — she needs no one. This arrogance is the theological root of her condemnation. Her self-sufficiency also recalls the boast of the Laodicean church ([Rev. 3:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-17.htm)): "I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing."

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 47:7](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-7.htm)–9; [Rev. 3:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-17.htm); 14:7; 15:4; 19:1; [Zeph. 2:15](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/2-15.htm); [Ezek. 28:2](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/28-2.htm).

### [Rev 18:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-8.htm) — "Therefore shall her plagues come in one day..."

**Text**: "Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong is the Lord God who judgeth her."

**Exegesis**: The "therefore" (*dia touto*) makes the logical connection explicit: Babylon's arrogance directly causes her sudden catastrophic destruction. The plagues — *thanatos* (death), *penthos* (mourning/grief), *limos* (famine) — are the opposite of everything she boasted: she claimed to be queen, not widow; she will receive death and mourning. She claimed never to see sorrow; she will receive famine. The inversion is total. "In one day" (*en mia hēmera*) echoes [Isaiah 47:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-9.htm) — ancient Babylon's fall came "in one day, suddenly." The "one day" here likely represents the entirety of the seven last plagues of Rev. 16, compressed into a single unified judgment event. The burning with fire (cf. [Rev. 17:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-16.htm)) evokes the OT punishment for a priest's daughter who commits prostitution ([Lev. 21:9](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/21-9.htm)) and for apostasy more broadly ([Lev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/20-14.htm); [Ezek. 16:38](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-38.htm)–41; 23:22–29) — all of which John has used as the template for Babylon's punishment as a spiritual prostitute. The closing declaration is the capstone of the unit: *hoti ischuros Kyrios ho Theos ho krinōn autēn* — "for strong/mighty is the Lord God who judges her." The word "judges" (*krinōn*) is an aorist participle, indicating completed action: the judgment has already been rendered; the sentence is already pronounced; the execution is now imminent. Against the boast of Babylon's self-sufficiency stands the irresistible strength of the Lord God who has already judged her.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 47:7](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-7.htm)–9; Rev. 16 (seven last plagues); [Rev. 17:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-16.htm); [Lev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/20-14.htm); 21:9; [Ezek. 16:38](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-38.htm)–41; [2 Thess. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-4.htm).

### [Rev 18:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-9.htm) — "And the kings of the earth...shall bewail her..."

**Text**: "And the kings of the earth, who have committed fornication and lived deliciously with her, shall bewail her, and lament for her, when they shall see the smoke of her burning,"

**Exegesis**: The chapter now shifts from proclamation and appeal (vv. 1–8) to a series of three mournful *dirges* or funeral laments (*thrēnoi*) — the formal genre of ancient Near Eastern lament over fallen cities. Three groups mourn sequentially: kings (vv. 9–10), merchants (vv. 11–17a), and shipmasters/sailors (vv. 17b–19). Each dirge concludes with the same refrain: "Woe, woe, the great city... for in one hour [your judgment has come]." The dirge form itself is significant: these are funeral songs, songs sung over the dead. Babylon is already dead before her mourners begin to speak. The kings of the earth are those governing political powers who had *eporneusaan* (committed fornication) and *estrēniasant* (lived luxuriously) with her — their lament is not for Babylon herself but for their own loss of power, security, and prestige. The image of watching smoke ascending from Babylon recalls Abraham watching the smoke of burning Sodom and Gomorrah ([Gen. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/genesis/19-28.htm)) — a typological connection OT prophets had already made: "Babylon, the beauty of kingdoms... will be as when God overthrew Sodom and Gomorrah" ([Isa. 13:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-19.htm)); "no man will live there, nor will any son of man reside in it" ([Jer. 50:40](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-40.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/genesis/19-28.htm); [Isa. 13:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-19.htm); [Jer. 50:40](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-40.htm); [Rev. 17:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-1.htm)–2, 12; 8:13; 9:12; 11:14.

### [Rev 18:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-10.htm) — "Standing afar off for the fear of her torment..."

**Text**: "Standing afar off for the fear of her torment, saying, Alas, alas that great city Babylon, that mighty city! for in one hour is thy judgment come."

**Exegesis**: The kings stand *apo makrothen* — "from afar," at a safe distance — *dia ton phobon tou basanismou autēs* ("because of the fear of her torment"). Their distance is not reverence but terror: they fear that Babylon's fate is contagious, that they will share in the destruction raining down on her. The *ouai ouai* — "Woe, woe" — is the mournful exclamation of the dirge, found seven times in Revelation (8:13; 9:12; 11:14; 12:12; 18:10, 16, 19), corresponding to the seven-woe structure of the book. They address her as "the great city" (*hē polis hē megalē*) and "the mighty city" (*hē polis hē ischyra*) — titles that contrast painfully with her sudden ruin. The phrase "in one hour" (*en mia hōra*) appears three times in the lament section (vv. 10, 17, 19), emphasizing the shocking speed and totality of the judgment. It connects to [Rev. 17:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-12.htm) where "one hour" denotes the brief moment when the ten kings, who once served Babylon, turn against her and destroy her. The realization that judgment has come (*ēlthen hē krisis sou*) — not "is coming" but "has come" — marks the kings' dawning recognition that God's righteous judgment has been executed.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 17:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-12.htm); 8:13; 9:12; 11:14; 12:12; [Ezek. 26:17](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/26-17.htm); [Isa. 13:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-19.htm); [Jer. 50:40](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/50-40.htm).

### [Rev 18:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-11.htm) — "And the merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn over her..."

**Text**: "And the merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn over her; for no man buyeth their merchandise any more:"

**Exegesis**: The second group of mourners are the *emporoi tēs gēs* — "merchants of the earth," who are also identified as "the great men of the earth" (v. 23; cf. [Isa. 23:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/23-2.htm), 8). The commentary is explicit that these merchants are best understood figuratively rather than literally: they are "the commercial salespeople of the spiritual merchandise of Babylon, those who have sold her doctrines and policies to the kings and peoples of earth." Their grief (*klaiousin kai penthousousin*) is entirely self-interested — *because nobody buys their merchandise any longer*. The destruction of Babylon means the end of the flow of corrupt goods sold and distributed in her name. Their weeping has no sympathy for Babylon herself; it is the weeping of those who have lost their income stream. The merchants' catalogue that follows (vv. 12–13) parallels Ezekiel's doom song over Tyre ([Ezek. 27:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-5.htm)–24) — itself a lament over a great commercial city whose wealth became the instrument of its pride and fall.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 27:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-5.htm)–24; [Isa. 23:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/23-2.htm), 8; [Rev. 18:23](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-23.htm).

### [Rev 18:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-12.htm)–13 — "The merchandise of gold, and silver..."

**Text**: "The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet, and all thyine wood, and all manner vessels of ivory, and all manner vessels of most precious wood, and of brass, and iron, and marble, And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men."

**Exegesis**: This catalogue of twenty-nine categories of merchandise moves deliberately from the most precious to the most base — from gold and precious stones down through spices, foodstuffs, animals, and finally, at the very bottom, *sōmata* (bodies/slaves) and *psychas anthrōpōn* (souls of men). The deliberate descent culminates in the shocking final items: the spiritual merchandise of Babylon ultimately traffics in human persons — bodies bought and sold, souls deceived and destroyed. The list is modeled on Ezekiel's catalogue of Tyre's trade goods ([Ezek. 27:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-5.htm)–24) and encompasses virtually the entire luxury economy of the ancient Mediterranean world. Each category carries figurative weight in the context of end-time Babylon: gold and silver = wealth and power; fine linen, purple, scarlet = the costly garments of Babylon the prostitute (cf. [Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm); 18:16); frankincense and spices = false worship and corrupted liturgy; horses and chariots = military power pressed into Babylon's service; slaves and souls of men = the ultimate indictment — Babylon commodifies human beings, treating the image of God as merchandise. The phrase "souls of men" (*psychas anthrōpōn*) stands apart grammatically — added after "bodies/slaves" as a horrified specification — suggesting John intends the reader to see that what Babylon ultimately trades in is not merely human labor but human spiritual destiny.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 27:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-5.htm)–24; [Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm); Isa. 23; Ezek. 28.

### [Rev 18:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-14.htm) — "And the fruits that thy soul lusted after are departed from thee..."

**Text**: "And the fruits that thy soul lusted after are departed from thee, and all things which were dainty and goodly are departed from thee, and thou shalt find them no more at all."

**Exegesis**: The dirge now addresses Babylon directly in the second person (*sou* — "your soul longed"). The *opōra* ("fruits," literally the ripe fruit of late summer — a word connoting luxurious abundance at peak season) have gone, departed forever. The triple emphasis — "departed from thee... departed from thee... thou shalt find them no more at all" (*ou mē auta heurēsousin eti*) — is emphatic negation stacked upon emphatic negation. Everything that Babylon's soul *epethumēsen* (lusted/longed for) is gone. The word *epithymia* here echoes the prohibited coveting of the Decalogue — Babylon's desires were themselves sinful, oriented toward luxury and self-indulgence rather than God. "All things dainty (*lipara*) and goodly (*lampra*)" — the Greek carries connotations of richness (oily, fatty) and brilliance (bright, resplendent) — the entire sumptuous, glittering world that Babylon inhabited has vanished. The "no more at all" (*eti*) language prefigures the repeated "no more" refrain of vv. 21–23.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 27:27](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-27.htm), 34; [Isa. 47:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-1.htm)–3.

### [Rev 18:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-15.htm) — "The merchants of these things...shall stand afar off..."

**Text**: "The merchants of these things, which were made rich by her, shall stand afar off for the fear of her torment, weeping and wailing,"

**Exegesis**: The merchants repeat the posture of the kings (v. 10): *apo makrothen stēsontai* — standing at a distance, *dia ton phobon tou basanismou autēs* — because of fear of her torment. The repetition is structural and intentional: both groups stand at a distance, both are motivated by terror rather than sympathy, both mourn purely for their own loss. Their wealth (*eploutēsan*) came entirely through her — *hoi ploutēsantes ap' autēs* — they were made rich "from her." Without Babylon, they are nothing. Their weeping (*klaiountes*) and wailing (*penthountes*) are the formal expressions of the ancient mourning rite, but the content is hollow: there is no love for Babylon in these tears, only self-pity.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 18:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-10.htm); [Ezek. 27:31](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-31.htm)–36.

### [Rev 18:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-16.htm) — "Alas, alas that great city, that was clothed in fine linen..."

**Text**: "And saying, Alas, alas that great city, that was clothed in fine linen, and purple, and scarlet, and decked with gold, and precious stones, and pearls!"

**Exegesis**: The second *ouai ouai* — "Woe, woe" — opens the merchants' dirge proper. Their description of Babylon's former splendor precisely mirrors the description of the great prostitute in [Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm): "arrayed in purple and scarlet, adorned with gold and precious stones and pearls." The *byssinos* (fine linen, bleached white) combined with *porphyra* (purple) and *kokkinos* (scarlet) constituted the highest-status garments of the ancient world. Purple was extraordinarily expensive, extracted from murex sea snails; scarlet signified wealth and royalty. The merchants recall Babylon's former glorious appearance as a lament — the *was* (*ēn*) that now is no more. The irony is complete: in [Rev. 19:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-8.htm), the Bride of Christ is also clothed in "fine linen, clean and white" (*byssinos lampros kai katharos*) — but her linen is the "righteousness of the saints," not the borrowed glory of a prostitute.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm); 19:8; [Ezek. 27:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-7.htm), 16; [Dan. 5:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/5-7.htm).

### [Rev 18:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-17.htm) — "For in one hour so great riches is come to nought..."

**Text**: "For in one hour so great riches is come to nought. And every shipmaster, and all the company in ships, and sailors, and as many as trade by sea, stood afar off,"

**Exegesis**: The merchants' dirge concludes with the devastating verdict: *hoti mia hōra ērēmōthē ho tosoutos ploutos* — "for in one hour so great wealth has been laid waste." The third "in one hour" occurrence (cf. vv. 10, 19). The wealth that took Babylon generations to accumulate — wealth that seemed indestructible, eternal, backed by the political powers of the entire world — is annihilated in a moment. This is the theological counterpoint to Babylon's boast of permanence. The verse then introduces the third group of mourners without completing a sentence, creating narrative breathlessness: every *kybernētēs* (shipmaster/pilot), all the *epi topōn pleontōn* (those who travel by sea for trade), every *nautēs* (sailor), and all who make their living from the sea — they also stand at a distance watching. In the figurative reading, shipmasters are those who navigate the distribution networks of Babylon's spiritual merchandise — the administrators and functionaries of the apostate system.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 27:27](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-27.htm)–29; [Rev. 18:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-10.htm), 19; 17:12.

### [Rev 18:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-18.htm) — "And cried when they saw the smoke of her burning..."

**Text**: "And cried when they saw the smoke of her burning, saying, What city is like unto this great city!"

**Exegesis**: The shipmasters' lament opens with a rhetorical question: *tis homoios tē polei tē megalē* — "What city is like the great city?" The form is that of the ancient *qinah* (lament) but the content is a parody. "What city is like...?" parodies the doxological question "Who is like God?" (*tis homoios soi en theois, Kyrie* — [Exod. 15:11](https://biblehub.com/exodus/15-11.htm); [Ps. 35:10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/35-10.htm); [Mic. 7:18](https://biblehub.com/micah/7-18.htm)), and also parallels "Who is like the beast?" ([Rev. 13:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-4.htm)). Babylon has usurped the place of God in the world's imagination; now that usurper is exposed as ash and smoke. The smoke (*kapnos*) is the sign of divine judgment — the same smoke that rose from Sodom ([Gen. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/genesis/19-28.htm)) and that will rise from the final judgment in [Rev. 19:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-3.htm) ("her smoke rose up for ever and ever"). The cry of the sea-traders before Babylon's burning smoke is the fulfillment of Ezekiel's lament over Tyre: "Who is like Tyre...?" ([Ezek. 27:32](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-32.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 15:11](https://biblehub.com/exodus/15-11.htm); [Ps. 35:10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/35-10.htm); [Mic. 7:18](https://biblehub.com/micah/7-18.htm); [Rev. 13:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-4.htm); [Gen. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/genesis/19-28.htm); [Ezek. 27:32](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-32.htm); [Rev. 19:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-3.htm).

### [Rev 18:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-19.htm) — "And they cast dust on their heads, and cried..."

**Text**: "And they cast dust on their heads, and cried, weeping and wailing, saying, Alas, alas that great city, wherein were made rich all that had ships in the sea by reason of her costliness! for in one hour is she made desolate."

**Exegesis**: The casting of dust (*balousi choun epi tas kephalas autōn*) on the head is the ancient gesture of extreme grief and mourning (cf. [Josh. 7:6](https://biblehub.com/joshua/7-6.htm); [Job 2:12](https://biblehub.com/job/2-12.htm); [Lam. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/lamentations/2-10.htm); [Ezek. 27:30](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-30.htm)). Here it directly echoes Ezekiel's description of the sailors lamenting over Tyre: "They will cast dust on their heads" ([Ezek. 27:30](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-30.htm)). The gesture signals total, inconsolable devastation. The third *ouai ouai* rings out. Their grievance is explicit: "all who had ships in the sea became rich through her wealth (*ek tēs timiotētos autēs* — literally 'through her costliness/value')." Babylon's economy of spiritual merchandise generated real commercial wealth for those who participated. The final "in one hour she became desolate (*ērēmōthē*)" — the third "one hour" — seals the triple emphasis on Babylon's instantaneous, total overthrow. The word *ērēmōthē* (made desolate) echoes the wilderness/desert imagery of [Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm) (where the woman sits in a wilderness) and the desolation oracles against Babylon and Tyre.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 27:29](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-29.htm)–32; [Josh. 7:6](https://biblehub.com/joshua/7-6.htm); [Job 2:12](https://biblehub.com/job/2-12.htm); [Lam. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/lamentations/2-10.htm); [Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm); 18:10, 17.

### [Rev 18:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-20.htm) — "Rejoice over her, thou heaven, and ye holy apostles and prophets..."

**Text**: "Rejoice over her, thou heaven, and ye holy apostles and prophets; for God hath avenged you on her."

**Exegesis**: The three dirges of the earth dwellers are answered by a single divine counter-command directed to heaven: *euphrainou ep' autē, ourane* — "Rejoice over her, O heaven!" This is the reversal of the laments — not mourning but exultation. The rejoicing commanded here is portrayed as actually taking place in [Rev. 19:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-1.htm)–10 (the great hallelujah chorus). The summons echoes [Jeremiah 51:48](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-48.htm) (RSV): "Then the heavens and the earth and all that is in them shall sing for joy over Babylon; for the destroyers shall come against them out of the north, declares the Lord." The call is addressed to *hoi hagioi kai hoi apostoloi kai hoi prophētai* — "saints and apostles and prophets" — i.e., the entire company of God's faithful throughout history who have suffered under Babylon's persecution. The reason for rejoicing: *hoti ekrinen ho Theos to krima hymōn ex autēs* — "because God has judged your judgment against her" (lit. "God has executed/vindicated your cause against her"). The noun *krima* here is a legal verdict in the plaintiffs' favor. The blood of the saints that cried out under the fifth seal ([Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11: "How long, O Lord... wilt thou not judge and avenge our blood?") has now been answered: God has acted as the divine Avenger (*ekdikēsis*). The fall of Babylon is simultaneously the vindication of every martyr.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:48](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-48.htm)–49; [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11; 19:1–10; [Deut. 32:43](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-43.htm); [Luke 18:3](https://biblehub.com/luke/18-3.htm), 5.

### [Rev 18:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-21.htm) — "And a mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone..."

**Text**: "And a mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone, and cast it into the sea, saying, Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all."

**Exegesis**: The millstone action is a prophetic sign-act (*dramatikon sēmeion*) — a symbolic deed that enacts what it signifies. A strong angel (*angelos ischyros*) takes a stone (*lithos*) the size of a great millstone (*hōs mylinon megan*) and hurls it into the sea with violence (*hormēmati*). The scene is borrowed directly from [Jeremiah 51:59](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-59.htm)–64, where God ordered Jeremiah to attach a stone to the scroll predicting Babylon's destruction and throw it into the Euphrates: "Just so shall Babylon sink down and not rise again, because of the calamity that I am going to bring upon her" ([Jer. 51:64](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-64.htm)). The act also evokes Jesus' warning in [Matthew 18:6](https://biblehub.com/matthew/18-6.htm): "Whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to stumble, it would be better for him to have a heavy millstone hung around his neck, and to be drowned in the depth of the sea." Babylon has been found guilty of precisely this: causing "little ones" to stumble through seduction and spiritual deception. The angel's declaration is final and absolute: Babylon "will never be found any longer" (*ou mē heurethē eti*) — the first of a sevenfold "no more" refrain that follows in vv. 22–23, each repetition hammering the finality of Babylon's annihilation. The violence (*hormēma*, "rush/force") of the throwing emphasizes that this is not a gradual decline but a sudden, catastrophic, irreversible overthrow.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:59](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-59.htm)–64; [Matt. 18:6](https://biblehub.com/matthew/18-6.htm); [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 16:19; 17:16.

### [Rev 18:22](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-22.htm) — "And the voice of harpers, and musicians...shall be heard no more at all..."

**Text**: "And the voice of harpers, and musicians, and of pipers, and trumpeters, shall be heard no more at all in thee; and no craftsman, of whatsoever craft he be, shall be found any more in thee; and the sound of a millstone shall be heard no more at all in thee;"

**Exegesis**: The sevenfold "no more" (*ou mē... eti*) catalogue is a structured desolation oracle, describing the total cessation of the activities that constitute urban civilized life. Each "no more" is a nail in the coffin of Babylon's existence. First: *phōnē kitharōdōn* (voice of harpists), *mousikōn* (musicians/singers), *aulētōn* (flute-players), *salpistōn* (trumpeters) — the entire spectrum of public and liturgical music falls silent. Music in the ancient world marked celebration, worship, and civic life; its absence signals utter desolation. Second: *technītēs pasēs technēs* — "every craftsman of any craft" (*technē*, from which "technology") — all skilled labor, all manufacturing, all economic production ceases. Third: *phōnē mylou* — "the sound of a mill(stone)" — the grinding of grain that sustained daily life, the most basic domestic activity, falls silent. These images are drawn from OT desolation prophecies. Ezekiel against Tyre: "So I will silence the sound of your songs, and the sound of your harps will be heard no more" ([Ezek. 26:13](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/26-13.htm)). Jeremiah against Jerusalem as a prototype of all desolated cities: "I will take from them the voice of joy and the voice of gladness, the voice of the bridegroom and the voice of the bride, the sound of the millstones and the light of the lamp" ([Jer. 25:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-10.htm)) — a text that John will continue to echo in the next verse.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 26:13](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/26-13.htm); [Jer. 25:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-10.htm); [Isa. 24:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/24-8.htm); [Ezek. 27:30](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/27-30.htm)–36.

### [Rev 18:23](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-23.htm) — "And the light of a candle shall shine no more at all in thee..."

**Text**: "And the light of a candle shall shine no more at all in thee; and the voice of the bridegroom and of the bride shall be heard no more at all in thee: for thy merchants were the great men of the earth; for by thy sorceries were all nations deceived."

**Exegesis**: The desolation oracle continues with two more "no mores." Fourth: *phōs lychnoy* — the light of a lamp (*lychnos*) — the most intimate symbol of domestic life, the hearth-light of home — shall shine no more. Fifth: *phōnē nymphiou kai nymphēs* — the voice of bridegroom and bride — the most joyful sound of human community, the celebration of new life and covenant love — shall be heard no more. Both images come directly from [Jeremiah 25:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-10.htm) (against Jerusalem/Judah) repurposed now for end-time Babylon. The lamp and the wedding are also types of the true light ([Rev. 22:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-5.htm); 21:23) and the true wedding ([Rev. 19:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-7.htm)–9; 21:2) that Babylon has counterfeited and corrupted. After the "no more" list, two reasons are given for Babylon's indictment. First: *hoti hoi emporoi sou ēsan hoi megistanes tēs gēs* — "because your merchants were the great men of the earth." The commercial retailers of Babylon's spiritual merchandise had placed themselves into proud service of the apostate system; their "greatness" resulted entirely from their illicit association with Babylon. Second: *hoti en tē pharmakeia sou eplanēthēsan panta ta ethnē* — "because by your *pharmakeia* (sorcery/magic spell) all the nations were deceived." The Greek *pharmakeia* (whence "pharmacy") appears in Paul's vice-list ([Gal. 5:20](https://biblehub.com/galatians/5-20.htm)). Isaiah lists sorcery among ancient Babylon's sins that brought her fall ([Isa. 47:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-9.htm), 12). By means of the wine of fornication and demonic signs (*sēmeia* — the counterfeit miracles of [Rev. 13:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-13.htm)–14; 19:20), Babylon has successfully deceived the entire world. The deception is the mechanism; the sorcery is its spiritual engine.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 25:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-10.htm); [Isa. 47:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-9.htm), 12; [Gal. 5:20](https://biblehub.com/galatians/5-20.htm); [Rev. 9:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-21.htm); 13:13–14; 19:20; 21:8; 22:15; [Rev. 19:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-7.htm)–9; 21:23; 22:5.

### [Rev 18:24](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-24.htm) — "And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints..."

**Text**: "And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth."

**Exegesis**: The final verse is the ultimate and most damning indictment: *en autē haima prophētōn kai hagiōn heurethē kai pantōn tōn esphagmenōn epi tēs gēs* — "and in her was found the blood of prophets and saints and of all who were slain on the earth." The word *heurethē* (was found) is forensic — this is the verdict of a completed investigation. Babylon stands convicted of the murder of God's people. The charge echoes [Jeremiah 51:49](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-49.htm) (RSV): "Babylon must fall for the slain of Israel, as for Babylon have fallen the slain of all the earth." The scope is universal: not merely prophets and saints of the Christian era, but "all those who have been slain upon the earth" — making end-time Babylon responsible for the entire history of righteous bloodshed, from Abel onward (cf. [Matt. 23:35](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-35.htm): "that upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, from the blood of righteous Abel to the blood of Zechariah"). This blood has been crying out from beneath the altar since [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–10: "How long, O Lord... dost thou not judge and avenge our blood?" The answer is now given: God has heard; the destroyer of the saints is destroyed; Babylon is found guilty; the prayers of the martyrs are answered (as [Rev. 19:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-1.htm)–2 immediately confirms: "his judgments are true and just; for he has judged the great harlot... and has avenged on her the blood of his servants"). The chapter closes not with mourning but with verdict — the most solemn and absolute foundation for the hallelujah chorus that immediately follows.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:49](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-49.htm); [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11; 17:6; 19:1–2; [Matt. 23:35](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-35.htm); [Luke 11:50](https://biblehub.com/luke/11-50.htm)–51; [Gen. 4:10](https://biblehub.com/genesis/4-10.htm).

## Summary of Chapter Structure and Key Themes

**Structure**: The chapter divides into two major sections — (A) Call to Separate (vv. 1–8): angelic proclamation of fall (vv. 1–3) + divine appeal to God's people (vv. 4–5) + sentence of judgment (vv. 6–8); and (B) Laments and Vindication (vv. 9–24): three dirges (kings vv. 9–10; merchants vv. 11–17a; shipmasters vv. 17b–19) + counter-summons to rejoice (v. 20) + millstone sign-act (v. 21) + sevenfold desolation oracle (vv. 22–23) + final indictment (v. 24).

**Key theological themes**:
1. **Prophetic-typological method**: Historical Babylon's fall (Jer. 50–51; Isa. 13; 47) is not merely illustrative but structurally determinative — God's pattern of action in the past guarantees his action in the future.
2. **Double recompense** (*diplosmos*): Babylon's punishment is equivalent in measure to her crime — not petty revenge but perfect justice on God's scales.
3. **The "Come Out" appeal**: Ch. 18 is fundamentally evangelistic — even in the midst of judgment proclamation, the dominant purpose is the rescue of God's wavering people from Babylon before the end.
4. **Threefold seduction**: Babylon operates through religious deception (wine of fornication → all nations), political alliance (fornication with kings), and economic enticement (merchants grow rich) — a comprehensive system of control.
5. **Martyrological vindication**: The blood of prophets and saints (v. 24), the cry of [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11, and the rejoicing of v. 20 form a single narrative arc: the persecution of God's people by Babylon is the crime that makes her judgment necessary and just.
