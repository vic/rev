## Revelation 6

---

### [Rev 6:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-1.htm) — "And I saw when the Lamb opened one of the seals"

**Text**: "And I saw when the Lamb opened one of the seals, and I heard, as it were the noise of thunder, one of the four beasts saying, Come and see."

**Exegesis**: The structural location of chapter 6 is determinative for its meaning. Revelation 4-5 narrates Christ's enthronement — his overcoming and sitting with the Father on his throne. Revelation 7 narrates the overcomers joining Christ on that throne. Chapter 6, set between them, describes "the ongoing period in which God's people are in the process of overcoming" (Paulien). The seven seals are not events in themselves; they are events *accompanying* the breaking of the seals by the enthroned Lamb who in chapter 5 was uniquely qualified to take and open the seven-sealed scroll.

The voice of thunder is most likely the first living being — the one resembling a lion (4:7) — whose thunderous voice suits a lion's roar. The Greek manuscripts underlying the KJV read "Come and see" (*erchou kai ide*), but the best witnesses (including Codex Alexandrinus) have only "Come!" (*erchou*), a summons not to John to look but to each horseman to step onto the stage of history (vv. 1, 3, 5, 7). The Lamb's act of breaking seals enacts in history what was ratified in heaven at the enthronement: Christ's authority over the course of history from Pentecost to the Second Coming. The seals are instruments Christ uses "for the sanctification of His Church and the extension of His kingdom" (Hendriksen).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 3:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-21.htm); 4:7; 5:5, 9; [Matt. 24:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-29.htm); [Luke 21:34](https://biblehub.com/luke/21-34.htm)-36.

### [Rev 6:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-2.htm) — "And I saw, and behold a white horse"

**Text**: "And I saw, and behold a white horse: and he that sat on him had a bow; and a crown was given unto him: and he went forth conquering, and to conquer."

**Exegesis**: Three interpretations exist for the white horse and rider: (1) Preterist/literal — the Parthians, Rome's feared eastern enemies renowned as mounted archers. This view fails because it deprives the vision of prophetic scope and the color white does not fit Parthian symbolism. (2) The Antichrist or forces of evil. Proponents note the bow sometimes symbolizes God's enemies ([Jer. 49:35](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/49-35.htm); 51:3, 56; [Ezek. 39:3](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/39-3.htm); [Hos. 1:5](https://biblehub.com/hosea/1-5.htm)) and that the beast of chapter 13 conquers the saints in language parallel to 6:2. (3) The most strongly supported view: the rider symbolizes the triumphant proclamation of the gospel of Christ initiated at the cross and launched at Pentecost.

The decisive argument for view (3) is that in Revelation *white always and without exception* refers to Christ or his followers, never to evil forces: white hair of Christ (1:14), white stone (2:17), white garments of the faithful (3:4-5, 18), white robes of elders (4:4) and martyrs (6:11), white cloud of the Son of Man (14:14), white horses of the heavenly armies (19:14), white throne (20:11). The *stephanos* (garland, victor's crown) worn here is distinct from the *diadem* (royal crown) of 19:12. *Stephanos* in Revelation is used almost always with reference to Christ or his overcoming people (2:10; 3:11; 4:4, 10; 12:1; 14:14), representing the victory won at the cross (5:5-6; 12:11). The bow recalls OT imagery of God going forth as warrior with bow in hand ([Hab. 3:8-9](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/3-8.htm); [Ps. 45:3-5](https://biblehub.com/psalms/45-3.htm); [Isa. 41:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-2.htm); [Zech. 9:13-16](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/9-13.htm)).

The rider goes forth "conquering and to conquer" — the conquest is spiritual: through the Holy Spirit and the preaching of the gospel, Christ wins human hearts. This conquest did not end in the apostolic era; it portrays the ongoing progress of the gospel throughout all Christian history until the Second Coming ([Matt. 24:14](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-14.htm)). At Pentecost, Christ was enthroned and all authority placed in his hands (Rev. 5); the first seal depicts that divine authority being *actuated in human history* through gospel proclamation.

**Cross-references**: [Hab. 3:8-9](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/3-8.htm); [Ps. 45:3-5](https://biblehub.com/psalms/45-3.htm); [Isa. 41:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-2.htm); [Zech. 9:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/9-13.htm); [Rev. 5:5-6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-5.htm); 12:11; 19:11-14; [1 Cor. 15:24-25](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-24.htm); [Matt. 24:14](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-14.htm); [John 12:31](https://biblehub.com/john/12-31.htm)-32; 16:11.

### [Rev 6:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-3.htm) — "And when he had opened the second seal"

**Text**: "And when he had opened the second seal, I heard the second beast say, Come and see."

**Exegesis**: The second living being calls for the second horse. Given the pattern of living beings in 4:7, this is likely the one with the appearance of a calf — the calf being a symbol of sacrifice, which fits the context of bloodshed and martyrdom that the red horse introduces. The summons follows the identical structure of v. 1, emphasizing the sequential and purposeful nature of Christ's opening the seals one by one.

### [Rev 6:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-4.htm) — "And there went out another horse that was red"

**Text**: "And there went out another horse that was red: and power was given to him that sat thereon to take peace from the earth, and that they should kill one another: and there was given unto him a great sword."

**Exegesis**: The Greek *puros* means "fiery-red," the color of blood and oppression. In [2 Kings 3:22](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/3-22.htm)-23 the Moabites saw water "red as blood." Isaiah describes Jerusalem's sins as scarlet for her "hands are covered with blood" ([Isa. 1:15-18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-15.htm)). In [Rev. 17:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-6.htm) the scarlet beast is linked to the harlot "drunk from the blood of the saints." The red color is wholly appropriate for persecution and bloodshed.

The interpretation of the second seal is determined by the first. The white horse has gone forth proclaiming the gospel; those who reject it lose the peace the gospel brings. Jesus stated explicitly: "Do not think that I came to bring peace on the earth; I did not come to bring peace, but a sword. For I came to set a man against his father" ([Matt. 10:34-36](https://biblehub.com/matthew/10-34.htm); cf. [Luke 12:51](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-51.htm)-53). The rider on the red horse is *given* (divine passive — God permits) authority "to take peace from the earth, in order that they might slay one another" (*sphazō*, literally "slaughter"). This Greek verb *sphazō* is the same word used of the death of Christ (5:6, 9, 12; 13:8) and the martyrs slain for their testimony (6:9; 18:24) — it is not ordinary battlefield language but sacrificial language.

The OT background includes: the Israelites killing one another with the sword at Sinai ([Exod. 32:27-29](https://biblehub.com/exodus/32-27.htm)); the Midianites slaying each other (Judg. 7:22); Isaiah's prophecy that God would stir up Egyptians so they fight one another ([Isa. 19:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/19-2.htm)); Zechariah's prediction that enemies would attack one another ([Zech. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-13.htm)). The "great sword" given to the rider links to Christ's sharp sword of judgment in 19:15, but here wielded against those who reject the gospel — persecution, division, and conflict are the natural consequences of rejecting love. As Jesus warned in his Olivet discourse: "They will deliver you up to tribulation, and will kill you, and you will be hated by all nations ... many will fall away, and will deliver up one another and hate one another" ([Matt. 24:9-10](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-9.htm)).

The fifth seal makes plain the human cost: the martyrs slain (*sphazō*) "because of the word of God and the testimony they held" (6:9) — the same verb, the same cause, the same reality.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 19:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/19-2.htm); [Exod. 32:27-29](https://biblehub.com/exodus/32-27.htm); Judg. 7:22; [Zech. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-13.htm); [Matt. 10:34-36](https://biblehub.com/matthew/10-34.htm); 24:9-10; [Luke 12:51](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-51.htm)-53; [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm); 19:15.

### [Rev 6:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-5.htm) — "And when he had opened the third seal ... lo a black horse"

**Text**: "And when he had opened the third seal, I heard the third beast say, Come and see. And I beheld, and lo a black horse; and he that sat on him had a pair of balances in his hand."

**Exegesis**: The third living being — likely the one with a human face (4:7) — summons the black horse. Black in Revelation is the color of darkness (the sun becomes "black as sackcloth" in 6:12). In the NT, darkness consistently symbolizes the *absence* of the gospel ([Matt. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/matthew/4-16.htm); [Luke 1:79](https://biblehub.com/luke/1-79.htm); [John 1:5](https://biblehub.com/john/1-5.htm); 3:19; [Acts 26:18](https://biblehub.com/acts/26-18.htm); [Col. 1:13](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-13.htm); [1 [John 1:5](https://biblehub.com/john/1-5.htm)](https://biblehub.com/1_john/1-5.htm)). The black horse, following the red horse of persecution and resistance to the gospel, represents the condition that follows: spiritual darkness and famine for the word of God.

The balance (*zygon*, a pair of scales) in the OT is a symbol of famine — specifically of rationing food by weight, the sign of extreme scarcity. The Lord warned through Moses: "When I break your staff of bread ... they will bring back your bread by weight" ([Lev. 26:26](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-26.htm)). Ezekiel prophesied that during Jerusalem's siege, inhabitants would "eat bread by weight and with anxiety, and drink water by measure" ([Ezek. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/4-16.htm)). The imagery is drawn directly from covenant curse language.

**Cross-references**: [Lev. 26:26](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-26.htm); [Ezek. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/4-16.htm); [Rev. 6:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-12.htm); [John 1:5](https://biblehub.com/john/1-5.htm); [Matt. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/matthew/4-16.htm).

### [Rev 6:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-6.htm) — "A measure of wheat for a penny ... see thou hurt not the oil and the wine"

**Text**: "And I heard a voice in the midst of the four beasts say, A measure of wheat for a penny, and three measures of barley for a penny; and see thou hurt not the oil and the wine."

**Exegesis**: A voice from the midst of the four living beings — likely the Father or Christ himself — announces the specific economic conditions of famine. The Greek *choinix* (translated "measure" or "quart") was the standard daily ration for a man. A *denarius* was the average daily wage of a Roman laborer. In ordinary times a denarius purchased twelve to fifteen times as much food; under the famine conditions described here, a full day's wage buys only enough wheat for one person to survive. Three measures of barley (the cheaper, inferior grain eaten by the poor) for the same price gives a family slightly more food but still at crushing cost.

This is not merely economic famine, however. The rider on the black horse symbolizes a *spiritual* famine for the word of God. If the white horse represents the proclamation of the gospel and the black horse is its opposite, then the scarcity is a scarcity of God's word. Amos's prophecy is programmatic: "Behold, days are coming ... when I will send a famine on the land, not a famine for bread or a thirst for water, but rather for hearing the word of the Lord" ([Amos 8:11](https://biblehub.com/amos/8-11.htm)-13). Grain symbolizes the word of God (cf. [Matt. 13:3-30](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-3.htm); [Luke 8:11](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-11.htm)) and Jesus himself as the bread of life ([Matt. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/matthew/4-4.htm); [John 6:35](https://biblehub.com/john/6-35.htm)-58).

Critically, oil and wine are not to be harmed. In Palestine, grain, wine, and oil were the three principal crops, all considered ordinary necessities of life ([Deut. 7:13](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/7-13.htm); 11:14; 28:51; 2 Chron. 32:28; [Hos. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/2-8.htm), 22; [Joel 2:19](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-19.htm)) — not luxuries. Olive trees and vines were deeply rooted and could survive drought far better than shallow-rooted grain. Symbolically, oil represents the Holy Spirit and wine represents God's gracious salvation in Christ. Even in the midst of spiritual famine, the Holy Spirit continues to work and salvation remains available. God is still at work even when his word is obscured.

This famine corresponds historically to the Medieval period when, as the gospel was resisted, forgotten, and replaced by tradition, the word of God was withheld from ordinary people — yet the Holy Spirit was not quenched.

**Cross-references**: [Lev. 26:26](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-26.htm); [Ezek. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/4-16.htm); [Amos 8:11](https://biblehub.com/amos/8-11.htm)-13; [Deut. 7:13](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/7-13.htm); 11:14; [Matt. 13:3-30](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-3.htm); [John 6:35](https://biblehub.com/john/6-35.htm)-58; [Luke 8:11](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-11.htm).

### [Rev 6:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-7.htm) — "And when he had opened the fourth seal"

**Text**: "And when he had opened the fourth seal, I heard the voice of the fourth beast say, Come and see."

**Exegesis**: The fourth living being — most likely the one like a flying eagle (4:7) — calls for the fourth horse. The eagle is a symbol of swift and far-ranging vision, appropriate for introducing the final and most comprehensive of the four horsemen who surveys the full consequence of the preceding three. The pattern is consistent: seals one through four are introduced by a living being's summons and feature a horse and rider.

### [Rev 6:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-8.htm) — "And I looked, and behold a pale horse: and his name that sat on him was Death, and Hell followed with him"

**Text**: "And I looked, and behold a pale horse: and his name that sat on him was Death, and Hell followed with him. And power was given unto them over the fourth part of the earth, to kill with sword, and with hunger, and with death, and with the beasts of the earth."

**Exegesis**: The Greek *chlōros* (elsewhere "green," used of vegetation in [Mark 6:39](https://biblehub.com/mark/6-39.htm); [Rev. 8:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-7.htm); 9:4) here denotes the sickly yellowish-green pallor of a corpse in advanced decomposition — "pale" captures it well. This is the color of death itself. The rider is named *Death* (*Thanatos*) and *Hades* (*hadēs*) follows close behind — a pair appearing together in 1:18 where Christ declares: "I hold the keys of Death and Hades." Their appearance here under Christ's authority (they are "given" power — divine passive) confirms they are not ultimate powers; they remain under the control of the enthroned Lamb.

Death and Hades are given authority over "one-fourth of the earth." This fractional authority is significant: it is partial and limited. The "one-fourth of the earth" corresponds to one of the four quarters of the earth; each of the four horsemen has authority over one quarter, paralleling the four winds of 7:1 related to the four corners of the earth.

The four instruments of judgment — sword, famine, death (pestilence), and wild beasts — are drawn directly from the covenant curse passages of the Torah and the Prophets. In [Leviticus 26:21](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-21.htm)-26, God warned Israel of escalating judgments for covenant unfaithfulness: wild beasts killing children and cattle, sword avenging covenant-breaking, pestilence in cities, breaking of the "staff of bread." Ezekiel announced God would send "four severe judgments against Jerusalem: sword, famine, wild beasts, and plague" ([Ezek. 14:21](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/14-21.htm); cf. 6:11-12; 33:27-29). These were intended to drive Israel to repentance; continued disobedience intensified them. The four horses collectively represent the OT covenant curses applied to those who reject the gospel — they are redemptive in intent, designed to awaken and produce repentance, but escalating toward exclusion and exile for the impenitent.

The fourth seal is the intensification and culmination of the first three: persecution (red) leads to spiritual famine (black) which leads to spiritual death (pale). Death and Hades are temporal enemies — "the day is coming when Death and Hades will have their end in the second death" (20:14; cf. [1 Cor. 15:26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-26.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-18.htm); 20:14; [Lev. 26:21-26](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-21.htm); [Deut. 32:41-43](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-41.htm); [Ezek. 14:21](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/14-21.htm); 6:11-12; 33:27-29; [1 Cor. 15:25-26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-25.htm).

### [Rev 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm) — "And when he had opened the fifth seal, I saw under the altar the souls of them that were slain"

**Text**: "And when he had opened the fifth seal, I saw under the altar the souls of them that were slain for the word of God, and for the testimony which they held:"

**Exegesis**: The last three seals differ structurally from the first four — no summons from a living being, no horses and riders. They shift focus from God's judgments on the unfaithful among his people to the vindication of the faithful martyrs and the final judgment on their persecutors.

The altar under which the martyrs' souls are found is the *altar of burnt offering* (not the incense altar), as indicated by the OT sacrificial ritual: "All the blood of the bull he shall pour out at the base of the altar of burnt offering" ([Lev. 4:7](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/4-7.htm), 18, 25, 30-34; 8:15; 9:9). Blood was poured beneath the altar because blood — symbolizing life — belonged to God ([Lev. 17:11-14](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/17-11.htm)). The martyrs are "under the altar" because their lifeblood has been "poured out as an offering to God" (Barclay). In Jewish tradition, the souls of the righteous were preserved under the throne of glory; John adapts this imagery for the martyrs. The altar of burnt offering was located in the *outer court* of the temple, not in the sanctuary proper — this spatial symbolism suggests the scene is earthly (the outer court representing the earth) rather than heavenly.

The martyrs were slain "because of the word of God and because of the testimony that they had." Their "testimony" (*martyria*) here is not "the testimony of Jesus" (1:2, 9; 20:4) — the objective genitive describing Christ's own testimony — but the martyrs' subjective witness *about* Christ, their testimony to the gospel (cf. 11:7; 12:11). The same word *sphazō* ("slain/slaughtered") is used here as in 6:4 (red horse rider's mission) and of Christ himself in 5:6, 9 — martyrdom is framed as sacrifice. Jesus predicted: "The hour is coming when everyone who kills you will think he is offering service to God" ([John 16:2](https://biblehub.com/john/16-2.htm)). Paul describes his own death as being "poured out as a drink offering" ([Phil. 2:17](https://biblehub.com/philippians/2-17.htm); [2 Tim. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/4-6.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Lev. 4:7](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/4-7.htm); 8:15; 17:11-14; [Rev. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-6.htm); 11:7; 12:11; 16:6-7; [John 16:2](https://biblehub.com/john/16-2.htm); [Phil. 2:17](https://biblehub.com/philippians/2-17.htm); [2 Tim. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/4-6.htm); 1 Enoch 47:4.

### [Rev 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm) — "How long, O Lord, holy and true, dost thou not judge and avenge our blood"

**Text**: "And they cried with a loud voice, saying, How long, O Lord, holy and true, dost thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth?"

**Exegesis**: The martyrs' cry "How long?" (*heōs pote*) is the perennial lament of God's suffering people throughout redemptive history. It echoes: the Psalmist's cry against the wicked who afflict the righteous ([Ps. 79:1-10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/79-1.htm)); Habakkuk's complaint about heathen nations afflicting God's people ([Hab. 1:1-4](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/1-1.htm)); Daniel's cry "How long will the holy place be trampled?" ([Dan. 8:13](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-13.htm); 12:6-7); the writer of 4 Ezra: "How long are we to remain here?" (4 [Ezra 4:35](https://biblehub.com/ezra/4-35.htm)a). It is the cry of those "bewildered by God's seeming inactivity, but [who] never doubted the ultimate action of God" (Barclay).

The title "holy and true" (*hagios kai alēthinos*) applied to God/Christ (cf. 3:7) undergirds the legal nature of the plea. The Greek *ekdikeō* ("avenge," "procure justice for someone") carries a forensic, legal sense — not personal revenge but a plea for legal vindication. In [Luke 18:3](https://biblehub.com/luke/18-3.htm), the widow cries to the judge: "Give me legal protection from my opponent" — the same word. The explicit legal parallel: the martyrs are petitioning God as the just judge to conduct a legal process leading to a verdict that will vindicate them. This is "a plea for the vindication of those who are oppressed and slaughtered, but also a plea for the vindication of God in the eyes of those who have placed their trust in God" (Fiorenza). It is a cry for acquittal in the heavenly court.

"Those who dwell on the earth" (*hoi katoikountes epi tēs gēs*) is a near-technical expression in Revelation for the wicked — those who resist the gospel and persecute God's faithful people (3:10; 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 17:2). By contrast, the redeemed are "those who dwell in heaven" (13:6). The martyrs' blood cries from the earth as did Abel's blood ([Gen. 4:10](https://biblehub.com/genesis/4-10.htm)). The Fifth Seal corresponds to the period of the great persecution of the Middle Ages — "the phrase 'How long?' is applied in [Daniel 7:21](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-21.htm), 25; 12:6-7 to the great tribulation of the Middle Ages" (Paulien).

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 4:10](https://biblehub.com/genesis/4-10.htm); [Ps. 79:1-10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/79-1.htm); [Hab. 1:1-4](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/1-1.htm); [Dan. 8:13](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-13.htm); 12:6-7; [Luke 18:3](https://biblehub.com/luke/18-3.htm)-5; [Rev. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-10.htm); 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 17:2; 19:2; [Rom. 12:19](https://biblehub.com/romans/12-19.htm); 4 [Ezra 4:35](https://biblehub.com/ezra/4-35.htm).

### [Rev 6:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-11.htm) — "And white robes were given unto every one of them"

**Text**: "And white robes were given unto every one of them; and it was said unto them, that they should rest yet for a little season, until their fellowservants also and their brethren, that should be killed as they were, should be fulfilled."

**Exegesis**: God answers the martyrs directly. The passive forms ("were given," "it was said") are both divine passives — Hebrew *qal passive* usage indicating God as the agent. Two answers are given: present comfort and future promise.

The white robes (*stolas leukas*) are garments of victory, triumph, and faithfulness (3:4-5). Later John sees the redeemed multitude "clothed in white garments before the throne ... who have washed their garments in the blood of Christ" (7:9, 13-14) and the bride dressed in "fine linen, bright and clean" which represents "the righteous deeds of the saints" (19:7-8). These white robes are proleptically awarded — the martyrs receive now in symbolic form what they will fully experience at the resurrection. White in Revelation consistently marks Christ's people as overcomers.

The martyrs are told to "rest for a little while" (*anapausontai eti chronon mikron*). Rest is the state of the faithful dead in Revelation: "Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord from now on ... that they may rest from their labors" (14:13). The unrepentant "do not have rest day and night" (14:11). The martyrs do not come to life until the return of Christ — they are seen in [Rev. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-4.htm) as "souls of those beheaded because of the testimony of Jesus and because of the Word of God" coming to life at the Second Coming and reigning for a thousand years.

The condition for the end of the waiting — "until their fellowservants, that is, their brothers who are about to be killed, might be made complete" — does not mean a fixed *number* must be reached (the word "number" does not appear in the Greek text). Ladd correctly states: "This statement is surely not to be understood in any mathematical way, as though God had decreed that there must be a certain number of martyrs." The Greek *plēroō* means "to make full," "to bring to completion" — and the evidence elsewhere in Revelation (7:13-14; 19:7-8) supports understanding this as completion of *character*, not count. The fellow servants must be made complete in faithfulness and obedience, as the martyrs already have been — the standard is readiness for the bridal garment, not a tally. This directly answers "How long?" with a redemptive "Not until my people are ready."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 3:4-5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-4.htm); 7:9, 13-14; 14:11, 13; 19:7-8; 20:4; 1 Enoch 47:4; 4 [Ezra 4:35](https://biblehub.com/ezra/4-35.htm)-36; 2 Baruch 23:4-5.

### [Rev 6:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-12.htm) — "And I beheld when he had opened the sixth seal, and, lo, there was a great earthquake"

**Text**: "And I beheld when he had opened the sixth seal, and, lo, there was a great earthquake; and the sun became black as sackcloth of hair, and the moon became as blood;"

**Exegesis**: The sixth seal introduces the Day of the Lord — the eschatological intervention of God in final judgment. The imagery is entirely drawn from OT prophetic descriptions of the *Yōm YHWH*, the Day of the Lord: God's final visitation of earth, bringing annihilation to the wicked and redemption to the faithful.

The *great earthquake*: In the OT the coming of the Lord is accompanied by earth-shaking. Ezekiel prophesied a violent earthquake at God's coming against Gog ([Ezek. 38:19-20](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-19.htm)). Joel: "The earth quakes before them" ([Joel 2:10](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-10.htm)). Amos: "Will not the land quake because of this?" ([Amos 8:8](https://biblehub.com/amos/8-8.htm)). Haggai: "I am going to shake the heavens and the earth" (Hag. 2:6). The earthquake here precedes the greater final earthquake of 16:18 which shatters end-time Babylon. Historicist commentators have identified the 6:12 earthquake with the Lisbon earthquake of November 1, 1755, which killed 30,000-50,000 and shook the intellectual foundations of the Enlightenment.

The *sun black as sackcloth of hair and the moon as blood*: OT background is primarily [Joel 2:31](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-31.htm) — "the sun will be turned into darkness, and the moon into blood" before "the great and awesome day of the Lord comes" (cf. [Joel 3:15](https://biblehub.com/joel/3-15.htm)). Isaiah depicts God darkening the sun ([Isa. 50:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/50-3.htm)); "the stars will not shine, the sun will be darkened, the moon will not shed its light, and the earth will shake from its place at the wrath of the Lord" ([Isa. 13:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-10.htm), 13). The use of "as" (*hōs*) — "black *as* sackcloth," "as blood" — introduces figurative analogies to actual events, suggesting these signs are *literally* intended: the sun actually darkens, the moon actually appears blood-red, though the comparison is to familiar objects. Historicist scholars date the dark sun and blood moon to May 19, 1780 (the Dark Day of New England), when an unexplained darkness covered eastern New York and southern New England.

**Cross-references**: [Joel 2:10](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-10.htm), 31; 3:15; [Amos 8:8](https://biblehub.com/amos/8-8.htm); [Ezek. 38:19-20](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-19.htm); Hag. 2:6; [Isa. 13:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-10.htm), 13; 50:3; [Matt. 24:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-29.htm); [Rev. 16:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-18.htm).

### [Rev 6:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-13.htm) — "And the stars of heaven fell unto the earth, even as a fig tree casteth her untimely figs"

**Text**: "And the stars of heaven fell unto the earth, even as a fig tree casteth her untimely figs, when she is shaken of a mighty wind."

**Exegesis**: The image is drawn from [Isaiah 34:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-4.htm) — "all the host of heaven will wear away, and the sky will be rolled up like a scroll; all their host will also wither away as a leaf withers from the vine, or as one withers from the fig tree." John selects the fig tree image specifically: late-season figs (unripe, winter figs) that have not fallen naturally are violently shaken off by a strong wind — they drop massively, suddenly, all at once. The image conveys the sudden, irresistible, comprehensive nature of the cosmic disruption.

Jesus predicted in his Olivet discourse: "The stars will fall from the sky and the powers of heaven will be shaken" ([Matt. 24:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-29.htm)). Historicist scholars identify the fulfillment of this sign in the spectacular Leonid meteor shower of November 13, 1833, which produced tens of thousands of visible meteors per hour and was witnessed across North America — observers described the stars as "falling like snowflakes."

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 34:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-4.htm); [Matt. 24:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-29.htm); [Rev. 6:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-14.htm).

### [Rev 6:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-14.htm) — "And the heaven departed as a scroll when it is rolled together"

**Text**: "And the heaven departed as a scroll when it is rolled together; and every mountain and island were moved out of their places."

**Exegesis**: The image of the sky being rolled up like a scroll comes from [Isaiah 34:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-4.htm) (the same verse providing v. 13's imagery): "the sky will be rolled up like a scroll." The Hellenistic scholar Aratus's astronomical poem *Phaenomena* uses similar language; John's language is independently drawn from Isaiah. The image conveys the unmaking of the visible heavens — the withdrawal of the familiar created order before the coming of God.

Mountains and islands moved from their places: Jeremiah speaks of mountains "quaking and all the hills moved to and fro" at God's coming ([Jer. 4:24](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-24.htm)). The removal of mountains and islands points to the total geological disruption accompanying the divine presence. [Revelation 6:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-14.htm) most likely refers to the *final* earthquake mentioned again in 16:18, which is distinct from and greater than the first (6:12). This is the earthquake that accompanies the Second Coming itself. At 16:18 it is said to be "such as there had not been since man came to be upon the earth, so great an earthquake was it and so mighty."

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 34:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-4.htm); [Jer. 4:24](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-24.htm); [Rev. 16:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-18.htm); Hag. 2:6.

### [Rev 6:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-15.htm) — "And the kings of the earth, and the great men ... hid themselves in the dens and in the rocks"

**Text**: "And the kings of the earth, and the great men, and the rich men, and the chief captains, and the mighty men, and every bondman, and every free man, hid themselves in the dens and in the rocks of the mountains;"

**Exegesis**: The comprehensive seven-fold list — kings, magistrates, military commanders, rich, powerful, slaves, free — signals that no level of human society is exempt from judgment. No earthly status, wealth, or power provides protection when God appears. The list moves from the highest political authority (kings) to the lowest social class (slaves) — totality of human civilization confronting the divine. [Revelation 19:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-18.htm) completes this scene with scavenger birds feeding on "the flesh of the kings and the flesh of the commanders of one thousand troops and the flesh of the strong and the flesh of horses and of those sitting upon them and the flesh of all, both free men and slaves, the small and great."

Hiding in caves and rocks echoes the response of Adam and Eve hiding from God in the garden after sinning ([Gen. 3:8](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-8.htm)) — sin drives humanity away from the divine presence. The direct OT allusion is [Isaiah 2:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/2-19.htm)-21: "Men will go into caves of the rocks, and into holes of the ground before the terror of the LORD and the splendor of His majesty, when He arises to make the earth tremble ... In order to go into the caverns of the rocks and the clefts of the cliffs, before the terror of the LORD and the splendor of His majesty, when He arises to make the earth tremble." Jesus also employs this language in depicting his return ([Luke 23:30](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-30.htm)).

The wicked do not merely *flee* — they actively seek to be buried under mountains, the ultimate futility of trying to escape omnipresence.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 3:8](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-8.htm); [Isa. 2:19-21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/2-19.htm); [Hos. 10:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/10-8.htm); [Luke 23:30](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-30.htm); [Rev. 19:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-18.htm).

### [Rev 6:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-16.htm) — "And said to the mountains and rocks, Fall on us, and hide us from the face of him that sitteth on the throne"

**Text**: "And said to the mountains and rocks, Fall on us, and hide us from the face of him that sitteth on the throne, and from the wrath of the Lamb:"

**Exegesis**: The cry of the wicked is borrowed from [Hosea 10:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/10-8.htm) — "They will say to the mountains, `Cover us!' And to the hills, `Fall on us!'" — originally spoken of apostate Israel facing divine judgment. Jesus cites the same passage in [Luke 23:30](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-30.htm) to the weeping women of Jerusalem as he goes to the cross: "The daughters of Jerusalem, do not weep for me, but weep for yourselves ... For if they do these things in the green tree, what will happen in the dry?"

The phrase "the face of him that sitteth on the throne" recalls 20:11 where the earth and heaven "fled from the face of him that sat on the throne." The *face* of God (Heb. *panîm*; Gr. *prosōpon*) is the most terrifying reality for the unrepentant — it is the presence and gaze of the holy Judge from whom nothing is hidden. To desire the mountains to fall on them rather than face God is the ultimate expression of impenitence: they know judgment is due but refuse to repent.

The theological paradox of "the wrath of the Lamb" (*orgē tou arniou*) is striking and deliberate. The Lamb — the sacrificial victim who died for the world — is now revealed as the agent of divine judgment. This is not contradiction but completion: the Lamb who died to save those who accept him becomes the judge of those who reject him. The atonement, having been refused, becomes the ground of condemnation. "Wrath" (*orgē*) here is not arbitrary rage but the settled, holy response of divine righteousness to persistent, willful rebellion against the gospel (see fuller Notes on [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Hos. 10:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/10-8.htm); [Luke 23:30](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-30.htm); [Rev. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-11.htm); 14:10; [Mal. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/malachi/3-2.htm).

### [Rev 6:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-17.htm) — "For the great day of his wrath is come; and who shall be able to stand?"

**Text**: "For the great day of his wrath is come; and who shall be able to stand?"

**Exegesis**: "The great day of his wrath" (*hē hēmera hē megalē tēs orgēs autou*) is the Day of the Lord — the consummation of the entire OT eschatological tradition. [Joel 2:11](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-11.htm) asks: "For the day of the Lord is great and very awesome; who can endure it?" [Malachi 3:2](https://biblehub.com/malachi/3-2.htm) asks: "Who can endure the day of His coming? And who can stand when He appears?" [Nahum 1:6](https://biblehub.com/nahum/1-6.htm): "Who can stand before His indignation? Who can endure the burning of His anger?"

The question "Who shall be able to stand?" is the great rhetorical hinge of chapters 6-7. It is left unanswered in chapter 6 — deliberately. The answer comes in chapter 7: those who are able to stand are the sealed people of God, the 144,000 and the great multitude (7:3-4, 9). The sealing *must* be completed before the destructive winds are released (7:3). This corresponds precisely to the answer given to the martyrs in 6:11 — the waiting ends when the fellow servants are made complete. The judgment on the persecutors cannot come until the full number of God's faithful is perfected.

The scene of the sixth seal portrays the *Second Coming* described in OT terms of the Day of the Lord. The martyrs under the altar asked for God's intervention; the sixth seal shows that intervention beginning. Their persecutors — all classes of human society — flee in terror. God is about to judge the sinners and vindicate his faithful people. The promise of [Isaiah 54:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/54-10.htm) to the sealed saints stands: "For the mountains may be removed and the hills may shake, but My loving-kindness will not be removed from you, and My covenant of peace will not be shaken." Nahum's rhetorical question receives its answer: "The Lord is good, a stronghold in the day of trouble, and He knows those who take refuge in Him" ([Nah. 1:6-7](https://biblehub.com/nahum/1-6.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Joel 2:11](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-11.htm); [Mal. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/malachi/3-2.htm); [Nah. 1:6-7](https://biblehub.com/nahum/1-6.htm); [Isa. 54:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/54-10.htm); [Zeph. 1:14-15](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/1-14.htm); [Rev. 7:3-4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-3.htm), 9; 16:17-18.

**Structural summary of Chapter 6** (Stefanovic): The six seals form two groups. Seals 1-4 describe the ongoing consequences in human history of the gospel's proclamation and rejection — covenant curses (Lev. 26; Deut. 32) applied as redemptive warnings. The white horse (gospel advance) → red horse (persecution, rejection-driven conflict) → black horse (spiritual famine) → pale horse (death) is a progression from cause to ultimate effect, covering the entire Christian age from Pentecost to the Second Coming, with particular historical focus on the first millennium. Seals 5-6 shift to the eschatological: the martyrs' cry for justice (corresponding historically to the period of medieval persecution) and the cosmic signs and terror of the Second Coming answering that cry. The chapter ends with a question (v. 17) that chapter 7 answers, making the interlude of chapter 7 theologically integral to the seal sequence.
