## Revelation 10

**Structural position**: Interlude between the sixth and seventh trumpets (cf. Rev 7 between seals 6 and 7). Revelation 10–11:14 answers the ecclesiological question raised at the sixth seal: "What is the task of the Church in these troublous times?" (Beasley-Murray). The chapter divides into two units: the strong angel with the open little scroll (vv. 1–7) and John's commission to eat the scroll and prophesy again (vv. 8–11).

---

### [Rev 10:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-1.htm) — "And I saw another mighty angel"

**Text**: "And I saw another mighty angel come down from heaven, clothed with a cloud: and a rainbow was upon his head, and his face was as it were the sun, and his feet as pillars of fire."

**Exegesis**: The angel is designated *allon* ("another"), distinguishing him from the seven trumpet angels, not from Christ per se — the same "another" construction appears in 8:3 for the incense angel. He is *ischuros* (strong/mighty), the identical Greek term used for the angel of 5:2 who cried "Who is worthy to open the scroll?" — both are associated with heavenly scrolls, strongly linking chapters 5 and 10. In the OT, the angel sent by God often functions in the full divine authority of the sender ([Gen. 31:11](https://biblehub.com/genesis/31-11.htm)–13; [Exod. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/exodus/3-2.htm)–6; Judg. 13:6, 21–22), making the distinction between the angelic representative and God himself deliberately blurred.

The angel's composite description is saturated with Christ-imagery from Rev 1: face like the sun ([Matt. 17:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/17-2.htm); [Rev. 1:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-16.htm)), feet like pillars of fire ([Rev. 1:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-15.htm); [Dan. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/daniel/10-6.htm)), recalling the burnished bronze of the glorified Christ. The Adventist Bible Commentary and William Shea argue the angel *is* Christ; Stefanovic holds he is a high-ranking angel functioning as Christ's full representative and commissioned directly by him — "it is frequently hard to distinguish between the appearance of the angel sent and commissioned by Christ and the appearance of Christ himself."

**Cloud**: In Revelation, clouds accompany the coming of Jesus (1:7; 14:14–16); in the OT they signal divine presence ([Exod. 13:21](https://biblehub.com/exodus/13-21.htm); [1 Kgs. 8:10](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/8-10.htm)–11). The descending cloud indicates this angel comes directly from God's throne room.

**Rainbow over his head**: The rainbow (Gr. *iris*) is the sign of the Noahic covenant ([Gen. 9:12](https://biblehub.com/genesis/9-12.htm)–17), appearing around God's throne in [Ezek. 1:28](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/1-28.htm) and [Rev. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-3.htm). Here it is probably formed by light from the angel's face shining through the cloud. It signals God's covenantal faithfulness — the message about to be delivered is covenant-rooted.

**Feet as pillars of fire**: Recalls the pillar of fire that guided Israel through the wilderness ([Exod. 14:19](https://biblehub.com/exodus/14-19.htm)), an image of divine leadership and judgment. The combination of cloud (above) and fire (below) maps the entire Exodus theophany onto this single angelic figure.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 9:12](https://biblehub.com/genesis/9-12.htm)–17; [Exod. 13:21](https://biblehub.com/exodus/13-21.htm); 14:19; [Ezek. 1:28](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/1-28.htm); [Dan. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/daniel/10-6.htm); [Matt. 17:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/17-2.htm); [Rev. 1:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-13.htm)–16; 4:3; 5:2; 14:14.

### [Rev 10:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-2.htm) — "And he had in his hand a little book open"

**Text**: "And he had in his hand a little book open: and he set his right foot upon the sea, and his left foot on the earth."

**Exegesis**: **The little scroll** (Gr. *biblaridion*, diminutive of *biblion*). Stefanovic's analysis shows John uses *biblaridion*, *biblion*, and *biblos* without a consistent pattern (e.g., the book of life is *biblion* in 20:12 but *biblos* in 20:15). The use of *biblaridion* here contrasts this scroll with the large, sealed scroll of Rev 5 (*biblion*). In v. 8 the same object is called *biblion*, confirming no rigid terminological distinction. The probable purpose: to highlight by contrast that this scroll is smaller (a portion) and, crucially, already *open*.

**"Opened"** translates a perfect passive participle (*aneogmenon*). The **perfect tense** signals the scroll was opened at a prior point and remains in that open state. The **passive voice** is a divine passive — God opened it, not the angel. The angel is a courier, not the author. The sequence: the sealed scroll of Rev 5 was given to Christ; Christ broke its seals progressively through chapters 6–8; by chapter 10, a portion is now disclosed in opened form to John.

**Identity of the scroll**: Stefanovic identifies three major interpretive positions: (1) it is the same scroll as Rev 5 (same strong angel association); (2) it is a partial disclosure of that scroll — the *biblaridion* is a portion of the *biblion* containing what God's end-time people need to know (Rev 12–22 is its content); (3) it is the book of Daniel sealed "until the time of the end" ([Dan. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-4.htm), 9). The parallels between Dan. 12 and Rev. 10 (oath, sealed scroll, time-statement) strongly favor option (3) as a component of (2). The two are not mutually exclusive — the open little scroll may be Daniel's prophetic portion now unsealed, itself a subset of the larger sealed scroll of Rev 5.

**Feet on sea and land**: Standing astride both sea and earth signals the **universal scope** of the proclamation. The entire inhabited world falls under the angel's (and thus God's) jurisdiction. Notably, in Rev. 13, the two beasts emerge from sea and land respectively — the same cosmic geography over which the angel now stands in sovereign authority, declaring God's right to judge and deliver.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-4.htm), 9; [Isa. 8:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/8-16.htm); 29:9–14; [Rev. 5:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-1.htm)–5; 13:1, 11.

### [Rev 10:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-3.htm) — "And cried with a loud voice, as when a lion roareth"

**Text**: "And cried with a loud voice, as when a lion roareth: and when he had cried, seven thunders uttered their voices."

**Exegesis**: **Lion's roar**: In the OT, when God speaks judgment or prophetic warning, the sound is compared to a lion's roar ([Jer. 25:30](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-30.htm); [Hos. 11:10](https://biblehub.com/hosea/11-10.htm); [Joel 3:16](https://biblehub.com/joel/3-16.htm); [Amos 1:2](https://biblehub.com/amos/1-2.htm); 3:4). The Amos text is theologically decisive: "A lion has roared! Who will not fear? The Lord God has spoken! Who can but prophesy?" ([Amos 3:8](https://biblehub.com/amos/3-8.htm)). The roar thus carries a double force: it is a call to fear (judgment) and a compulsion to prophesy — both themes that play out through the rest of Rev. 10. When a strong angel announces in Revelation, it is always in a loud voice (cf. 5:2; 7:2; 14:7, 9, 15; 18:2), marking the solemnity of divine declaration.

**Seven thunders** (Gr. *hai hepta brontai*, with the definite article): The definite article is significant — "the seven thunders," as if a recognized entity. This suggests familiarity to John's audience, though the precise referent is never specified. The most likely OT background is **Psalm 29**, where God's voice is sevenfold (vv. 3–9), described as thunder. A later Jewish tradition held that "the voice of Yahweh on Sinai was heard as seven thunders." Throughout Revelation, thunder functions as revelatory forewarning of divine judgment-activity: it precedes the breaking of the seals (4:5; 6:1), the trumpet plagues (8:5), the cosmic conflict leading to the last plagues (11:19), and the final judgment (16:18). The number seven denotes divine fullness and totality — the seven thunders thus represent the complete forewarning of God's coming actions.

The seven thunders speak *articulately* — John hears intelligible content, not mere sound. This distinguishes them from ordinary thunder and heightens the shock of the command not to write.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 29:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/29-3.htm)–9; [Jer. 25:30](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/25-30.htm); [Hos. 11:10](https://biblehub.com/hosea/11-10.htm); [Joel 3:16](https://biblehub.com/joel/3-16.htm); [Amos 1:2](https://biblehub.com/amos/1-2.htm); 3:8; [Rev. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-5.htm); 6:1; 8:5; 11:19; 16:18.

### [Rev 10:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-4.htm) — "Seal up those things which the seven thunders uttered"

**Text**: "And when the seven thunders had uttered their voices, I was about to write: and I heard a voice from heaven saying unto me, Seal up those things which the seven thunders uttered, and write them not."

**Exegesis**: John's instinct is to write — consistent with his commission throughout the book (1:11, 19; 14:13; 19:9; 21:5). Here a voice from heaven (either the Father or Christ) explicitly countermands that instinct: "Seal up … and do not write them."

This is the **one place in Revelation where John is told to seal**. Everywhere else he is commanded *not* to seal (22:10: "do not seal the words of the prophecy of this book, for the time is near"). The prohibition is jarring and intentional. The theological framework draws on [Deut. 29:29](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/29-29.htm): "The secret things belong to the Lord our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our sons forever." Paul's parallel experience: caught up to the third heaven, he "heard inexpressible words, which a man is not permitted to speak" ([2 Cor. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/12-4.htm)). Some divine knowledge is withheld because its disclosure would not serve the church's preparation.

Bauckham's summary is precise: "the seven thunders are not the prophetic revelation given to him [John] to communicate, whereas the content of the little scroll is." The contrast defines the chapter: the sealed thunders (hidden) versus the eaten scroll (disclosed). What the seven thunders contain remains permanently unrevealed — any speculation about their content is illegitimate exegetically, since John himself was forbidden knowledge.

**Cross-references**: [Deut. 29:29](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/29-29.htm); [2 Cor. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/12-4.htm); [Rev. 1:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-11.htm), 19; 14:13; 19:9; 21:5; 22:10.

### [Rev 10:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-5.htm) — "And the angel… lifted up his hand to heaven"

**Text**: "And the angel which I saw stand upon the sea and upon the earth lifted up his hand to heaven."

**Exegesis**: The gesture of **raising the hand to swear an oath** is rooted in OT legal and covenantal practice ([Gen. 14:22](https://biblehub.com/genesis/14-22.htm); [Deut. 32:40](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-40.htm); [Ezek. 20:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/20-5.htm)–6; [Dan. 12:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-7.htm)). The KJV says "his hand" (singular, right hand); the Greek text uses the right hand. In [Daniel 12:5](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-5.htm)–7, the heavenly figure raises *both* hands toward heaven when swearing his oath regarding the duration of persecution. John's angel raises one hand — a formal oath-gesture signaling absolute divine authority and unbreakable commitment.

The angel is described as standing with one foot on the sea and one on the land (v. 2), so the raised hand posture with scroll in the other hand creates a visual tableau of cosmic sovereignty: the messenger spans the entire earth and swears by the Creator of that earth. The structural parallel to [Daniel 12:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-7.htm) is John's direct signal to read Revelation 10 through the lens of Daniel's end-time discourse.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 14:22](https://biblehub.com/genesis/14-22.htm); [Deut. 32:40](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-40.htm); [Ezek. 20:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/20-5.htm)–6; [Dan. 12:5](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-5.htm)–7; [Heb. 6:13](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/6-13.htm)–18.

### [Rev 10:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-6.htm) — "That there should be time no longer"

**Text**: "And sware by him that liveth for ever and ever, who created heaven, and the things that therein are, and the earth, and the things that therein are, and the sea, and the things which are therein, that there should be time no longer."

**Exegesis**: The oath is sworn by **God as eternal Creator** — the comprehensive formula ("heaven … earth … sea") echoes the creation account and appears in the Decalogue rationale ([Exod. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/exodus/20-11.htm)). Swearing by the Creator of all things is swearing by the highest conceivable authority; the eternal God's oath is, by definition, the most binding possible commitment ([Heb. 6:17](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/6-17.htm)–18).

**"There should be time no longer"** (Gr. *hoti chronos ouketi estai*): This is the most exegetically contested phrase in Rev. 10. Greek has two time-words:
- *Kairos*: a fixed point or appointed season
- *Chronos*: duration, a span of time

The phrase uses *chronos*. Two major interpretations:
1. **"No more delay"** — many scholars read this as "the eschatological delay is over; events begin now." David Aune contests "delay" as an inappropriate rendering since it implies postponement; he argues the phrase means "the time will be up" — the clock expires, events are irrevocably triggered.
2. **End of prophetic time** — the SDA/Adventist reading, grounded in the parallel with Dan. 12. In [Dan. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-4.htm)–9, the sealed scroll and the swearing of an oath by "the One who lives forever" address the end of a specific prophetic time-period. Ellen G. White's formulation (quoted by Stefanovic): "This time… is not the end of this world's history, neither of probationary time, but of **prophetic time**, which should precede the advent of our Lord… After this period of time, reaching from 1842 to 1844, there can be no definite tracing of the prophetic time. The longest reckoning reaches to the autumn of 1844." On this reading, *chronos* in [Rev. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-6.htm) declares that the era of time-specific prophetic reckoning (Daniel's 2300-day/year period ending in 1844) is concluded — no further prophetic time-chain extends beyond it.

The **Daniel 12 parallels** that anchor the *chronos* discussion: (a) sealed scroll/command to seal ([Dan. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-4.htm); cf. [Rev. 10:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-4.htm)); (b) question "How long before fulfillment?" ([Dan. 12:5](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-5.htm)–6; cf. [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11); (c) raising of hands toward heaven ([Dan. 12:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-7.htm)); (d) oath by "him who lives forever" ([Dan. 12:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-7.htm); [Rev. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-6.htm)); (e) completion of a time-period; (f) "all these things will be completed" ([Dan. 12:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-7.htm); cf. [Rev. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm)). The density of these parallels is not coincidental — John is explicitly mapping his vision onto Daniel's end-time framework.

In [Revelation 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm)–11, the martyrs under the altar cry "How long?" and are told to wait *eti chronon mikron* ("a little time more"). [Revelation 10:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-6.htm) answers that plea: *chronos ouketi estai* — the waiting-time is finished. God's deliverance is now irrevocably set into motion.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/exodus/20-11.htm); [Dan. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-4.htm)–7; [Heb. 6:13](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/6-13.htm)–18; [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm)–11; 11:15–18.

### [Rev 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm) — "But in the days of the voice of the seventh angel… the mystery of God should be finished"

**Text**: "But in the days of the voice of the seventh angel, when he shall begin to sound, the mystery of God should be finished, as he hath declared to his servants the prophets."

**Exegesis**: The strong adversative *alla* ("but") pivots from the oath's negative declaration (no more *chronos*) to its positive content. The oath defines not just an ending but a fulfillment-horizon: "in the days of the voice of the seventh angel."

**"Mystery of God"** (Gr. *to mysterion tou theou*): In the NT, *mysterion* is always a divine secret formerly hidden but now being disclosed. Specific referents:
- **Dan. 2**: God reveals mysteries to Nebuchadnezzar about the latter days — the rise and fall of world empires culminating in God's eternal kingdom (2:28–29, 44–45)
- **[Amos 3:7](https://biblehub.com/amos/3-7.htm)**: "Surely the Lord God does nothing unless he reveals his secret counsel to his servants the prophets" — the standard OT theology of prophetic disclosure
- **[Rom. 16:25](https://biblehub.com/romans/16-25.htm)–26**: the mystery kept secret for long ages, now made known through the gospel proclamation
- **[Eph. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/3-4.htm)–12; [Col. 1:26](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-26.htm)–27**: the mystery of Christ, hidden for generations, now disclosed to the saints
- **[1 Cor. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/4-1.htm)**: believers are "stewards of the mysteries of God"

In Revelation's symbolic architecture, **the sealed scroll of Rev 5 *is* the mystery of God in image-form**: its contents sealed for ages, accessible only to Christ by virtue of his cross. Through the open little scroll, a portion has been disclosed. The *full* completion of the mystery is eschatological — it belongs to the sounding of the seventh angel ([Rev. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-15.htm)–18) and the final judgment scene of [Rev. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-11.htm)–15 where the book of destiny is opened before the universe and every case adjudicated.

**"As he hath declared to his servants the prophets"**: The fulfillment of the mystery is not a surprise — it was announced through the prophetic word ([Amos 3:7](https://biblehub.com/amos/3-7.htm); cf. Dan. 2; [Isa. 46:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/46-10.htm)). This declaration affirms the continuity between OT prophetic promise and NT eschatological completion. The prophets — Daniel especially — are the primary chain of transmission.

**Seventh trumpet timing**: When the seventh angel sounds (11:15), the heavenly proclamation declares: "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever." This is the completion of the mystery — the full establishment of the kingdom that has been history's telos since creation. The investigative/eschatological judgment that fully discloses the mystery to the universe ([Rev. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-11.htm)–15) includes "bringing to light the things hidden in the darkness and disclosing the motives of men's hearts" ([1 Cor. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/4-5.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 2:28](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-28.htm)–29, 44–45; [Amos 3:7](https://biblehub.com/amos/3-7.htm); [Rom. 16:25](https://biblehub.com/romans/16-25.htm)–26; [1 Cor. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/4-5.htm); [Eph. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/3-4.htm)–12; [Col. 1:26](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-26.htm)–27; [Rev. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-15.htm)–18; 20:11–15.

### [Rev 10:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-8.htm) — "Go and take the little book which is open"

**Text**: "And the voice which I heard from heaven spake unto me again, and said, Go and take the little book which is open in the hand of the angel which standeth upon the sea and upon the earth."

**Exegesis**: John shifts from passive observer to **active participant**. The same heavenly voice that sealed the thunders (v. 4) now directs John to approach the angel and take the scroll. The command structure is deliberate: John does not reach for it himself; he is sent (*hupago* = "go"). Divine initiative precedes prophetic reception.

The scroll is identified again as *the* open scroll in the angel's hand — the one the angel has been holding throughout the vision, already opened by divine action. John must go to where the angel stands — on sea and land — meaning John must approach the very locus of cosmic sovereignty to receive the message.

This verse introduces the **eating-of-the-scroll commission**, which has OT precedent in two major prophetic figures: Jeremiah and Ezekiel (see v. 9–10). The pattern — receive the scroll, eat it, taste sweetness, experience bitterness, go prophesy — is the standard prophetic commissioning sequence.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/2-8.htm)–3:4; [Jer. 15:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/15-16.htm); [Rev. 10:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-2.htm).

### [Rev 10:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-9.htm) — "Take it, and eat it up; and it shall make thy belly bitter, but it shall be in thy mouth sweet as honey"

**Text**: "And I went unto the angel, and said unto him, Give me the little book. And he said unto me, Take it, and eat it up; and it shall make thy belly bitter, but it shall be in thy mouth sweet as honey."

**Exegesis**: **Eating the scroll** is a commissioning act with two clear OT precedents:

1. **Ezekiel** ([Ezek. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/2-8.htm)–3:4): God commands Ezekiel to open his mouth and eat the scroll covered with lamentations, mourning, and woe. Ezekiel eats it and it is "sweet as honey" in his mouth. He is then sent to the house of Israel. The scroll's bitterness (lamentations written on it) becomes manifest only as the prophet engages his audience's rejection.

2. **Jeremiah** ([Jer. 15:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/15-16.htm)): "Thy words were found and I ate them, and thy words became to me a joy and the delight of my heart." Yet the same prophet writes: "I have become a laughing stock all day long; everyone mocks me" (20:7b–8). The initial sweetness of the word received privately gives way to the bitterness of public opposition and rejection.

**Sweetness in the mouth**: Receiving God's word is consistently associated with honey ([Ps. 19:10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/19-10.htm): "sweeter also than honey and the drippings of the honeycomb"; [Ps. 119:103](https://biblehub.com/psalms/119-103.htm): "sweeter than honey to my mouth"). The initial delight of prophetic reception — the joy of divine revelation, the clarity of understanding, the privilege of divine commission — is real, not feigned.

**Bitterness in the belly**: As the message is *digested* — internalized, proclaimed, opposed — the prophet encounters resistance, disappointment, derision. The belly (*koilia*) is the seat of the deepest interior experience. What begins as joy in the mouth becomes anguish in the gut.

The **angel predicts** the bittersweet sequence before John experiences it — divine forewarning of the price of prophetic ministry. John cannot be surprised by the bitterness because God tells him in advance it is coming.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 19:10](https://biblehub.com/psalms/19-10.htm); 119:103; [Jer. 15:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/15-16.htm); 20:7–8; [Ezek. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/2-8.htm)–3:4.

### [Rev 10:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-10.htm) — "And it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter"

**Text**: "And I took the little book out of the angel's hand, and ate it up; and it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter."

**Exegesis**: John's **obedient execution** matches the angel's prediction exactly — the symmetry between v. 9 (prediction) and v. 10 (fulfillment) is tight and deliberate, functioning as narrative confirmation that the angel's word is authoritative.

**Adventist Great Disappointment application**: Stefanovic identifies the formal Adventist reading: "Seventh-day Adventist Christians have seen in Revelation 10 a special prophetic significance for their life and mission. In John's bittersweet experience they have seen what is known as the Great Disappointment experienced by the Millerite movement in 1844." Under William Miller (Baptist lay preacher), Millerites from multiple Protestant denominations concluded that Christ would return in the fall of 1844. The expectation was intense — believers prepared thoroughly, shared the message widely, and experienced what they regarded as the sweetness of prophetic discovery. When the Second Coming did not occur on October 22, 1844, the disappointment was severe and literal. "The disappointed Millerites experienced in different ways the bitter taste of the message they believed and shared."

The Adventist hermeneutic: John eating the scroll and experiencing first sweetness then bitterness is understood as a symbolic pre-enactment of the Millerite experience. The 2300-day/year prophecy of [Dan. 8:14](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-14.htm) — ending in 1844 — was the *chronos* referenced in [Rev. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-6.htm). The message was sweet (the discovery of the prophetic timeline, the joy of expectation), bitter in the belly (the Great Disappointment when the predicted event was misidentified — not the second coming but the beginning of the heavenly sanctuary investigative judgment).

The broader exegetical point holds even apart from the specifically Adventist application: the pattern of prophetic sweetness-then-bitterness describes the universal experience of those who receive and then proclaim unpopular divine truth. The gospel is always "good news" — but its messengers regularly encounter opposition, derision, and apparent failure.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 3:3](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/3-3.htm); [Jer. 15:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/15-16.htm); [Dan. 8:14](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-14.htm); [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)–12.

### [Rev 10:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-11.htm) — "Thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings"

**Text**: "And he said unto me, Thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings."

**Exegesis**: **"Must"** (Gr. *dei*) signals divine necessity — this is not a suggestion but an obligation rooted in eschatological purpose. The same *dei* appears in [Rev. 1:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-1.htm) ("things which must shortly come to pass") and throughout the Gospels for Jesus' necessary suffering ([Luke 24:26](https://biblehub.com/luke/24-26.htm)). The prophetic commission is non-negotiable.

**"Again"** (*palin*): John has already prophesied — the earlier chapters of Revelation constitute his prophetic ministry to the seven churches. He believed the end was imminent after the sixth trumpet. The "again" indicates a renewed, broader commission — the end is not yet; there is one more proclamation before history closes.

**Preposition *epi* + dative**: The Greek *epi* with dative can mean "concerning/about" or "against." Scholars generally prefer "concerning" — John is commissioned to prophesy *about* (regarding, in reference to) many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings. This parallels [Jer. 46:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/46-1.htm): "the word of the Lord came to Jeremiah concerning the nations."

**Fourfold universal formula**: "Peoples, nations, tongues, and kings" — the Danielic universality formula ([Dan. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/3-4.htm), 7, 29; 4:1; 5:19; 6:25; 7:14). In Revelation this formula appears in several variations (5:9; 7:9; 11:9; 13:7; 14:6; 17:15). Here "kings" replaces the usual "tribes," underscoring that the prophetic message overrides even the highest human authority structures.

**"Prophesy again"** as eschatological gospel proclamation: The commission's content is Revelation 12–22 — the disclosure of the opened little scroll. The scope — worldwide, universal, encompassing all peoples — anticipates [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm), where John sees a symbolic angel flying in midheaven "preaching the eternal gospel to those who dwell on the earth, and to every nation and tribe and tongue and people." The connection to [Matt. 24:14](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-14.htm) is explicit in Stefanovic: "This gospel of the kingdom shall be preached in the whole world for a witness to all the nations, and then the end shall come." The commission to prophesy again is thus the commission to proclaim the final gospel message that brings the end — the worldwide Advent proclamation.

**Adventist ecclesiological application**: "In Christ's commission to John to 'prophesy again' to many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings, Adventists have seen the commissioning of God's end-time church to proclaim the message of the Second Coming 'to those who dwell on the earth, and to every nation and tribe and tongue and people' ([Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)). When the proclaimed gospel message is heard by the whole world, then the end will come and earth's history will reach its conclusion ([Matt. 24:14](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-14.htm))."

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/daniel/3-4.htm); 7:14; [Jer. 46:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/46-1.htm); [Matt. 24:14](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-14.htm); [Rev. 1:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-1.htm); 5:9; 7:9; 14:6; 14:14–20.

**Chapter 10 — Synthesis**

The chapter functions as the hinge between the trumpet-sequence and the second half of Revelation. The sealed scroll of Rev 5 (the mystery of God) has been opened progressively through seals and trumpets; now an open little scroll — a partial disclosure, identified closely with Daniel's sealed prophetic portion — is delivered to John by the supreme angelic representative of Christ. John must not merely receive but *embody* the message (eating), experiencing the prophetic pattern of sweet reception and bitter proclamation. The oath of v. 6 closes the era of prophetic time-calculation; no further definite prophetic *chronos* follows the 1844 endpoint. The mystery of God will be fully completed only at the seventh trumpet — the final eschatological judgment. The commissioned prophesying of v. 11 is the church's mandate for the final age: worldwide gospel proclamation across every people, tongue, nation, and ruling authority until the end comes.
