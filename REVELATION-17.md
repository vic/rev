## Revelation 17

---

### [Rev 17:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-1.htm) — "And there came one of the seven angels..."

**Text**: "And there came one of the seven angels which had the seven vials, and talked with me, saying unto me, Come hither; I will shew unto thee the judgment of the great whore that sitteth upon many waters:"

**Exegesis**: The angel is most likely the seventh-bowl angel who in 16:19 announced the collapse of Babylon. The same angel later summons John to show him New Jerusalem, the bride of Christ (21:9), establishing a structural antithesis: where Babylon is exposed and destroyed, the Bride is seen in true worth. The verb *deiknymi* ("show") frames this as an authorized disclosure of divine mystery. The phrase "sitting on many waters" (*epi hydatōn pollōn*) is drawn directly from [Jeremiah 51:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-13.htm), where it describes historical Babylon astride the Euphrates — a city that drew wealth from control of waterways. John borrows that image and transforms it: the waters are the world's secular nations (explained in v.15). "Sitting" (*kathēmenēs*) connotes dominion and control. The "judgment" (*krima*) frames the entire vision prospectively — John is not watching Babylon at her peak but being shown the logic of her condemnation. [Revelation 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm) and 16:19 have twice announced Babylon's fall without identifying her; chapter 17 is the unveiling.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-13.htm); [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 16:19; 21:9; cf. 1:4, 8.

### [Rev 17:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-2.htm) — "With whom the kings of the earth have committed fornication..."

**Text**: "With whom the kings of the earth have committed fornication, and the inhabitants of the earth have been made drunk with the wine of her fornication."

**Exegesis**: "Fornication" (*porneia*) is an OT prophetic metaphor for illicit alliances between apostate nations and/or cities. Isaiah called Tyre a city that "played harlot with all the kingdoms on the face of the earth" ([Isa. 23:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/23-17.htm)). Nahum announced Nineveh's judgment for her "harlotries" by which she "sells nations" ([Nah. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/nahum/3-4.htm)). Jeremiah speaks of Israel as "a harlot with many lovers" ([Jer. 3:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-1.htm); Hos. 3–4). Ezekiel accuses Israel of playing the harlot with Egypt, Assyria, and Chaldea ([Ezek. 16:26](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-26.htm)–29; 23:3–30). John's portrait of Babylon-as-prostitute also draws on Jezebel as the model for faithless Jerusalem ([Jer. 4:30](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-30.htm); cf. [2 Kings 9:30](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/9-30.htm)). The "kings of the earth" represent the governing political powers of the world who have placed their authority into the service of end-time Babylon — not for love but for economic advantage, security, and power. Their union is portrayed as sexual immorality because it is fundamentally an act of infidelity to God. The second clause describes a cascading effect: the "inhabitants of the earth" (*hoi katoikountes tēn gēn*) — a repeated phrase in Revelation always designating the godless who are hostile to God's people (cf. 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14) — are made drunk by following the lead of the kings. Drunkenness impairs moral reasoning; Babylon controls through deception, not raw compulsion.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 23:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/23-17.htm); [Nah. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/nahum/3-4.htm); [Jer. 3:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-1.htm); [Ezek. 16:26](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-26.htm)–29; 23:3–30; Hos. 3–4; [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm); 13:8; 14:8; 18:3, 9.

### [Rev 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm) — "So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness..."

**Text**: "So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast, full of names of blasphemy, having seven heads and ten horns."

**Exegesis**: This is John's fourth "in the Spirit" transport (cf. 1:10; 4:2; 17:3; 21:10), each marking a major visionary transition authenticated by the Holy Spirit rather than human will (cf. [2 Pet. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/1-20.htm)). The wilderness (*erēmos*) setting is theologically charged: it was in the wilderness that the red dragon persecuted the woman-church during the 1,260 prophetic days (12:6, 14). The very place of the church's persecution now becomes the stage for Babylon's judgment — a reversal of fortune. The woman sits "upon a scarlet beast" (*epi thērion... kokkinos*): she sits also on "many waters" (v.1), but this image is specific — her relationship to the beast as her vehicle and enforcer. The beast is scarlet (*kokkinos*), associating it with the red dragon of 12:3 (marking satanic origin), with blood and oppression ([2 Kings 3:22](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/3-22.htm)–23; [Rev. 6:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-4.htm)), and with Isaiah's description of harlot Jerusalem whose "hands are covered with blood" ([Isa. 1:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-15.htm)–23). The beast is "full of names of blasphemy" (*gemonta onomata blasphēmias*). In the NT, blasphemy is the act of claiming divine prerogatives ([Mark 2:7](https://biblehub.com/mark/2-7.htm)) or equality with God ([John 10:33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm); [Matt. 26:63](https://biblehub.com/matthew/26-63.htm)–65). This beast is identical to the sea beast of [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm) which had "blasphemous names" on its heads. Seven heads and ten horns are shared by the dragon (12:3), the sea beast (13:1), and this beast, indicating they are all manifestations of the same satanic power through history.

**Cross-references**: [2 Pet. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/1-20.htm); [Rev. 12:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-3.htm), 6, 14; 13:1, 6–7; [John 10:33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm); [Mark 2:7](https://biblehub.com/mark/2-7.htm); [2 Kings 3:22](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/3-22.htm)–23; [Isa. 1:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-15.htm)–23.

### [Rev 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm) — "And the woman was arrayed in purple and scarlet colour..."

**Text**: "And the woman was arrayed in purple and scarlet colour, and decked with gold and precious stones and pearls, having a golden cup in her hand full of abominations and filthiness of her fornication:"

**Exegesis**: Purple (*porphyra*) was the color of royalty in antiquity (Judg. 8:26; [Esther 8:15](https://biblehub.com/esther/8-15.htm); [Song 3:10](https://biblehub.com/songs/3-10.htm); [Dan. 5:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/5-7.htm)). Scarlet in the context of a prostitute recalls [Jeremiah 4:30](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-30.htm), where harlot-Jerusalem arrays herself in scarlet to seduce her lovers. The combination of purple (royal authority) and scarlet (seductive harlotry) signals that this end-time system simultaneously claims kingship and practices immorality. The gold, precious stones, and pearls are a direct antithesis to the New Jerusalem, whose foundations and gates are also adorned with precious stones and pearls (21:11, 19–21). In the OT, precious stones are associated with the visible presence of God; here Babylon's adornment is an arrogant counterfeit of divine glory, expressing "arrogance and a desire to dominate." Harlot cities in the OT are consistently characterized by extravagant wealth combined with moral corruption ([Isa. 1:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-21.htm)–22; [Jer. 51:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-13.htm); [Ezek. 16:10](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-10.htm)–13; [Nah. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/nahum/2-9.htm)). The golden cup (*potērion chrysoun*) alludes to [Jeremiah 51:7](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-7.htm): "Babylon has been a golden cup in the hand of the LORD, intoxicating all the earth." The irony is devastating: what appears to be a precious offering (*chrysoun* = gold) is filled with "abominations" (*bdelygmatōn*) and the "unclean things of her fornication." Babylon deceives with beauty while dispensing poison.

**Cross-references**: Judg. 8:26; [Esther 8:15](https://biblehub.com/esther/8-15.htm); [Dan. 5:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/5-7.htm); [Jer. 4:30](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-30.htm); 51:7–8; [Ezek. 16:10](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-10.htm)–13; [Isa. 1:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-21.htm)–22; [Nah. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/nahum/2-9.htm); [Rev. 18:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-16.htm); 21:11, 19–21.

### [Rev 17:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-5.htm) — "And upon her forehead was a name written, MYSTERY, BABYLON THE GREAT..."

**Text**: "And upon her forehead was a name written, MYSTERY, BABYLON THE GREAT, THE MOTHER OF HARLOTS AND ABOMINATIONS OF THE EARTH."

**Exegesis**: Roman prostitutes wore frontlets (*tituli*) on their foreheads bearing their names in public brothels — the inscription identifies the woman as a prostitute openly engaged in commerce (Seneca, *Controversiae* 1.2.7; Juvenal, *Saturae* 6.123). Jeremiah refers to "a harlot's forehead" ([Jer. 3:3](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-3.htm)). "Mystery" (*mystērion*) most likely functions as a prefix to the title rather than as part of the proper name "Mystery Babylon." It indicates that Babylon is not to be understood in a literal, geographical sense but as a cryptic, symbolic name — a divine secret now being disclosed. Only those with spiritual discernment can grasp its full meaning (cf. v.9: "the mind which hath wisdom"). "Babylon the great" echoes [Daniel 4:30](https://biblehub.com/daniel/4-30.htm) (Nebuchadnezzar's boast) and [Revelation 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm) and 16:19. Babylon in the OT is the archetype of rebellion against God — its ambition was to "reach heaven in order to usurp the place and the ruling power of God" ([Gen. 11:4](https://biblehub.com/genesis/11-4.htm); [Isa. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/14-13.htm)–14; [Jer. 51:53](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-53.htm)). As such it became the source of all organized rebellion throughout history. "The mother of harlots" (*mētēr tōn pornōn*) means that end-time Babylon is the organizational source, the matrix, of all apostate religious systems — a direct antithesis to the heavenly Jerusalem which is "our mother" ([Gal. 4:26](https://biblehub.com/galatians/4-26.htm)). Babylon is not merely one false system among many; she mothers them all. The children of Babylon bear the mark of the beast on their foreheads (13:17); the offspring of the true woman bear God's name on their foreheads (14:1) — a direct forehead-to-forehead contrast.

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 3:3](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-3.htm); 51:53; [Gen. 11:4](https://biblehub.com/genesis/11-4.htm); [Isa. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/14-13.htm)–14; [Dan. 4:30](https://biblehub.com/daniel/4-30.htm); [Gal. 4:26](https://biblehub.com/galatians/4-26.htm); [Rev. 13:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-17.htm); 14:1, 8; 16:19.

### [Rev 17:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-6.htm) — "And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints..."

**Text**: "And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints, and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus: and when I saw her, I wondered with great admiration."

**Exegesis**: The woman who makes others drunk (v.2) is herself drunk — with blood. This is a stunning inversion. Her drunkenness (*methyousa*) on "the blood of the saints and the blood of the witnesses of Jesus" echoes the cry of the fifth-seal martyrs: "How long, O Lord... will you not judge and avenge our blood upon those who dwell on the earth?" (6:10). End-time Babylon is directly responsible for the death decree of the final crisis (cf. 13:14–17). The two phrases — "saints" (*hagiōn*) and "witnesses of Jesus" (*martyrōn Iēsou*) — refer to the same group from two angles: those set apart by God and those who bore testimony to Jesus at cost of their lives. Babylon's persecution of the faithful is a continuation of the sea beast's war against the saints (13:7). John's reaction is extraordinary: he "wondered with great admiration" (*ethaumasen thauma mega*) — the same Greek root used in v.8 for the world marveling at the beast. The repetition is deliberate: the world will marvel at the beast, but John marvels at the woman who sits upon it. His astonishment is the trigger for the angel's interpretive revelation in v.7.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm); 13:7, 14–17; 12:11; 20:4; cf. 2:13.

### [Rev 17:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-7.htm) — "And the angel said unto me, Wherefore didst thou marvel?"

**Text**: "And the angel said unto me, Wherefore didst thou marvel? I will tell thee the mystery of the woman, and of the beast that carrieth her, which hath the seven heads and ten horns."

**Exegesis**: The angel's rhetorical question redirects John's awe toward understanding (*gnōsis*). The angel offers to explain "the mystery" (*to mystērion*) of both the woman *and* the beast — not one or the other, because the two are inseparable. Babylon derives her character and power from the beast; understanding the beast is the key to understanding end-time Babylon. The fact that the angel explains the beast (vv.8–14) before returning to the woman (vv.15–18) confirms this interpretive priority: the woman cannot be identified apart from the beast she rides. The repetition of "seven heads and ten horns" anchors the vision in the dragon (12:3) and sea beast (13:1) imagery. This literary gesture assures the reader that all three entities — dragon, sea beast, and Babylon's beast — are manifestations of the same satanic power operating in different modes across history.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 12:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-3.htm); 13:1; 17:3.

### [Rev 17:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-8.htm) — "The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit..."

**Text**: "The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit, and go into perdition: and they that dwell on the earth shall wonder, whose names were not written in the book of life from the foundation of the world, when they behold the beast that was, and is not, and yet is."

**Exegesis**: The tripartite formula "was, and is not, and is about to come" (*ēn kai ouk estin kai mellei*) is a direct parody of God's covenant name "who was and who is and who is coming" (*ho ōn kai ho ēn kai ho erchomenos*, 1:4, 8; 4:8). The formula applied to God expresses eternal self-existence; the same pattern applied to the beast exposes his imitation as a counterfeit. Where God's "coming" is salvific, the beast's "coming up" is from the abyss (*abyssos*) — the abode of demons (cf. [Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm); [2 Pet. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-4.htm); Jude 6; [Rom. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/romans/10-7.htm)). The beast passes through three historical phases: (1) "was" — the sea beast of Rev. 13 actively persecuting the saints during the forty-two months; (2) "is not" — the beast receives the mortal wound, temporarily "dead," absent from the world scene; (3) "is about to come up" — the resurrection of the beast in renewed satanic fury at the end of the age (cf. [2 Thess. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-8.htm)–12). The descent into the abyss corresponds to the mortal wound; ascending from it corresponds to the healing of that wound. A. Yarbro Collins connects the ascending beast to the demonic locusts of chapter 9 (also from the abyss), who share the same function of demonic oppression. The pattern "was and is not and will come" is also called "an epitaph used widely in the ancient world" to mean one who "lived" and "who no longer lives" — here applied to a corporate entity, not an individual. Those who "dwell on the earth" (*katoikountes tēn gēn*) — always God's opponents in Revelation — marvel at this reappearance just as 13:3–4 describes the whole earth marveling at the healed sea beast. The parallel between 13:8 and 17:8 ("names not written in the book of life from the foundation of the world") confirms that the beast of chapter 17 is the same entity as the sea beast of chapter 13. Only those with their names in the book of life will resist the deception.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-4.htm), 8; 4:8; 9:1–2; 11:7; 13:3–4, 8; 19:20; [2 Thess. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-8.htm)–12; [Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm); [Rom. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/romans/10-7.htm).

### [Rev 17:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-9.htm) — "And here is the mind which hath wisdom. The seven heads are seven mountains..."

**Text**: "And here is the mind which hath wisdom. The seven heads are seven mountains, on which the woman sitteth."

**Exegesis**: "Here is the mind which hath wisdom" (*hōde ho nous ho echōn sophian*) is a call to theological discernment, not geographical or mathematical calculation — the same call given before the number 666 (13:18). When divine wisdom is invoked, symbolic and theological interpretation is required, not literal identification. The declaration has been used by many commentators to identify Rome — the famed "city on seven hills" — as Babylon. However, the angel immediately explains that the seven mountains are seven kings (v.10), meaning they are used metaphorically (cf. [Dan. 7:17](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-17.htm) where "kings" mean "kingdoms"). Mountains in the OT are used consistently as symbols of kingdoms and empires ([Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm); [Dan. 2:35](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-35.htm), 44–45; [Amos 4:1](https://biblehub.com/amos/4-1.htm)), never as symbols of individual rulers — see also [Rev. 8:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-8.htm). The seven mountains and the seven heads/kings are consecutive (successive in time), not parallel or simultaneous — this rules out identifying them with seven hills simultaneously visible from one vantage point. As Alan Johnson argues: "Whenever divine wisdom is called for, the description requires theological and symbolical discernment, not mere geographical or numerical insight." George Ladd's summary: "The great prostitute sits upon a succession of empires. She found her embodiment in historical Babylon, in the first century in historical Rome, and at the end of the age in eschatological Babylon. No simple identification with any single historical city is possible."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 13:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-18.htm); [Dan. 7:17](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-17.htm); [Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm); [Dan. 2:35](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-35.htm), 44–45; [Rev. 8:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-8.htm).

### [Rev 17:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-10.htm) — "And there are seven kings: five are fallen, and one is, and the other is not yet come..."

**Text**: "And there are seven kings: five are fallen, and one is, and the other is not yet come; and when he cometh, he must continue a short space."

**Exegesis**: The seven heads = seven mountains = seven kings: a chain of symbolic equivalences. "Kings" here stands for "kingdoms" or "empires" (cf. [Dan. 7:17](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-17.htm)). Two main interpretations compete: (A) Seven Roman Emperors — five fallen (Augustus, Tiberius, Caligula, Claudius, Nero), one ruling (Vespasian), the seventh yet to come (Titus). But this requires excluding Galba, Otho, and Vitellius for brevity of reign, which is arbitrary; it places composition under Vespasian (A.D. 69–79), contradicting strong evidence for Domitian's reign (A.D. 81–96); and 17:8, 11 explicitly state the beast "is not" at the time of writing, making neither Vespasian nor Domitian a clean fit. (B) Seven successive world empires that have oppressed God's people: five fallen = Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece; the one existing at John's writing = Imperial Rome; the seventh "not yet come" = medieval ecclesiastical Christianity (the sea beast of Rev. 13 with its forty-two months of authority). This interpretation best fits the full scope of Revelation's OT allusions and the chapter's own context. The seventh "must continue a short space" (*oligon autōn dei meinai*) — its duration, though historically extended, is "short" from an eschatological perspective. The healing of its mortal wound produces the eighth (v.11).

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 7:17](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-17.htm), 24; [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm)–10; cf. [Dan. 2:31](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-31.htm)–45.

### [Rev 17:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-11.htm) — "And the beast that was, and is not, even he is the eighth..."

**Text**: "And the beast that was, and is not, even he is the eighth, and is of the seven, and goeth into perdition."

**Exegesis**: This is the interpretive crux of the chapter. The beast is simultaneously "the eighth" (*ogdoos*) and "of the seven" — it is distinct yet continuous. The eighth head is the revived seventh: the seventh kingdom (medieval ecclesiastical Christianity, the sea beast of Rev. 13), which received a mortal wound and appeared to die, re-emerges at the end of history as the eighth manifestation of satanic power. "Of the seven" (*ek tōn hepta*) most likely means it is specifically "of" — arising from — the seventh, the same power in a new phase. The formula "was and is not" recalls the epitaph of death but adds eschatological revival — a direct parody of Christ who "was put to death yet came back to life and now is alive forevermore" (1:18; 2:8; cf. 5:5). The parody is precise: Christ's resurrection is unto eternal life; the beast's resurrection is unto *perdition* (*apōleian*). As Robert L. Thomas notes, each head is "a partial incarnation of satanic power that rules for a given period." The eighth is the final, fullest incarnation — the beast of Rev. 17 upon which Babylon sits — before the ultimate destruction at the Second Coming (19:20). "We evidently live in the era of the seventh head, for the eighth head with its united ten kingdoms has not yet ascended from the abyss."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-18.htm); 2:8; 5:5; 13:3, 12; 19:20; [Dan. 7:21](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-21.htm)–25.

### [Rev 17:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-12.htm) — "And the ten horns which thou sawest are ten kings, which have received no kingdom as yet..."

**Text**: "And the ten horns which thou sawest are ten kings, which have received no kingdom as yet; but receive power as kings one hour with the beast."

**Exegesis**: The ten horns echo [Daniel 7:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-7.htm), 24 — horns arise from the fourth beast and receive royal authority. The phrase "have not yet received a kingdom" (*basileian oupō elabon*) confirms from John's vantage point that the ten kings are purely eschatological, future entities — not historical Parthian satraps (the Nero redivivus view), since there were fourteen Parthian satraps, not ten, and they had existing kingdoms. Isbon T. Beckwith correctly identifies them as "purely eschatological figures representing the totality of the powers of all nations on the earth which are to be made subservient to Antichrist." The "one hour" (*mian hōran*) is a brief, concentrated period of the final crisis — not sixty minutes but a short eschatological span of time (cf. 18:10, 17, 19 where Babylon falls "in one hour"). Ten is the number of totality in Revelation (cf. 2:10); the ten kings represent the complete unity of world political powers marshaled behind the beast in the end time. Their authority is conferred *with* the beast — they do not precede or supersede him but co-reign in subordination to his agenda.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 7:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-7.htm), 24; [Rev. 18:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-10.htm), 17, 19; cf. 2:10.

### [Rev 17:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-13.htm) — "These have one mind, and shall give their power and strength unto the beast."

**Text**: "These have one mind, and shall give their power and strength unto the beast."

**Exegesis**: "One mind" (*mian gnōmēn echousin*) describes unprecedented political unity — the ten kings form a monolithic bloc with no dissent. This unity is given to the beast: they voluntarily surrender their sovereignty. The ten kings will become "the backbone in the creation of the religious-political confederacy in the final crisis." This is the political dimension of Babylon's dominion: the apostate religious system (Babylon/the woman) rides and directs the beast, which in turn is supported by the unified political powers (the ten kings). The structure is hierarchical: God → Christ → the saints; Satan → the beast → the ten kings → the nations. The ten kings give their power to the beast; the beast gives it to Babylon. This voluntarism distinguishes the final crisis from earlier persecutions — the political powers are not coerced but seduced (cf. v.2: they have "committed fornication" willingly).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 17:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-17.htm); 16:13–14; 13:12; cf. [Dan. 7:24](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-24.htm).

### [Rev 17:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-14.htm) — "These shall make war with the Lamb, and the Lamb shall overcome them..."

**Text**: "These shall make war with the Lamb, and the Lamb shall overcome them: for he is Lord of lords, and King of kings: and they that are with him are called, and chosen, and faithful."

**Exegesis**: The climactic battle described here (the ten kings + beast vs. the Lamb) is not narrated at this point but in 19:11–21 — this verse provides the outcome in preview. The combat is theological, not military: Babylon, the beast, and the ten kings aim to destroy God's people through deception and persecution (13:15–17), but the Lamb overcomes (*nikaō*) them. This answers the rhetorical question of 13:4: "Who is able to wage war with the beast?" The answer: the Lamb. The title "Lord of lords and King of kings" (*kyrios kyriōn kai basileus basileōn*) is the reversal of the beast's blasphemous self-claim. In the OT, this title belongs to YHWH alone ([Deut. 10:17](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/10-17.htm); [Ps. 136:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/136-3.htm); [Dan. 2:47](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-47.htm)); Revelation appropriates it for Christ (cf. 19:16). The Lamb's companions are described by three adjectives: "called" (*klētoi*), "chosen" (*eklektoi*), and "faithful" (*pistoi*). The order is significant — calling precedes election, and both precede faithfulness, but faithfulness is what distinguishes the genuinely redeemed. The overcoming of the Lamb through his followers echoes 12:11: "They overcame him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 19:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-11.htm)–21; 13:4, 15–17; 12:11; 19:16; [Deut. 10:17](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/10-17.htm); [Ps. 136:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/136-3.htm); [Dan. 2:47](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-47.htm).

### [Rev 17:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-15.htm) — "And he saith unto me, The waters which thou sawest, where the whore sitteth, are peoples..."

**Text**: "And he saith unto me, The waters which thou sawest, where the whore sitteth, are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues."

**Exegesis**: The angel provides the authoritative decoding of the symbol introduced in v.1. The "many waters" upon which Babylon sits are not the literal Euphrates ([Jer. 51:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-13.htm)) but the full breadth of world nations: "peoples and multitudes and nations and tongues" (*laoi kai ochloi kai ethnē kai glōssai*) — the four-fold formula appears throughout Revelation (5:9; 7:9; 10:11; 11:9; 13:7; 14:6) always denoting universality of scope. In the OT, water is used figuratively for the enemies of God's people (Pss. 18:4; 124:2–5; [Isa. 8:7](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/8-7.htm)–8; [Jer. 47:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/47-1.htm)–2). The image means that Babylon's power rests upon a global base of popular secular support. The same nations whose "drying up" (16:12 — the Euphrates drying) signals the withdrawal of Babylon's support are explained here as the structural underpinning of her authority. This popular support is temporary — "it will last but a short time."

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-13.htm); [Rev. 16:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-12.htm); 5:9; 7:9; 13:7; Pss. 18:4; 124:2–5; [Isa. 8:7](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/8-7.htm)–8.

### [Rev 17:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-16.htm) — "And the ten horns which thou sawest upon the beast, these shall hate the whore..."

**Text**: "And the ten horns which thou sawest upon the beast, these shall hate the whore, and shall make her desolate and naked, and shall eat her flesh, and burn her with fire."

**Exegesis**: The dramatic reversal of v.16 is the interpretive key to the sixth plague (16:12): the drying of the Euphrates symbolizes exactly this — the withdrawal of political support from Babylon. The nations that loyally served Babylon become her executioners. The reason for the reversal is not explicit here; the commentary suggests the political powers, after Babylon proves impotent to protect herself (cf. 16:10–11, 19), become disillusioned, turn hostile, and act with savage fury. This echoes the OT pattern of enemies turning on each other in the eschatological turmoil ([Ezek. 38:21](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-21.htm); Hag. 2:22; [Zech. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-13.htm)). The specific acts — "desolate and naked," "eat her flesh," "burn her with fire" — are drawn directly from Ezekiel's prophecies of judgment against prostitute Jerusalem ([Ezek. 16:37](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-37.htm)–41; 23:22–29), in both of which former lovers strip, expose, devour, and burn the harlot. "Eating flesh" (*sarkas autēs phagontai*) is the savage action of an extreme enemy ([Ps. 27:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/27-2.htm); [Mic. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/micah/3-2.htm)–3; [Jer. 10:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-25.htm)). "Burning with fire" (*katakauthēsontai*) is the Mosaic penalty for sexual immorality ([Lev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/20-14.htm); 21:9) — John applies the sexual immorality punishment framework to Babylon's ultimate fate. The pattern of Satan's kingdom destroying its predecessor (Babylon destroyed Assyria, Persia destroyed Babylon...) reaches its ultimate expression here, and then the battle of Armageddon "breaks this cycle" as God himself intervenes.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 16:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-12.htm); [Ezek. 16:37](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-37.htm)–41; 23:22–29; [Ps. 27:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/27-2.htm); [Mic. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/micah/3-2.htm)–3; [Lev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/20-14.htm); 21:9; [Jer. 10:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-25.htm); [Ezek. 38:21](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-21.htm); [Zech. 14:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-13.htm).

### [Rev 17:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-17.htm) — "For God hath put in their hearts to fulfil his will..."

**Text**: "For God hath put in their hearts to fulfil his will, and to agree, and give their kingdom unto the beast, until the words of God shall be fulfilled."

**Exegesis**: The theological pivot of the chapter: behind the apparent chaos of political powers turning on Babylon is the sovereign will of God (*ho theos edōken eis tas kardias autōn*). God places in the hearts of the ten kings both the unity of purpose ("to agree," *mian gnōmēn poiēsai*) and the action of giving their kingdom to the beast — so that in thinking they serve the beast's purposes, they are actually executing God's judgment on Babylon. This is a case of divine irony: the wicked, in following their own rebellious inclinations, fulfill God's decree. Paul articulates the same principle: "God will send upon them a deluding influence so that they might believe what is false, in order that they all may be judged who did not believe the truth" ([2 Thess. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-10.htm)–12). William Barclay: "God never loses control of human affairs. In the last analysis God is always working things together for good." The "until the words of God shall be fulfilled" (*achri telesthēsontai hoi logoi tou theou*) establishes the temporal limit of the beast's collaboration with Babylon — it serves God's redemptive-historical timetable, after which God withdraws permission and the alliance implodes. The sovereignty of God over even satanic political alliances is here emphatically declared.

**Cross-references**: [2 Thess. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-10.htm)–12; [Rev. 16:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-19.htm); cf. [Isa. 10:5](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/10-5.htm)–7 (Assyria as God's unwitting instrument); [Dan. 4:17](https://biblehub.com/daniel/4-17.htm).

### [Rev 17:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-18.htm) — "And the woman which thou sawest is that great city, which reigneth over the kings of the earth."

**Text**: "And the woman which thou sawest is that great city, which reigneth over the kings of the earth."

**Exegesis**: The final verse provides the culminating identification: the prostitute woman = the great city (*hē polis hē megalē*) which rules over (*basileuousa epi*) the kings of the earth. "The great city" is used in Revelation also for fallen Babylon (16:19; 17:18; 18:10, 16, 18–19, 21) and for the spiritual Sodom/Egypt where the Lord was crucified (11:8). The equation "woman = city" mirrors the Bride of Christ = New Jerusalem equation (21:2, 9–10): both women are cities, both cities are women, but one is the counterfeit of the other. Babylon is explicitly a religious system, not merely a political one — it "rules over the kings" rather than being ruled by them. This reverses the apparent surface image where Babylon rides the beast (seeming to be the weaker party); she is in fact the ruling party. The commentary is emphatic: "Babylon cannot be identified as Imperial Rome, for it is not a political but rather a religious system dominating political powers of the world at the conclusion of this world's history." Babylon the prostitute and Babylon the city are one entity — the end-time worldwide apostate religious confederacy empowered by the satanic trinity (dragon, sea beast, earth beast) opposing God and his people. The judgment announced in v.1 ("I will show you the judgment of the great whore") is thus the judgment of this city-system, described more fully in chapter 18.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 11:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-8.htm); 16:19; 18:10, 16, 18–21; 21:2, 9–10; cf. 17:3, 9, 15.

## Retrospect on Revelation 17 (Synthesis)

Revelation 17 elaborates the sixth-bowl motif of the drying Euphrates (16:12), the withdrawal of Babylon's political support. Its architecture divides into two panels: **vv.1–6** identify Babylon as the end-time apostate religious system through the symbol of the great prostitute — seductive, royally attired, blood-drunk, globally dominant; **vv.7–18** decode the beast as a tripartite power passing through three historical phases (*was / is not / will come*) that parody the divine eternity formula (*was / is / is coming*). The seven heads = seven mountains = seven successive empires through which Satan has worked to oppress God's people (Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Persia, Greece, Rome, medieval ecclesiastical power); the eighth = the revived seventh, the beast of the final crisis. The ten horns are purely eschatological end-time political powers that first support and then destroy Babylon — unwittingly fulfilling God's sovereign will. The chapter's theological architecture is anti-type: Babylon is the point-by-point counterfeit of the New Jerusalem — same angel guide, same "come I will show you," same woman-city metaphor, same jeweled adornment, but inverted moral content and opposite fate. Where New Jerusalem offers life by God's grace, Babylon offers death through satanic deception. The final battle (v.14) belongs to the Lamb, who is Lord of lords and King of kings — and the faithful, called, and chosen saints who stand with him.
