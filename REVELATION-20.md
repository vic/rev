## Revelation 20

**Chapter overview**: Revelation 20 completes the "judgment circle" begun in chapter 17. It moves through three acts: (1) Satan bound on the desolate earth for a thousand years (vv. 1–3); (2) the first resurrection and millennial reign of the saints in heaven (vv. 4–6); (3) Satan's release, the final assault of Gog and Magog, divine fire, and the great white throne judgment (vv. 7–15). The chapter answers the final question left by chapter 19: what is the fate of Satan himself?

---

### [Rev 20:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-1.htm) — "And I saw an angel come down from heaven"

**Text**: "And I saw an angel come down from heaven, having the key of the bottomless pit and a great chain in his hand."

**Exegesis**: The vision follows immediately upon the destruction of the beast and false prophet in chapter 19. John sees an unidentified angel descending from heaven — a deliberate contrast with the "star fallen from heaven" in [Revelation 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm) (Satan himself), who was given the key of the abyss to *open* it and unleash demonic forces. Here the angel comes with the key to *lock* the abyss. Some interpreters have equated this angel with the 9:1 figure, but the differences are crucial: this angel descends from heaven (divine commission), and his purpose is confinement rather than release. The key and chain are figurative instruments — the angel comes clothed in the full power and authority of God. The Greek *abyssos* (translated "bottomless pit" in KJV) is the dark, subterranean prison of chaotic forces; its background is [Genesis 1:2](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-2.htm) (the *tehom*, the formless deep) and the intertestamental conception of a prison for fallen angelic powers (cf. [Isa. 24:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/24-21.htm)–22; [2 Pet. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-4.htm); [Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm)). In Revelation it is the source from which the beast arises (11:7; 17:8) and from which demonic locust-forces emerge (9:2–3).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm); 11:7; 17:8; [Gen. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-2.htm); [Isa. 24:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/24-21.htm)–22; [Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm); [2 Pet. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-4.htm).

### [Rev 20:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-2.htm) — "And he laid hold on the dragon, that old serpent"

**Text**: "And he laid hold on the dragon, that old serpent, which is the Devil, and Satan, and bound him a thousand years,"

**Exegesis**: The fourfold identification — dragon, ancient serpent, Devil, Satan — is identical to the characterization in [Revelation 12:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-9.htm) where Satan was cast from heaven to earth. In chapter 12 he is thrown down; here he is bound. The same adversary who has prosecuted the cosmic conflict throughout Revelation's second half now is seized and chained. The terms are cumulative: *dragon* evokes the chaos monster of Near Eastern mythology (Leviathan, Rahab) used in the OT for cosmic enemies of God ([Isa. 27:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/27-1.htm); [Ezek. 29:3](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/29-3.htm)); *ancient serpent* (*ophis ho archaios*) traces the identity back to Eden (Gen. 3); *Devil* (Gk. *diabolos*, "slanderer/accuser") identifies him by function; *Satan* (Heb. *śāṭān*, "adversary") is his personal name. The binding for "a thousand years" is the chapter's central chronological marker. Stefanovic notes three views: (1) **Postmillennialism** — Christ returns after 1,000 years of increasing Christianization; effectively died with World War I. (2) **Amillennialism** — the millennium is the current church age; Satan's binding occurred at the cross ([Matt. 12:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/12-29.htm); [John 12:31](https://biblehub.com/john/12-31.htm)); his activity is limited but not ended. The Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, and many Reformed churches hold this view. (3) **Premillennialism** — the Second Coming precedes the millennium. The Adventist reading (premillennial) holds that Revelation 15–20 follows a strict chronological sequence (close of intercession → destruction of Babylon → destruction of beast/false prophet → binding of Satan) and that the abyss in 20:1–3 denotes the desolated, depopulated earth after the Second Coming. Whether the thousand years is literal or symbolic, the text insists it is "a real period of time."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 12:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-9.htm); [Gen. 3:1](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-1.htm); [Isa. 27:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/27-1.htm); [Matt. 12:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/12-29.htm); [John 12:31](https://biblehub.com/john/12-31.htm)–32.

### [Rev 20:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-3.htm) — "And cast him into the bottomless pit, and shut him up"

**Text**: "And cast him into the bottomless pit, and shut him up, and set a seal upon him, that he should deceive the nations no more, till the thousand years should be fulfilled: and after that he must be loosed a little season."

**Exegesis**: Three escalating actions secure Satan's imprisonment: cast in (*ebalen*), locked (*ekleisen*), sealed (*esphragisen*). The sealing recalls [Matthew 27:66](https://biblehub.com/matthew/27-66.htm) where the tomb of Jesus was sealed — an attempt to prevent escape. The stated purpose of the confinement is not punitive suffering but rather the cessation of deception: "that he should deceive the nations no more." Throughout the second half of Revelation, Satan is identified as the deceiver behind all the lying powers — the beast (13:11–17), Babylon (18:2–3), the false prophet (19:20). He is "the deceiver of the whole world" (12:9). But with the Second Coming, the wicked are destroyed ([2 Thess. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/1-6.htm)–10) and the righteous are translated to heaven ([1 Thess. 4:15](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-15.htm)–17). No living human remains on earth. Satan is thus bound not by a literal chain but by a chain of circumstances — he has no one to deceive. The abyss in this context, as Stefanovic explains, represents the earth itself in a condition of desolation and chaos, resembling the pre-creation state of [Genesis 1:2](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-2.htm) and the devastated Palestine described in [Jeremiah 4:23](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-23.htm)–26: "I looked on the earth, and behold, it was formless and void." The seven last plagues have produced precisely this desolation. Satan is left, as Richard Rice states, "to contemplate the bleak consequences of his rebellion against God." The "little season" (*mikron chronon*) at the end signals that his release is temporary and purposeful — it will precipitate the final judgment. The Greek *dei* ("must be loosed") signals divine necessity: God controls the final sequence.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 12:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-9.htm); 12:12, 17; 13:11–17; 16:13; 18:2–3; 19:20; [Gen. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-2.htm); [Jer. 4:23](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/4-23.htm)–26; [Isa. 24:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/24-21.htm)–22; [Matt. 27:66](https://biblehub.com/matthew/27-66.htm); [2 Thess. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/1-6.htm)–10; [1 Thess. 4:15](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-15.htm)–17.

### [Rev 20:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-4.htm) — "And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them"

**Text**: "And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and judgment was given unto them: and I saw the souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus, and for the word of God, and which had not worshipped the beast, neither his image, neither had received his mark upon their foreheads, or in their hands; and they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years."

**Exegesis**: The scene shifts from earth to heaven. John sees thrones — the same term used throughout Revelation almost exclusively for heavenly thrones; Leon Morris notes that "throne" appears 47 times in Revelation, and except for Satan's throne (2:13) and the beast's throne (13:2; 16:10), all are in heaven, making the location of the millennial reign celestial rather than terrestrial. "Judgment was given to them" fulfills Jesus' promise in [Matthew 19:28](https://biblehub.com/matthew/19-28.htm) that his disciples would "sit upon twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel," and Paul's declaration in [1 Corinthians 6:2](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/6-2.htm) that "the saints will judge the world." Two overlapping groups are described: (a) "souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus and for the word of God" — a direct allusion to [Revelation 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm), where these very souls cry from under the altar for vindication; they were told in 6:11 to "rest a little longer." Their rest (14:13) is now ended; they come to life. (b) Those "which had not worshipped the beast, neither his image, neither had received his mark" — the end-time faithful of [Revelation 12:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-17.htm)–13:18 who endured the great persecution. The Greek *ezesan* ("they lived," aorist of *zaō*) is an ingressive aorist meaning "they came to life" — a bodily resurrection. Together with the living saints translated at the Second Coming ([1 Thess. 4:15](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-15.htm)–17), they form the millennial assembly reigning with Christ. The phrase "the testimony of Jesus and the word of God" appears three times in Revelation (1:2, 9; 20:4), connecting the martyred witnesses to the prophetic tradition of the whole book.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-2.htm), 9; 2:13; 6:9–11; 12:17; 13:2, 15–17; 14:13; [Matt. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/matthew/19-28.htm); [1 Cor. 6:2](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/6-2.htm); [1 Thess. 4:15](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-15.htm)–17.

### [Rev 20:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-5.htm) — "But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished"

**Text**: "But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished. This is the first resurrection."

**Exegesis**: This parenthetical statement ("the rest of the dead lived not again") explicitly divides humanity into two resurrection groups separated by a thousand years. "The rest of the dead" are the unrighteous — destroyed at the Second Coming by the glory of Christ's appearance ([2 Thess. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/1-9.htm); cf. [Rev. 19:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-21.htm)) — who remain in death until the close of the millennium, when they are raised for the great white throne judgment (vv. 11–15). The clause "This is the first resurrection" retrospectively identifies the coming to life of the saints in verse 4 as the *first* resurrection — by implication establishing a *second* resurrection at the millennium's end. The numbering is emphatic and apologetic: it guards against amillennialist re-spiritualizing. The Greek *ezesan* in v.5 (same verb as in v.4) used for "the rest of the dead" who do *not* come to life confirms that "came to life" in v.4 refers to bodily resurrection, not spiritual rebirth — for if it were merely spiritual regeneration, there would be no contrast, since the wicked do not receive spiritual regeneration at all. The same verb *ezesan* is used of Christ in 2:8 ("who was dead and came to life"), confirming the bodily sense.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 2:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-8.htm); 19:21; 20:12–13; [2 Thess. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/1-9.htm); [1 Thess. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-16.htm); [John 5:28](https://biblehub.com/john/5-28.htm)–29; [Dan. 12:2](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-2.htm).

### [Rev 20:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-6.htm) — "Blessed and holy is he that hath part in the first resurrection"

**Text**: "Blessed and holy is he that hath part in the first resurrection: on such the second death hath no power, but they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with him a thousand years."

**Exegesis**: This is the fifth of seven beatitudes in Revelation (1:3; 14:13; 16:15; 19:9; 20:6; 22:7, 14), and it occupies the "B'" position in the chiastic structure of the beatitudes — corresponding to the "B" beatitude at 14:13 ("Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord"), creating a death/life parallel. The two adjectives *makarios* ("blessed") and *hagios* ("holy") together describe the eschatological status of the resurrected saints. They are blessed because immune to the "second death" — the lake of fire (v. 14). This directly fulfills the promise to the church at Smyrna: "he that overcometh shall not be hurt of the second death" ([Rev. 2:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-11.htm)). The second death is George Ladd's "eternal death," the final and irreversible extinction of the wicked, not eternal torment in the traditional sense (see v. 14 exegesis). The dual role of the saints as "priests of God and of Christ" recalls the OT high-priestly inscription "Holy to the LORD" ([Exod. 28:36](https://biblehub.com/exodus/28-36.htm)) and the book's inaugural declaration that Christ "hath made us kings and priests" ([Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); cf. 5:10). Their priestly role during the millennium suggests they are in the immediate presence of God. Their judicial role fulfills the promise to the Laodicean overcomers to sit with Christ on his throne ([Rev. 3:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-21.htm)). The suggestion from Stefanovic (following Joel Badina and C. Mervyn Maxwell) is that this millennial reign involves examining God's records — satisfying every question about the fairness of God's decisions regarding the lost — so that eternity begins with every created being fully persuaded of divine justice.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-3.htm), 6; 2:11; 3:21; 5:10; 14:13; [Exod. 28:36](https://biblehub.com/exodus/28-36.htm); [Matt. 19:28](https://biblehub.com/matthew/19-28.htm); [1 Cor. 6:2](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/6-2.htm); 13:12.

### [Rev 20:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-7.htm) — "And when the thousand years are expired, Satan shall be loosed out of his prison"

**Text**: "And when the thousand years are expired, Satan shall be loosed out of his prison,"

**Exegesis**: The Greek *elythe* ("was loosed," aorist passive) is the precise reversal of the binding action in verses 2–3. "Prison" (*phylakē*) is the word used for the abyss; the same word appears in 18:2 for Babylon as a "hold of every foul spirit." The loosing of Satan is caused by the second resurrection (v. 5): as Stefanovic cites from the SDA Bible Commentary, "It was the depopulation of the earth that terminated his deceptive work. His loosing will therefore be accomplished by a repopulation of the earth, an event brought about by the resurrection of the wicked at the close of the thousand years." The release parallels [Revelation 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm) (the opening of the abyss releasing demonic forces), but here the scale is cosmic and final. The loosing serves God's purpose: it provides Satan one final occasion to demonstrate the immutable character of those who have permanently chosen rebellion — they emerge from death unchanged, still susceptible to deception, still hostile to God.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm)–2; 18:2; 20:2–3, 5.

### [Rev 20:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-8.htm) — "And shall go out to deceive the nations which are in the four quarters of the earth, Gog, and Magog"

**Text**: "And shall go out to deceive the nations which are in the four quarters of the earth, Gog, and Magog, to gather them together to battle: the number of whom is as the sand of the sea."

**Exegesis**: Satan's activity after release is immediately defined as deception — his characteristic identity (12:9; 20:3, 10). He goes to "the four quarters of the earth" — a universal gathering, all four compass points representing totality (cf. [Rev. 7:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-1.htm)). The identification with "Gog and Magog" is drawn from Ezekiel 38–39, where Gog from the land of Magog (38:2), prince of Rosh, Meshech, and Tubal, leads an invasion of restored Israel from the north and is catastrophically defeated by God. In Jewish apocalyptic tradition, Gog and Magog became symbolic for the eschatological coalition of nations hostile to God and his Messiah (cf. the Babylonian Talmud, Berakoth 7b, 10a, 13a; Sanhedrin 17a; Ps. 2's vision of the nations raging against the LORD and his Anointed). Revelation does not use them as geopolitical entities but as symbolic representatives of all who have permanently aligned with rebellion against God. Their multitude being "as the sand of the sea" is a parody of God's promise to Abraham that his seed would be "as the sand which is on the seashore" ([Gen. 22:17](https://biblehub.com/genesis/22-17.htm)) — the wicked ironically mirroring the redeemed in sheer scale, yet destined for destruction rather than blessing.

**Cross-references**: Ezek. 38–39; [Ps. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-1.htm)–3; [Gen. 22:17](https://biblehub.com/genesis/22-17.htm); [Rev. 7:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-1.htm); 12:9; 20:3, 10; [Isa. 24:21](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/24-21.htm)–22.

### [Rev 20:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-9.htm) — "And they went up on the breadth of the earth, and compassed the camp of the saints about"

**Text**: "And they went up on the breadth of the earth, and compassed the camp of the saints about, and the beloved city: and fire came down from God out of heaven, and devoured them."

**Exegesis**: The massed forces of Satan "go up" (*anebēsan*) — the language of military assault. "The breadth of the earth" (*to platos tēs gēs*) denotes the vast geographic spread of the encirclement. "The camp of the saints" (*tēn parembolēn tōn hagiōn*) uses military camp language, evoking Israel's wilderness encampment around the tabernacle; "the beloved city" is the New Jerusalem, which by this time has descended from heaven to earth (cf. [Rev. 21:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-2.htm); 3:12). The attack against the holy city is Satan's ultimate attempt to dethrone God and seize dominion — a replay of every previous Satanic assault throughout the cosmic conflict, now compressed into a single final act. No battle occurs. Divine intervention is immediate and total: "fire came down from God out of heaven." The passive construction (*katebē*) emphasizes divine agency. This is a direct allusion to [Ezekiel 38:22](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-22.htm)–23: "I shall rain on him, and on his troops, and on the many peoples who are with him, a torrential rain, with hailstones, fire, and brimstone. And I shall magnify Myself, sanctify Myself, and make Myself known in the sight of many nations." The consuming fire is identified with God's holy indignation against sin ([Deut. 4:24](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/4-24.htm); [Heb. 12:29](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-29.htm)). A more detailed account of this destruction follows in verses 11–15.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 3:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-12.htm); 21:2; [Ezek. 38:22](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-22.htm)–23; 39:6; [Deut. 4:24](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/4-24.htm); [Heb. 12:29](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-29.htm); [2 Kings 1:10](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/1-10.htm) (Elijah and fire from heaven); [Ps. 2:5](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-5.htm), 9.

### [Rev 20:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-10.htm) — "And the devil that deceived them was cast into the lake of fire and brimstone"

**Text**: "And the devil that deceived them was cast into the lake of fire and brimstone, where the beast and the false prophet are, and shall be tormented day and night for ever and ever."

**Exegesis**: Satan's final disposition mirrors the fate of the beast and false prophet (19:20): the lake of fire and brimstone (*limnan tou pyros kai theiou*). Satan, cast down from heaven in chapter 12 and confined to earth in chapter 20, now meets his ultimate end. The language of "tormented day and night for ever and ever" (*basanisthēsontai hēmeras kai nyktos eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn*) echoes [Revelation 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm)–11 ("the smoke of their torment ascends forever and ever"). The phrase must be understood against its OT background: Sodom and Gomorrah experienced "eternal fire" (Jude 7), yet no fire burns there today; Edom was prophesied to burn with smoke rising "forever" ([Isa. 34:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-8.htm)–10), yet Edom is a silent ruin. As Desmond Ford states, "the purpose of fire is to consume, never to preserve." The phrase "for ever and ever" in biblical usage denotes that the action continues until God's purpose is complete — here, the total and irreversible destruction of evil. Stefanovic concludes: "The punishment of Satan is irreversible; all those who follow him must share his destiny." This also fulfills the prophecy of [Ezekiel 39:6](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/39-6.htm) against Gog. God "does not destroy Satan at the Second Coming but gives him one thousand years to contemplate the results of his rebellion" (Badina); now that contemplation ends in the lake of fire. [Matthew 25:41](https://biblehub.com/matthew/25-41.htm) is the background: "Depart from me, ye cursed, into everlasting fire, prepared for the devil and his angels."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm)–11; 19:20; [Isa. 34:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/34-8.htm)–10; Jude 7; [Gen. 19:24](https://biblehub.com/genesis/19-24.htm)–28; [Matt. 25:41](https://biblehub.com/matthew/25-41.htm); [Ezek. 39:6](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/39-6.htm); [Mal. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-1.htm).

### [Rev 20:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-11.htm) — "And I saw a great white throne, and him that sat on it"

**Text**: "And I saw a great white throne, and him that sat on it, from whose face the earth and the heaven fled away; and there was found no place for them."

**Exegesis**: The destruction of Satan initiates the final judgment. John sees "a great white throne" (*thronon megan leukon*). The three adjectives are theologically loaded: *great* signals supreme authority and cosmic scale; *white* connotes absolute purity, righteousness, and impartiality — the throne of grace ([Heb. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/4-16.htm)) is now the throne of judgment. The One seated on the throne is God himself, identified by his indescribable majesty rather than by name — consistent with how John refers to God throughout Revelation. The throne is also the throne of judgment in [Daniel 7:9](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-9.htm)–14, where the Ancient of Days is seated and books are opened. This is the only passage in the entire Bible where God is explicitly portrayed as the direct Judge in the final scene, without the mediating imagery used elsewhere. "From whose face the earth and the heaven fled away" (*ephugen hē gē kai ho ouranos*) is the dissolution of the present cosmic order — evoking [Isaiah 51:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/51-6.htm) ("The sky will vanish like smoke, and the earth will wear out like a garment"), [2 Peter 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm)–12 (the elements dissolving with fervent heat), and [Revelation 6:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-14.htm) (the sky receding like a scroll at the sixth seal). This cosmic dissolution makes way for the new creation (21:1). "There was found no place for them" — the universe's self-evacuation before divine holiness leaves no refuge.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-9.htm)–14; [Isa. 51:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/51-6.htm); [Ps. 96:13](https://biblehub.com/psalms/96-13.htm); 98:9; [Rev. 6:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-14.htm); [2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm)–12; [Heb. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/4-16.htm).

### [Rev 20:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-12.htm) — "And I saw the dead, small and great, stand before God"

**Text**: "And I saw the dead, small and great, stand before God; and the books were opened: and another book was opened, which is the book of life: and the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works."

**Exegesis**: The resurrected wicked — "the rest of the dead" of verse 5 — now stand before the throne. "Small and great" (*tous mikrous kai tous megalous*) indicates every socioeconomic, political, and social category without exception; none is too powerful to escape judgment, none too insignificant to receive justice. The scene has a strong parallel in [Daniel 7:10](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-10.htm) (the court convened, books opened) and the Jewish apocalyptic 4 [Ezra 7:32](https://biblehub.com/ezra/7-32.htm)–35 ("the Most High shall be revealed upon the seat of judgment, and compassion shall pass away, and patience shall be withdrawn; but judgment alone shall remain"). Two categories of books are opened: (1) **The books of records** (*ta biblia*) — ledgers of human deeds that reveal whether a person has been loyal to God or to Satan. These records determine the basis of judgment. (2) **The book of life** (*to biblion tēs zōēs*) — the eschatological register of the redeemed (cf. [Rev. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-5.htm); 13:8; 17:8; 21:27; [Phil. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-3.htm); [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm)). Only those found in the book of life inhabit the new earth (21:27); those absent are cast into the lake of fire (v. 15). The judgment standard is "according to their works" — this does not contradict salvation by grace, since the works recorded are evidence of the loyalty of the heart, expressing either faith or unbelief (Alan Johnson). Paul's statement in [Romans 2:6](https://biblehub.com/romans/2-6.htm) ("God will render to every man according to his deeds") and [1 Peter 1:17](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/1-17.htm) provide the theological backbone. The judgment is fully transparent: "no sentence executed on any wicked will be arbitrary, biased, or unfair" (SDA Bible Commentary).

### [Rev 20:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-13.htm) — "And the sea gave up the dead which were in it; and death and hell delivered up the dead which were in them"

**Text**: "And the sea gave up the dead which were in it; and death and hell delivered up the dead which were in them: and they were judged every man according to their works."

**Exegesis**: The universality of the second resurrection is emphasized by three domains of the dead: the *sea*, *death* (*thanatos*), and *hell* (*hadēs*). The threefold enumeration ensures that no burial circumstance, no manner of death, and no geographic location exempts anyone from this resurrection and judgment. The sea in the ancient world was the domain of chaos, death, and the unknown — those lost at sea, drowned, devoured, were popularly thought unreachable by any future hope. The text refutes this: even the sea yields its dead. *Hades* in Greek thought (equivalent to the Hebrew *Sheol*) is the realm of the dead, the grave. John has already personified Death and Hades as twin enemies of humanity (1:18; 6:8); they appear as the rider on the pale horse in the fourth seal. Now they must give up their contents. The repetition "according to his works" from v. 12 reinforces the individual accountability principle — each person is judged on their own record, not en masse. This is the fulfillment of [Revelation 11:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-18.htm): "the time has come... to judge the dead." The parallel in 4 [Ezra 7:32](https://biblehub.com/ezra/7-32.htm) ("the earth shall give up those who are asleep in it; and the chambers shall give up the souls") provides the closest Jewish textual parallel.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-18.htm); 6:8; 11:18; [Dan. 12:2](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-2.htm); [John 5:28](https://biblehub.com/john/5-28.htm)–29; [Acts 24:15](https://biblehub.com/acts/24-15.htm); 4 [Ezra 7:32](https://biblehub.com/ezra/7-32.htm); [Isa. 26:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/26-19.htm).

### [Rev 20:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-14.htm) — "And death and hell were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death."

**Text**: "And death and hell were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death."

**Exegesis**: This verse delivers the most dramatic statement of the entire chapter. Death and Hades — personified throughout Revelation as the two supreme enemies of humanity — are themselves judged and destroyed. The lake of fire receives not only persons but the very *concept* and *power* of death. Stefanovic observes that this proves the lake of fire cannot be a literal burning hell: "The concept of the lake of fire must be understood figuratively." Death and Hades cannot be literally set on fire. They are abstract powers; their destruction is the total abolition of the conditions that produced death. This is the fulfillment of Paul's eschatological hope in [1 Corinthians 15:26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-26.htm): "The last enemy that will be abolished is death." The redeemed cannot experience eternal life fully "until death itself is banished from the universe" (Ladd). The "second death" is defined explicitly: it is the lake of fire. The "first death" is biological death — the common end of mortal human existence. The second death is the final, irreversible, total extinction that follows the second resurrection and judgment. It stands in contrast to the "second resurrection" (the resurrection of the wicked). Crucially, the second death has no power over those in the first resurrection (v. 6) — they have already passed through death once and risen; the second death cannot touch them (cf. [Rev. 2:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-11.htm)). The word "death" in Greek carries the definite article (*ho thanatos*) throughout, treating it as a specific, named, personal enemy — and that enemy is now terminated.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-18.htm); 2:11; 6:8; 20:6; 21:4, 8; [1 Cor. 15:26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-26.htm), 54–55; [Isa. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/25-8.htm); [Hos. 13:14](https://biblehub.com/hosea/13-14.htm); [Mal. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-1.htm).

### [Rev 20:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-15.htm) — "And whosoever was not found written in the book of life was cast into the lake of fire."

**Text**: "And whosoever was not found written in the book of life was cast into the lake of fire."

**Exegesis**: The chapter's final verse is stark and decisive. The criterion for the ultimate destination is singular: presence or absence in the book of life (*to biblion tēs zōēs*). This book first appeared in [Revelation 3:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-5.htm) (promise that overcomers' names will not be blotted out), 13:8 (those whose names are not in it worship the beast), 17:8 (those not written in it marvel at the beast), and 21:27 (only those written in it enter the new Jerusalem). The book of life is not a register of moral achievement — it is the register of those who have believed in Christ, identified with his testimony, and persevered (cf. [Phil. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-3.htm); [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm)). Stefanovic explains: "Only those whose names are found in the book of life shall live in God's kingdom on the new earth (21:27); those whose names are not in the book of life will be cast into the lake of fire." The lake of fire, established as "the second death" in v. 14, is metaphorical for complete and final destruction: "the tormenting flames of the lake of fire destroy them completely, 'so that it will leave them neither root nor branch' ([Mal. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-1.htm)) — 'Satan the root, his followers the branches'" (White, *The Great Controversy*, 673). The passive voice (*eblēthē*, "was cast") indicates divine agency. The verse closes Revelation 20's judgment circle, first opened in chapter 17. An eternity without sin is ready to begin.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-5.htm); 13:8; 17:8; 20:12; 21:8, 27; [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm); [Phil. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-3.htm); [Mal. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-1.htm); [Matt. 25:41](https://biblehub.com/matthew/25-41.htm), 46.

**Retrospect on the chapter's structure and theology**:

Revelation 20 concludes the judgment circle that began in chapter 17. The chapter's three movements form a coherent eschatological sequence in Adventist premillennial reading: (1) Vv. 1–3: desolated earth becomes Satan's prison; his confinement is secured by divine power. (2) Vv. 4–6: the millennial reign in heaven — saints as priests and judges, examining God's ways, with full transparency of the cosmic controversy. The millennium serves both justice and theodicy: every question about God's dealings with the lost is answered before eternity begins ([1 Cor. 13:12](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/13-12.htm)). (3) Vv. 7–15: second resurrection brings Satan fresh opportunity to demonstrate the permanence of wicked rebellion; the assault on the beloved city is crushed by divine fire; the great white throne reveals the total, universal, transparent judgment; Death and Hades are abolished; the lake of fire accomplishes complete extinction. The "mystery of God" announced in [Revelation 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm) — the sealed scroll of Revelation 5 containing the full record of the cosmic controversy — is finally unsealed and its contents disclosed ([Rev. 20:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-11.htm)–15), fulfilling [Revelation 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm) and 11:15–18. "An eternity without sin is now ready to begin."
