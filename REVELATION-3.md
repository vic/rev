## Revelation 3

---

### [Rev 3:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-1.htm) — "And unto the angel of the church in Sardis write"

**Text**: "And unto the angel of the church in Sardis write; These things saith he that hath the seven Spirits of God, and the seven stars; I know thy works, that thou hast a name that thou livest, and art dead."

**Exegesis**: Sardis was located thirty miles southeast of Thyatira, on a steep volcanic hill so defensively formidable it was considered a natural citadel. Once the capital of the Lydian kingdom under the fabulously wealthy Croesus, by the first century its glory was a memory rather than a reality. W. M. Ramsay noted "no city of Asia at that time showed such a melancholy contrast between past splendor and present decay as Sardis." The city was the center of the wool and dyeing industries; its patron deity was Cybele (equated with Artemis), whose temple hosted eunuch priests and who was believed to have special power to restore the dead to life — an ironic backdrop for a church Christ pronounces dead.

The self-introduction draws from the vision of [Rev 1:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-4.htm), 16, 20. The **seven Spirits of God** denotes the fullness of the Holy Spirit (cf. [Isa. 11:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/11-2.htm); [Rev. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-5.htm); 5:6) — Christ comes to the spiritually dying Sardians bearing the full reviving power of the Spirit. The **seven stars** are the angels/leaders of the churches ([Rev. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-20.htm)); Christ holds their destiny in his hand. The description is deliberately matched to Sardis's condition: a dead church needs the life-giving Spirit.

The verdict is unsparing: **no commendation is offered**, only rebuke. The church has a *name* (Greek *onoma*) — a public reputation, a report — for being alive and active. In reality it is dead. The NT consistently uses death as a metaphor for sin and spiritual alienation: "dead through trespasses and sins" ([Eph. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-1.htm)); "she who gives herself to wanton pleasure is dead even while she lives" ([1 Tim. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/5-6.htm)); those who "hold to a form of godliness, although they have denied its power" ([2 Tim. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/3-5.htm)). The Sardians had absorbed the lethargic character of the city. No specific heresy or immorality is named — the failure is existential: lifeless Christianity, faith without the animating Spirit. Their works have not been "fulfilled" (Greek *peplērōmena*, perfected, completed) before God — they do not measure up to God's standard.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-4.htm), 16, 20; [Eph. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-1.htm); [1 Tim. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/5-6.htm); [2 Tim. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/3-5.htm); [Isa. 11:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/11-2.htm).

### [Rev 3:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-2.htm) — "Be watchful, and strengthen the things which remain"

**Text**: "Be watchful, and strengthen the things which remain, that are ready to die: for I have not found thy works perfect before God."

**Exegesis**: The imperative **"Be watchful"** (Greek *grēgoreuō*, present imperative = continuous action) carries pointed historical irony. Sardis was built on a nearly vertical cliff face, a site so defensively strong the citizens grew complacent. The city was captured by Cyrus the Persian in 549 B.C. and again by Antiochus in 218 B.C. — both times by night, both times because the guards had not been posted on the seemingly impregnable walls. Enemy troops scaled the precipice undetected. This carelessness had migrated from the city into the church.

The few things that **remain** (*ta loipa*, the remaining things) are not yet dead but are on the verge of death. Even the small remnant of faithful members (v. 4) who had not yet fully compromised were in danger of slipping into the surrounding lethargy. The church as a whole was in profound spiritual arrest.

"I have not found thy works perfect (*peplērōmena*) before God" — their works were incomplete, unfinished, not meeting the divine measure. This is not a condemnation of hypocrisy per se but of a gradual surrender to the ambient spiritual atmosphere of the city. They were Christians in name; the reality had hollowed out.

**Cross-references**: [Matt. 26:41](https://biblehub.com/matthew/26-41.htm); [Eph. 5:14](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/5-14.htm); [1 Thess. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-6.htm).

### [Rev 3:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-3.htm) — "Remember therefore how thou hast received and heard"

**Text**: "Remember therefore how thou hast received and heard, and hold fast, and repent. If therefore thou shalt not watch, I will come on thee as a thief, and thou shalt not know what hour I will come upon thee."

**Exegesis**: Three imperatives structure Christ's counsel to Sardis: **remember**, **hold fast**, **repent**.

**"Keep remembering"** (*mnēmoneue*, present imperative) does not suggest a one-time act of recall but an ongoing, active bearing-in-mind (the Greek does not connote mere recollection but sustained retention). The Sardians had received (*elabēs*, aorist — a definite past event) and heard the gospel. They have not entirely forgotten — but they are failing to keep it alive and operative. Remembering means keeping the past afresh and applying it to the present, not merely nostalgia.

**"Hold fast"** (*tērei*, keep it) — retain and observe what was received.

**"Repent"** (*metanoēson*, aorist imperative) — a decisive, once-for-all turning. The aorist suggests a radical break from the present condition, not a gradual improvement. Like the prodigal son ([Luke 15:17](https://biblehub.com/luke/15-17.htm)–19), the Sardians must bring to mind their previous standing and make a decisive turnaround.

The **warning**: if they do not watch, Christ will come **as a thief** — without warning, at an hour they cannot predict. This imagery appears in Christ's eschatological discourse ([Matt. 24:42](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-42.htm)–44; [Mark 13:35](https://biblehub.com/mark/13-35.htm)–37) and in Paul and Peter ([1 Thess. 5:2](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-2.htm); [2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm)) for the sudden, unexpected nature of the Second Coming. In the Sardis context, the thief-coming functions doubly: it recalls the historical night raids on their city while pointing to the eschatological arrival of Christ in judgment. Their lethargic failure to watch replicates in the spiritual sphere the civic failure that twice led to the city's destruction.

**Cross-references**: [Matt. 24:42](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-42.htm)–44; [Mark 13:35](https://biblehub.com/mark/13-35.htm)–37; [Luke 15:17](https://biblehub.com/luke/15-17.htm)–19; [1 Thess. 5:2](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-2.htm); [2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm); [Rev. 16:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-15.htm).

### [Rev 3:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-4.htm) — "Thou hast a few names even in Sardis"

**Text**: "Thou hast a few names even in Sardis which have not defiled their garments; and they shall walk with me in white: for they are worthy."

**Exegesis**: Amid the pervasive deadness, a remnant exists: **"a few names"** (*oligá onomata*) — individuals, not a group, identified by name (a Hebraism: names standing for persons, cf. [Num. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/numbers/1-2.htm); [Acts 1:15](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-15.htm)). These are not described as standing apart from the church in schism but as those who within the church have maintained faithfulness.

**"Defiled their garments"** (*emolunan ta himatia autōn*) most likely refers to compromise with the surrounding pagan environment — the pervasive pollution that afflicted all seven Asian churches in various forms. Garments in the ancient world were loaded with social and theological symbolism. Moral and ritual defilement rendered one unfit for divine presence; undefiled garments signify maintained purity and integrity.

The promise to the undefiled remnant: **"they shall walk with me in white"** (*peripatēsousin met' emou en leukois*). White garments throughout Revelation signify justification, salvation, and eschatological victory ([Rev. 6:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-11.htm); 7:9, 13–14; 19:8). This finds fulfillment in [Rev. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-9.htm)–17 and 19:7–8 where the redeemed stand before the throne clothed in white. "These are those coming out of the great tribulation, who have washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb" ([Rev. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-14.htm)). Worthiness (*axios*) here is not human merit but relational fidelity — those found worthy in the judgment are those who held fast to Christ.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 6:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-11.htm); 7:9, 13–14; 19:7–8; [Num. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/numbers/1-2.htm); [Acts 1:15](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-15.htm).

### [Rev 3:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-5.htm) — "He that overcometh, the same shall be clothed in white raiment"

**Text**: "He that overcometh, the same shall be clothed in white raiment; and I will not blot out his name out of the book of life, but I will confess his name before my Father, and before his angels."

**Exegesis**: The overcomer (*ho nikōn*, the one conquering — present participle, denoting ongoing conquest) in Sardis receives a **threefold promise**.

**First**: clothed in **white garments** — repeating and expanding the promise of v. 4 to the remnant. White garments are salvation-garments; in Roman culture, white robes were worn by citizens on the day of a military triumph. The image fuses political honor and divine justification. These are the "righteous deeds of the saints" ([Rev. 19:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-8.htm)), garments washed in the Lamb's blood ([Rev. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-14.htm)).

**Second**: Christ **will not blot out his name** from the **book of life** (*biblos tēs zōēs*). The book of life is the heavenly eschatological register of those saved by faith in Christ's atoning death — elsewhere called the Lamb's book of life ([Rev. 13:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-8.htm); 21:27). This concept appears throughout Scripture: Moses intercedes for Israel's names not to be blotted out ([Exod. 32:32](https://biblehub.com/exodus/32-32.htm)–33); [Ps. 69:28](https://biblehub.com/psalms/69-28.htm) speaks of the wicked being erased from the book; [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm) presents it as the eschatological register of the saved; [Phil. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-3.htm) and [Heb. 12:23](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-23.htm) affirm it in the NT. The promise is stated as a strong litotes (negated contrary): "I will absolutely not (*ou mē*, strongest Greek negation) erase his name." At the final judgment, only those written in the book enter the new earth ([Rev. 20:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-12.htm), 15; 21:27); those not found there are cast into the lake of fire ([Rev. 20:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-15.htm)).

**Third**: Christ **will confess his name** before the Father and his angels. This directly echoes Jesus' own teaching: "Everyone who confesses me before men, I will also confess before my Father in heaven; but whoever denies me before men, I will also deny before my Father" ([Matt. 10:32](https://biblehub.com/matthew/10-32.htm)–33; [Luke 12:8](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-8.htm)–9). The overcomer's name, once merely a *reputation* for being alive, is transformed — acknowledged by Christ in the heavenly court.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 32:32](https://biblehub.com/exodus/32-32.htm)–33; [Ps. 69:28](https://biblehub.com/psalms/69-28.htm); [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm); [Matt. 10:32](https://biblehub.com/matthew/10-32.htm)–33; [Luke 12:8](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-8.htm)–9; [Phil. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-3.htm); [Heb. 12:23](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-23.htm); [Rev. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-14.htm); 13:8; 19:8; 20:12, 15; 21:27.

### [Rev 3:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-6.htm) — "He that hath an ear, let him hear"

**Text**: "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches."

**Exegesis**: The standard closing formula of all seven messages (cf. [Rev. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-7.htm), 11, 17, 29; 3:13, 22), echoing Christ's call to attentive hearing in the Synoptic Gospels ([Matt. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/matthew/11-15.htm); 13:9, 43; [Mark 4:9](https://biblehub.com/mark/4-9.htm), 23). The singular ear of individual hearing is called to receive the plural witness to **the churches** — the message to Sardis is catholic in scope, a warning for any church or believer in any era who substitutes reputation for reality. Stefanovic notes that what happened to the Sardians can happen to every Christian regardless of place or time. Historically, this message is read as reflecting the Protestant scholasticism of the 16th–17th centuries: the vibrant Reformation generation's successors degenerating into formalism, doctrinal controversy, and spiritual lethargy — a church that *appeared* alive but had lost its animating Spirit.

**Cross-references**: [Matt. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/matthew/11-15.htm); 13:9, 43; [Mark 4:9](https://biblehub.com/mark/4-9.htm), 23; [Rev. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-7.htm), 11, 17, 29.

## Philadelphia (vv. 7–13)

---

### [Rev 3:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-7.htm) — "And to the angel of the church in Philadelphia write"

**Text**: "And to the angel of the church in Philadelphia write; These things saith he that is holy, he that is true, he that hath the key of David, he that openeth, and no man shutteth; and shutteth, and no man openeth;"

**Exegesis**: Philadelphia (modern Alaşehir) was the youngest of the seven cities, located twenty-five miles southeast of Sardis on a high volcanic plateau. Founded by Pergamenian king Attalus II (159–138 B.C.) — nicknamed *Philadelphus* ("lover of his brother") for his devotion to his brother Eumenes II — the city was deliberately designed as a **missionary outpost** to spread Greek language and culture into Lydia and Phrygia. It lay on the imperial post road, making it a natural gateway to the East. The severe earthquake of A.D. 17 devastated the city along with Sardis, creating a population skittish about remaining inside city walls — a detail possibly relevant to the "pillar" imagery of v. 12.

Christ's self-introduction employs three titles with no parallel in the chapter-1 composite vision:

**"He that is holy"** (*ho hagios*) — in the OT, "the Holy One" is a divine title par excellence: "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts" ([Isa. 6:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/6-3.htm)); "I am the LORD, your Holy One" ([Isa. 43:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/43-15.htm)); "Thus says the high and exalted One who lives forever, whose name is Holy" ([Isa. 57:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/57-15.htm)). In the NT it is also applied to Jesus ([Mark 1:24](https://biblehub.com/mark/1-24.htm); [John 6:69](https://biblehub.com/john/6-69.htm); [1 [John 2:20](https://biblehub.com/john/2-20.htm)](https://biblehub.com/1_john/2-20.htm)). Christ here asserts his full divine essence.

**"He that is true"** (*ho alēthinos*) — Greek *alēthinos* means "genuine" or "real," as opposed to that which is unreal or counterfeit (distinct from *alēthēs*, "true" as opposed to false). In the OT context, the word evokes God's faithfulness to his covenant promises (cf. [Ps. 146:6](https://biblehub.com/psalms/146-6.htm); [Isa. 65:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/65-16.htm), where God is the "God of truth/amen"). Jesus is the real God, not an idol; he is the genuine fulfillment of every divine promise.

**"The key of David"** — an explicit allusion to [Isa. 22:20](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/22-20.htm)–22. There, Eliakim son of Hilkiah, the faithful chief steward of King Hezekiah, receives "the key of the house of David on his shoulder; when he opens, no one will shut; when he shuts, no one will open." Eliakim was given full authority over the royal household and the king's storehouse. In Revelation, Christ is the antitype: he has received all authority ([Matt. 28:18](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-18.htm)), is appointed "head over all things to the church" ([Eph. 1:22](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/1-22.htm)), and is "faithful as a Son over His house" ([Heb. 3:6](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/3-6.htm)). He controls access to the heavenly storehouse from which he dispenses all promised blessings to the overcomers. What he opens — no one shuts. What he shuts — no one opens.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 6:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/6-3.htm); 22:20–22; 43:15; 57:15; 65:16; [Mark 1:24](https://biblehub.com/mark/1-24.htm); [John 6:69](https://biblehub.com/john/6-69.htm); [1 [John 2:20](https://biblehub.com/john/2-20.htm)](https://biblehub.com/1_john/2-20.htm); [Matt. 28:18](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-18.htm); [Eph. 1:22](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/1-22.htm); [Heb. 3:6](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/3-6.htm).

### [Rev 3:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-8.htm) — "I know thy works: behold, I have set before thee an open door"

**Text**: "I know thy works: behold, I have set before thee an open door, and no man can shut it: for thou hast a little strength, and hast kept my word, and hast not denied my name."

**Exegesis**: This church receives **no rebuke** — one of only two (with Smyrna) so treated. The assessment is entirely affirmative with one acknowledged limitation.

**"An open door"** (*thyran ēneōgmenēn*, perfect passive participle — a door having been permanently opened and remaining open) is a Pauline metaphor for missionary opportunity: "a wide door for effective service has opened to me" ([1 Cor. 16:9](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-9.htm); [2 Cor. 2:12](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/2-12.htm)); "that God may open up to us a door for the word, so that we may speak forth the mystery of Christ" ([Col. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/colossians/4-3.htm)); God "opened a door of faith to the Gentiles" ([Acts 14:27](https://biblehub.com/acts/14-27.htm)). Philadelphia's very raison d'être as a city was to be the gateway spreading Greek culture eastward — Christ appropriates that civic identity for the gospel: the church in this missionary city is given a missionary open door that no adversary can close.

**"A little strength"** (*mikrán dynamin*) — the Philadelphian church was small, numerically or financially weak, without social influence. Yet faithfulness does not require power; God's open doors are not contingent on human resources. When God opens a door, no opposition — not even the synagogue of Satan (v. 9) — can shut it.

**"Hast kept my word... hast not denied my name"** — a double affirmation: doctrinal fidelity and confessional faithfulness under pressure. No compromise, no apostasy. They are a church of *hypomonē* — endurance and patient steadfastness (v. 10).

**Cross-references**: [Acts 14:27](https://biblehub.com/acts/14-27.htm); [1 Cor. 16:9](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-9.htm); [2 Cor. 2:12](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/2-12.htm); [Col. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/colossians/4-3.htm); [Matt. 23:13](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-13.htm); [Luke 11:52](https://biblehub.com/luke/11-52.htm).

### [Rev 3:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-9.htm) — "Behold, I will make them of the synagogue of Satan"

**Text**: "Behold, I will make them of the synagogue of Satan, which say they are Jews, and are not, but do lie; behold, I will make them to come and worship before thy feet, and to know that I have loved thee."

**Exegesis**: Philadelphia faced the same Jewish opposition that afflicted Smyrna ([Rev. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-9.htm)). Those who claim to be Jews but are not constitute the **synagogue of Satan** — not because they are ethnically Jewish (that question is not at issue) but because they are doing Satan's work: shutting the door of the gospel (cf. [Matt. 23:13](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-13.htm); [Luke 11:52](https://biblehub.com/luke/11-52.htm)), slandering the church, opposing its mission. The claim to be the true people of God while acting as instruments of the adversary makes them liars.

The promise of reversal echoes the OT Zion-restoration texts. In Isaiah, the nations will come and bow before Israel: "The sons of those who afflicted you will come bowing to you, and all those who despised you will bow themselves at the soles of your feet" ([Isa. 49:23](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/49-23.htm); cf. 60:14). Christ applies this to the church in Philadelphia: those who are opposing the Philadelphians will be brought to acknowledge that Christ has loved (*ēgapēsa*, aorist — expressing definite, historical, covenant love) this church. The word *proskunēsousin* (worship/bow down) is striking — the very posture of divine worship adopted toward the faithful community, demonstrating public vindication.

The phrase "I have loved you" — notably, Philadelphians and Laodiceans are the only two churches explicitly told they are loved by Christ in these messages.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-9.htm); [Isa. 49:23](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/49-23.htm); 60:14; [Matt. 23:13](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-13.htm); [Luke 11:52](https://biblehub.com/luke/11-52.htm).

### [Rev 3:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-10.htm) — "Because thou hast kept the word of my patience"

**Text**: "Because thou hast kept the word of my patience, I also will keep thee from the hour of temptation, which shall come upon all the world, to try them that dwell upon the earth."

**Exegesis**: **"The word of my patience"** (*ton logon tēs hypomonēs mou*) — the genitive is most naturally read as "the word characterized by endurance" — the gospel message which calls for patient, persevering faithfulness (cf. [Rev. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-9.htm); 13:10; 14:12). The Philadelphians have kept this word; Christ responds in kind with keeping them.

**"The hour of trial"** (*tēs hōras tou peirasmou*) — a definite, eschatological event: "the hour of trial that is about to come upon all the world, to test *tous katoikountas epi tēs gēs*" (those who dwell upon the earth). This phrase (*hoi katoikountes epi tēs gēs*) is a technical term in Revelation for the wicked, the enemies of God ([Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm); 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 14:6; 17:8) — not for believers. The hour of trial is therefore God's eschatological judgment poured upon the wicked prior to the Second Coming, as portrayed in the bowl plagues of Rev. 16.

The crucial exegetical question: does Christ promise to keep them *from* the trial (removal before it) or *through* it (protection during it)? Stefanovic argues for the latter: Jesus prayed "I do not ask You to take them out of the world, but to keep them from the evil one" ([John 17:15](https://biblehub.com/john/17-15.htm)); in Revelation the last plagues fall on those with the mark of the beast, not on those sealed by God (Rev. 16); [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm) similarly promises that at the time of unprecedented distress, those found written in the book will be delivered — not removed. Christ promises to be with his people and protect them *during* the hour of trial, not to rapture them out of it.

**Cross-references**: [John 17:15](https://biblehub.com/john/17-15.htm); [Rev. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-9.htm); 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 14:6; 16; 17:8; [Dan. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-1.htm).

### [Rev 3:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-11.htm) — "Behold, I come quickly: hold that fast which thou hast"

**Text**: "Behold, I come quickly: hold that fast which thou hast, that no man take thy crown."

**Exegesis**: **"I come quickly"** (*erchomai tachy*) — Christ's imminent coming functions as both motivation and comfort. The adverb *tachy* conveys sudden swiftness rather than chronological nearness; when he comes, there will be no time for last-minute repositioning.

In view of this, the counsel: **"hold what you have"** (*kratei ho echeis*) — do not surrender the little strength, the kept word, the undenied name. The danger is not persecution but defection under pressure — losing one's *place* by being found unworthy.

**"That no one take your crown"** (*stephanos*, the victor's laurel crown of athletic contest — see [Rev. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-10.htm)) — Barclay lists biblical figures who lost their place to another because they were found unfit: Esau to Jacob ([Gen. 25:34](https://biblehub.com/genesis/25-34.htm); 27:36); Reuben to Judah ([Gen. 49:4](https://biblehub.com/genesis/49-4.htm), 8); Saul to David ([1 Sam. 16:1](https://biblehub.com/1_samuel/16-1.htm), 13); Judas to Matthias ([Acts 1:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-25.htm)); Israel to the Gentiles ([Rom. 11:11](https://biblehub.com/romans/11-11.htm)). When God grants a task or calling and the recipient abandons it, the task passes to another. The crown is not taken by force — it is forfeited through unfaithfulness.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 2:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-10.htm); [Gen. 25:34](https://biblehub.com/genesis/25-34.htm); 27:36; 49:4, 8; [1 Sam. 16:1](https://biblehub.com/1_samuel/16-1.htm), 13; [Acts 1:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-25.htm); [Rom. 11:11](https://biblehub.com/romans/11-11.htm).

### [Rev 3:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-12.htm) — "Him that overcometh will I make a pillar in the temple of my God"

**Text**: "Him that overcometh will I make a pillar in the temple of my God, and he shall go no more out: and I will write upon him the name of my God, and the name of the city of my God, which is new Jerusalem, which cometh down out of heaven from my God: and I will write upon him my new name."

**Exegesis**: The overcomer in Philadelphia receives three inscriptions and one architectural metaphor — the most elaborate promise in any of the seven messages.

**"A pillar in the temple"** (*stylon en tō naō*) — a pillar is fixed, immovable, load-bearing. It conveys stability and permanence. Paul calls the church "the pillar and support of the truth" ([1 Tim. 3:15](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/3-15.htm)) and names Peter, James, and John as "pillars" ([Gal. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/galatians/2-9.htm)). For earthquake-scarred Philadelphia, whose citizens had repeatedly fled the city's trembling walls for the open country, the image of permanent, immovable security in God's sanctuary would have been intensely meaningful. The overcomer will **never go out** (*exō ou mē exelthē eti*, strong negation + "no longer") — eternal security, the antithesis of earthquake-driven exile.

The **threefold inscription** (*grapsō ep' auton*):
1. **"The name of my God"** — belonging to God, ownership, identity, and protection. In [Rev. 22:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-4.htm), "his name will be on their foreheads." The 144,000 bear the Father's name ([Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm)).
2. **"The name of the city of my God, the new Jerusalem, which cometh down out of heaven"** — citizenship in the eschatological city ([Rev. 21:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-2.htm), 10). Ezekiel gave the new city its name: "The LORD is there" (*YHWH shammah*, [Ezek. 48:35](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-35.htm)), signifying the eternal presence of God: "The tabernacle of God is among men, and he will tabernacle with them" ([Rev. 21:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-3.htm)).
3. **"My new name"** — Christ's new, eschatological name, a name not yet fully revealed, known only at the consummation (cf. [Rev. 19:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-12.htm)). To receive it is to be permanently identified with and under the authority of the risen Christ.

**Cross-references**: [Gal. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/galatians/2-9.htm); [1 Tim. 3:15](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/3-15.htm); [Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm); 21:2, 3, 10; 22:4; [Ezek. 48:35](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-35.htm); [Rev. 19:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-12.htm).

### [Rev 3:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-13.htm) — "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches"

**Text**: "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches."

**Exegesis**: The universal call to hearing closes the Philadelphia message as it does all seven. Even though God's people may have only *little strength*, this does not excuse inattentiveness. The Spirit's word to Philadelphia is a word to all churches: faithful weakness empowered by an open door is preferable to strong reputation masking spiritual death. Historically, Stefanovic reads Philadelphia as fitting the missionary era of the 18th–19th centuries — the Wesleyan revival, the Great Awakenings, the worldwide Protestant missionary movements (Carey, Livingstone, Hudson Taylor) — a period of remarkable gospel advance driven not by institutional power but by enduring, Word-faithful witness.

## Laodicea (vv. 14–22)

---

### [Rev 3:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-14.htm) — "And unto the angel of the church of the Laodiceans write"

**Text**: "And unto the angel of the church of the Laodiceans write; These things saith the Amen, the faithful and true witness, the beginning of the creation of God;"

**Exegesis**: Laodicea (modern Eski-hisar) lay forty-five miles southeast of Philadelphia and forty miles east of Ephesus. Its favorable position on Roman road networks made it one of the greatest commercial and financial centers of the ancient world. It was enormously wealthy: when destroyed by an earthquake around A.D. 60, the citizens refused imperial aid (Tacitus, *Annals* 14.27) and rebuilt entirely from their own resources. Its wealth derived from three industries — (1) **banking**: a major gold-reserve and financial center; (2) **black wool textile trade**: famous throughout the empire for soft, glossy black wool used in garments and carpets; (3) **medical school**: renowned for treatment of eye diseases using an eyesalve made from "Phrygian powder" mixed with oil. Each of these civic boasts is directly subverted by Christ's threefold counsel in v. 18.

Christ introduces himself with three titles:

**"The Amen"** (*ho Amēn*) — from Hebrew *'amen*, meaning "truly," affirming absolute truth and trustworthiness. In [Isa. 65:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/65-16.htm), God is called "the God of truth" (Heb. *'elohei 'amen*). In the Gospels, Jesus uniquely introduces his own sayings with "Truly, truly I say to you" (*amēn amēn legō hymin*, [John 1:51](https://biblehub.com/john/1-51.htm); 3:3, 5). Paul's use in [2 Cor. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/1-20.htm) is illuminating: "For as many as are the promises of God, in him they are Yes; therefore also through him is our Amen to the glory of God through us." Christ is the living Amen — the ultimate divine affirmation and guarantor of all promises.

**"The faithful and true witness"** (*ho martys ho pistos kai alēthinos*) — he is the witness whose testimony is completely reliable. To the self-deceived Laodicean church, this is crucial: Christ's diagnosis of their condition is the accurate one, not their self-assessment. His faithful testimony exposes their true state; therefore, when he speaks, the church must listen and obey.

**"The beginning of the creation of God"** (*hē archē tēs ktiseōs tou theou*) — Greek *archē* can mean "beginning [in time]," "origin," "source," or "ruler/first principle." Paul uses the same word of Christ in [Col. 1:18](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-18.htm) ("the firstborn from the dead, that in everything he might be preeminent") and affirms "all things were created by him and for him" ([Col. 1:16](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-16.htm); cf. [John 1:3](https://biblehub.com/john/1-3.htm)). Laodicea was close to Colossae, and Paul explicitly urged the Laodiceans to read his Colossian letter ([Col. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/colossians/4-16.htm)), making them familiar with this Christology. Stefanovic prefers "source" or "origin" and "ruler" over "first created being" — Christ is the originating source and sovereign ruler of all creation, with absolute authority over this self-sufficient city and church.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 65:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/65-16.htm); [John 1:3](https://biblehub.com/john/1-3.htm), 51; 3:3; [2 Cor. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/1-20.htm); [Col. 1:16](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-16.htm), 18; 4:16; [Rev. 19:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-11.htm).

### [Rev 3:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-15.htm) — "I know thy works, that thou art neither cold nor hot"

**Text**: "I know thy works, that thou art neither cold nor hot: I would thou wert cold or hot."

**Exegesis**: Christ's appraisal begins with a thermal diagnosis drawn directly from Laodicea's geography. The city had no natural water supply. About six miles north stood Hierapolis, with its bubbling hot mineral springs used medicinally. Laodicea piped this water through a six-mile aqueduct; by arrival, it was lukewarm — distasteful to drink, useless for healing.

Greek *psychros* ("cold") can mean "icy cold" (Sirach 43:20 uses it of water frozen by a north wind to ice). Greek *zestos* ("hot") means "boiling hot"; the verb *zeō*, "to boil," is translated "fervent" ([Acts 18:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/18-25.htm); [Rom. 12:11](https://biblehub.com/romans/12-11.htm)).

**Cold** water: refreshing, useful, clarifying. **Hot** water: medicinal, therapeutic. **Lukewarm**: nauseating, fit for neither purpose. The Laodicean church is spiritually fit for neither refreshment nor healing. Christ's wish that they were cold or hot is not a preference for outright rejection over compromise — rather, an antagonistic or openly opposing spirit still has more hope of redemption than the comfortable indifference of lukewarmness. A cold person knows they are cold and might seek warmth; a lukewarm person is comfortable and self-deceived.

**Cross-references**: [Acts 18:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/18-25.htm); [Rom. 12:11](https://biblehub.com/romans/12-11.htm); Sir. 43:20.

---

### [Rev 3:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-16.htm) — "So then because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth"

**Text**: "So then because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth."

**Exegesis**: The threat is visceral and graphic: *emesai se ek tou stomatos mou* — "I am about to vomit you out of my mouth." The verb (*emeō*) is the standard term for vomiting; it conveys Christ's utter revulsion at the lukewarm condition. It is not merely distaste but a physical rejection, an expulsion.

Lukewarmness (*chliaros*, appearing only here in the NT) denotes the condition between extremes: spiritual compromise, divided loyalty, self-sufficient complacency. The Laodiceans are not condemned for specific apostasy or heresy — no false teaching is named, no immorality charged. The sin is **indifference**: they have settled into a comfortable religion that neither burns with zeal nor freezes with explicit rejection of Christ. They have, as Stefanovic writes, "lost their original enthusiasm and zeal for spiritual matters." Their condition provides "neither refreshment for the spiritually weary, nor healing for the spiritually sick."

Christ will not tolerate divided loyalty: "No man can serve two masters" ([Matt. 6:24](https://biblehub.com/matthew/6-24.htm); [Luke 16:13](https://biblehub.com/luke/16-13.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Matt. 6:24](https://biblehub.com/matthew/6-24.htm); [Luke 16:13](https://biblehub.com/luke/16-13.htm).

### [Rev 3:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-17.htm) — "Because thou sayest, I am rich, and increased with goods"

**Text**: "Because thou sayest, I am rich, and increased with goods, and have need of nothing; and knowest not that thou art wretched, and miserable, and poor, and blind, and naked:"

**Exegesis**: The Laodiceans' self-assessment mirrors the city's own civic pride. The five-fold description of their actual condition (*talaipōros kai eleeinos kai ptōchos kai typhlos kai gymnos* — wretched, miserable, poor, blind, naked) directly inverts their self-perception ("I am rich, increased with goods, have need of nothing"). The contrast could not be starker.

- **"Rich"** (*plousios*) — material wealth mistaken for spiritual wealth, or material prosperity misread as divine favor. This echoes Hosea's rebuke of Ephraim: "Surely I have become rich, I have found wealth for myself; in all my labors they will find in me no iniquity, which would be sin" ([Hos. 12:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/12-8.htm)).
- **"Wretched"** (*talaipōros*) — miserable, in a pitiable condition.
- **"Miserable"** (*eleeinos*) — deserving of pity, pitiable.
- **"Poor"** (*ptōchos*) — Greek for *extremely* poor, poor like a beggar (the most destitute level of poverty). The same word is used of the spiritually poor who inherit the kingdom ([Matt. 5:3](https://biblehub.com/matthew/5-3.htm)). Smyrna is materially poor yet spiritually rich ([Rev. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-9.htm)); Laodicea has the opposite inversion.
- **"Blind"** (*typhlos*) — ironic given Laodicea's famous eye-medicine industry. They cannot see their own condition. Spiritual blindness is worse than physical blindness because it is self-concealing.
- **"Naked"** (*gymnos*) — nakedness in the ancient world signified being under judgment and severe humiliation ([2 Sam. 10:4](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/10-4.htm)–5; [Isa. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/20-4.htm); [Ezek. 16:37](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-37.htm)–39; [Nah. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/nahum/3-5.htm); [Rev. 17:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-16.htm)). This despite the city's famous black-wool textile trade. To be clothed in fine robes was honor ([Gen. 41:42](https://biblehub.com/genesis/41-42.htm); [Esther 6:6](https://biblehub.com/esther/6-6.htm)–11); nakedness was its shameful opposite.

**Cross-references**: [Hos. 12:8](https://biblehub.com/hosea/12-8.htm); [Rev. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-9.htm); [Matt. 5:3](https://biblehub.com/matthew/5-3.htm); [2 Sam. 10:4](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/10-4.htm)–5; [Isa. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/20-4.htm); [Ezek. 16:37](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-37.htm)–39.

### [Rev 3:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-18.htm) — "I counsel thee to buy of me gold tried in the fire"

**Text**: "I counsel thee to buy of me gold tried in the fire, that thou mayest be rich; and white raiment, that thou mayest be clothed, and that the shame of thy nakedness do not appear; and anoint thine eyes with eyesalve, that thou mayest see."

**Exegesis**: Christ's counsel is patterned as a threefold antidote corresponding point-for-point to Laodicea's three civic boasts and three spiritual deficiencies:

| Civic boast | Spiritual defect | Christ's antidote |
|---|---|---|
| Banking / gold wealth | Poor | Gold refined in fire |
| Black wool textile industry | Naked | White garments |
| Eye-salve medical school | Blind | Eyesalve from Christ |

**"Buy"** (*agorasei*) — the market metaphor implies exchange: they must give up something (their pride and self-sufficiency) to receive what Christ offers. The language of buying suggests the transaction is non-trivial; it costs them their self-sufficiency.

**"Gold refined in fire"** (*chrysion pepyrōmenon ek pyros*) — [1 Pet. 1:7](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/1-7.htm) is the interpretive key: "the proof of your faith, being more precious than gold which is perishable, even though tested by fire, may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ." The gold Christ offers is **tested faith** — genuine spiritual wealth that endures divine scrutiny. Material gold cannot buy spiritual reality.

**"White raiment"** (*himatia leuka*) — white garments are the consistent Revelation symbol for salvation, justification, and right standing before God ([Rev. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-4.htm)–5; 6:11; 7:9, 13–14; 19:8). "The righteous deeds of the saints" ([Rev. 19:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-8.htm)); "He has clothed me with garments of salvation, he has wrapped me with a robe of righteousness" ([Isa. 61:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/61-10.htm)). Nakedness covered = shame removed, dignity restored, standing before God secured ([Luke 15:22](https://biblehub.com/luke/15-22.htm); [Rev. 16:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-15.htm)).

**"Eyesalve"** (*kollyrion*) — the Laodicean medical school's famous Phrygian powder eye-remedy, here a metaphor for spiritual discernment. Only Christ can open spiritually blind eyes. The Laodiceans need to see what Christ already sees — their real condition — before they can seek remedy. Spiritual diagnosis precedes spiritual cure.

**Cross-references**: [1 Pet. 1:7](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/1-7.htm); [Isa. 61:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/61-10.htm); [Rev. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-4.htm)–5; 6:11; 7:9, 13–14; 19:8; [Luke 15:22](https://biblehub.com/luke/15-22.htm); [Rev. 16:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-15.htm).

### [Rev 3:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-19.htm) — "As many as I love, I rebuke and chasten"

**Text**: "As many as I love, I rebuke and chasten: be zealous therefore, and repent."

**Exegesis**: This verse is the interpretive key to the Laodicean message. The severity of Christ's diagnosis and threat is grounded in love (*philō*, the love of affection and friendship — the only use of *phileō* in Revelation for Christ's love for the churches; cf. [John 5:20](https://biblehub.com/john/5-20.htm); 16:27). Rebuke and chastening are not rejection but the discipline of a Father for his children.

The OT and NT together affirm this: [Prov. 3:11](https://biblehub.com/proverbs/3-11.htm)–12 ("My son, do not reject the discipline of the LORD, nor loathe his reproof, for whom the LORD loves he reproves, even as a father the son in whom he delights"); [Heb. 12:5](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-5.htm)–11 (extended reflection on divine discipline as evidence of sonship: "What son is there whom his father does not discipline? But if you are without discipline... you are illegitimate children"). Stefanovic notes that Philadelphians and Laodiceans are the only two churches explicitly told they are loved — but the expressions differ: Philadelphia is loved with *agapaō* (covenant love, v. 9); Laodicea with *phileō* (tender affection).

The exhortation: **"be zealous"** (*zēleue*, present imperative) — the word for ardent devotion, the opposite of lukewarmness; *zēlos* is related to *zestos* (hot, v. 15). To be zealous is to become hot. Then, **"repent"** (*metanoēson*, aorist imperative) — a decisive, single act of turning. Barclay, cited by Stefanovic, describes repentance as the moment when "the things he thought important become unimportant, and the things he disregarded become the most important things in life" — a total reversal of orientation and values.

**Cross-references**: [Prov. 3:11](https://biblehub.com/proverbs/3-11.htm)–12; [John 5:20](https://biblehub.com/john/5-20.htm); 16:27; [Heb. 12:5](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/12-5.htm)–11; [Rev. 3:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-15.htm)–16.

### [Rev 3:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-20.htm) — "Behold, I stand at the door, and knock"

**Text**: "Behold, I stand at the door, and knock: if any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him, and will sup with him, and he with me."

**Exegesis**: One of the most famous images in the NT, and one of the most intimate. The present tense "I **am** standing" (*hestēka*, perfect of *histēmi* = "I have taken my stand and remain standing") and "knocking" (*krouō*, present participle) indicate a continuous, ongoing action — Christ is not making a single knock but persistently, patiently knocking.

The background is the Song of [Solomon 5:2](https://biblehub.com/songs/5-2.htm)–6, where the lover stands at the beloved's door at night, knocking and pleading for entry. The image is one of romantic urgency and patient yearning — Christ outside the door of the church he addresses, seeking readmission. The Laodiceans have, in their self-sufficient religion, effectively displaced Christ from the center of their communal life. Their religion is not Christ-centered. He stands outside, not inside.

**"Eat supper with him"** (*deipnēsō met' autou*) — *deipneō* refers to the *deipnon*, the principal evening meal, the usual occasion for hospitality, intimacy, and friendship. In the Near East, sharing a common meal established and expressed a "strong bond of affection and companionship" and was a common symbol of the intimacy of the messianic kingdom. David Aune notes that Christian homes functioned as worship-gathering places in the first century. But here the address shifts from the congregation to the **individual** (*tis*, "anyone") — even if the church as a whole does not respond, any individual may open the door. Leon Morris: "Even if the church as a whole does not heed the warning, some individuals may."

Christ does not force the door. He waits. The choice belongs to the lukewarm Laodicean. The tragedy is that the most self-satisfied church is the one that has left Christ standing outside.

**Cross-references**: [Song 5:2](https://biblehub.com/songs/5-2.htm)–6; [Rev. 19:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-9.htm); [John 14:23](https://biblehub.com/john/14-23.htm); [Luke 24:29](https://biblehub.com/luke/24-29.htm)–30.

### [Rev 3:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-21.htm) — "To him that overcometh will I grant to sit with me in my throne"

**Text**: "To him that overcometh will I grant to sit with me in my throne, even as I also overcame, and am set down with my Father in his throne."

**Exegesis**: The promise to the Laodicean overcomer is the grandest of all seven overcomer promises — to **share Christ's throne**, as Christ shares the Father's throne. This is the climactic promise of the entire seven-letter sequence.

The structure is theologically dense: Christ's enthronement with the Father is the ground and pattern for the believer's co-enthronement with Christ. The logic mirrors Paul's: God "raised us up with him, and seated us with him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus" ([Eph. 2:6](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-6.htm)). The believer's royal dignity is derived, shared, and participatory — not autonomous.

**"The throne"** (*thronos*) — in the ancient Near East, the royal throne was "more like a couch than a single seat" (Stefanovic), enabling multiple persons to be seated together on it. Thus to be granted a place at the king's right hand on his throne was the highest conceivable honor ([1 Kings 2:19](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/2-19.htm); [Ps. 110:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/110-1.htm); 1 Esdras 4:29). God's faithful people have been promised this share with Christ on his throne. Already "elevated in the heavenly places" in present standing ([Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:9–10), the final fulfillment comes at the Second Coming ([Rev. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-4.htm)–6), when the saints reign with Christ for a thousand years.

This promise implicitly addresses the Laodicean condition: those who seek a throne of wealth and self-sufficiency in this life will be vomited out; those who overcome self-deception and lukewarmness through repentance will share the ultimate throne.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 110:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/110-1.htm); [1 Kings 2:19](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/2-19.htm); [Eph. 2:6](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-6.htm); [Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:9–10; 20:4–6; 1 Esdras 4:29.

### [Rev 3:22](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-22.htm) — "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches"

**Text**: "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches."

**Exegesis**: The closing formula — identical to vv. 6 and 13, and to the closings in Rev. 2 — here reaches its final iteration in the seven-letter section of Revelation. In Rev. 2, the ear-call came before the overcomer promise; in Rev. 3, this order is reversed — the overcomer promise precedes the ear-call, heightening the urgency: hearing is the gateway to overcoming.

The Laodicean message is addressed universally: it speaks to any church or individual in the condition of comfortable, heresy-free, sin-free, *indifferent* Christianity. The lack of overt opposition or persecution does not indicate health; it may indicate the most dangerous spiritual condition of all — self-satisfied blindness. The message is eschatological in scope: Stefanovic sees verbal and conceptual parallels between the Laodicean letter and [Rev. 16:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-15.htm) (the final warning at Armageddon: "Blessed is the one who stays awake and keeps his garments, so that he will not walk about naked and men will not see his shame") — the same triad of "clothing," "nakedness," and "shame." This suggests John intended the Laodicean letter as a paradigm for the last period of earth's history: a church free from gross apostasy yet captive to self-sufficiency and halfhearted service, receiving Christ's most urgent personal appeal before the final crisis.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-7.htm), 11, 17, 29; 3:6, 13; 16:15; [Matt. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/matthew/11-15.htm); 13:9.
