## Revelation 8

### [Rev 8:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-1.htm) — "And when he had opened the seventh seal..."

**Text**: "And when he had opened the seventh seal, there was silence in heaven about the space of half an hour."

**Exegesis**: The seventh seal concludes the entire seal series (Rev. 6–8:1) and marks a structural hinge in Revelation. The seal is opened by the Lamb (cf. 5:9), and the result is not a vision of catastrophe but profound, cosmic silence. Several OT texts illuminate this silence: [Hab. 2:20](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/2-20.htm) ("The LORD is in his holy temple: let all the earth keep silence before him"), [Zeph. 1:7](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/1-7.htm), and [Zech. 2:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/2-13.htm) — in each, silence is enjoined before God's imminent judgment-advent from his temple. The silence thus signals that God is about to leave his heavenly sanctuary and descend in judgment; the universe holds its breath. Some interpreters link it to the offering of the saints' prayers (8:3–4) — in Second Temple liturgy (Tamid 7:3), the congregation fell silent while the priest burned incense on the golden altar. Structurally, the silence may function as "a calm after the storm of destruction occasioned by Christ's second coming" (Paulien): the four horsemen have finished, the sealing is complete (7:3), the great tribulation is over, and the Second Coming has occurred (cf. 6:16–17). The silence may correspond to the millennium (a relatively short period vs. eternity) or the post-millennial moment before the new creation, recalling Jewish apocalyptic tradition: "And the world shall be turned back to primeval silence for seven days, as it was at the first beginnings" (4 [Ezra 7:30](https://biblehub.com/ezra/7-30.htm)). The "half an hour" is enigmatic and most likely symbolic for a brief, unspecified period — not a chronological marker. Jewish sources also cite the silence in heaven so that saints' prayers can ascend and be heard in judgment on the wicked (Bauckham). The silence is a dramatic pause before the trumpets intensify divine judgment; it assures God's faithful that they are about to find their rest ([Rev. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-14.htm)–17). Battistone: "John pictures what heaven will be like when God leaves His holy dwelling and heads for earth to bring his righteous judgment on its inhabitants."

**Cross-references**: [Hab. 2:20](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/2-20.htm); [Zeph. 1:7](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/1-7.htm); [Zech. 2:13](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/2-13.htm); [Rev. 6:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-16.htm)–17; 7:1–3; 7:14–17; 4 [Ezra 7:30](https://biblehub.com/ezra/7-30.htm); 2 Bar. 3:7; Rev. 20 (millennium parallel).

### [Rev 8:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-2.htm) — "And I saw the seven angels..."

**Text**: "And I saw the seven angels which stood before God; and to them were given seven trumpets."

**Exegesis**: With v2 a new phase of the vision begins; the seventh seal directly inaugurates the trumpet series. "The seven angels" (Gr. *hoi hepta angeloi*) bear the definite article, implying a well-known, specific class of beings. They are not described earlier in canonical Scripture, but [Isa. 63:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/63-9.htm) mentions "the angel of His presence," and [Luke 1:19](https://biblehub.com/luke/1-19.htm) records Gabriel identifying himself as one who "stands in the presence of God." Jewish tradition (1 Enoch 20:1–7; Tobit 12:15) names seven: Uriel, Raphael, Raguel, Michael, Saraqael, Gabriel, and Remiel. Their posture — *standing before God* — is OT idiom for readiness in service: "as the LORD lives, before whom I stand" ([1 Kings 17:1](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/17-1.htm); 18:15; [2 Kings 3:14](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/3-14.htm); 5:16). They are a distinct class from the living creatures and elders of chapters 4–5. These may be the same angels later commissioned to pour out the seven bowl plagues (chs. 15–16), suggesting continuity between the trumpet and bowl series.

"Trumpets" (Gr. *salpinx*; Heb. *shophar* or *chatsotserah*): sacred instruments with a wide OT range of functions — military alarm ([Num. 10:9](https://biblehub.com/numbers/10-9.htm); Judg. 3:27; [Jer. 51:27](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-27.htm)), royal proclamation ([1 Kings 1:34](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/1-34.htm), 39), festival summons ([Num. 10:10](https://biblehub.com/numbers/10-10.htm)), and temple liturgy (2 Chron. 5:12–13; 13:12–14). The *chatsotserah* was the priests' instrument of hammered metal; the *shophar*, made of ram's horn, was the signal instrument whose function was noise rather than music ([Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm); [Josh. 6:4](https://biblehub.com/joshua/6-4.htm)). Both were regarded as sacred. In eschatological texts, the *shophar/salpinx* signals the end-time appearance of God in judgment ([Isa. 27:13](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/27-13.htm); [Joel 2:1](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-1.htm); [Zeph. 1:16](https://biblehub.com/zephaniah/1-16.htm); [Zech. 9:14](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/9-14.htm); [Matt. 24:31](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-31.htm); [1 Cor. 15:51](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-51.htm)–53; [1 Thess. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-16.htm)). The "were given" (Gr. *edothesan*) is the Semitic divine passive — God is the implied agent who commissions these angels and entrusts them with the instruments of judgment. The seven trumpets are therefore God's sovereign interventions in history, not random catastrophes.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 63:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/63-9.htm); [Luke 1:19](https://biblehub.com/luke/1-19.htm); 1 Enoch 20:1–7; Tobit 12:15; [1 Kings 17:1](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/17-1.htm); [Num. 10:2](https://biblehub.com/numbers/10-2.htm)–10; [Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm); [Josh. 6:4](https://biblehub.com/joshua/6-4.htm); [Joel 2:1](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-1.htm); [1 Cor. 15:51](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-51.htm)–53; [1 Thess. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/4-16.htm); Rev. 15–16.

### [Rev 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm) — "And another angel came and stood at the altar..."

**Text**: "And another angel came and stood at the altar, having a golden censer; and there was given unto him much incense, that he should offer it with the prayers of all saints upon the golden altar which was before the throne."

**Exegesis**: "Another angel" (*allos angelos*) is a distinct figure from the seven trumpet angels — an intercessory angel mediating the saints' prayers before God. He carries a *golden censer* (Gr. *libanoton chrysoun*) and stands at "the altar" — this is the OT altar of burnt offering (located outside the temple proper), distinct from "the golden altar which is before the throne" mentioned later in this verse. The two altars mirror the OT tabernacle/temple's dual-altar structure: the bronze altar of burnt offering in the outer court, and the golden altar of incense in the Holy Place directly "in front of the veil … in front of the mercy seat" ([Exod. 30:6](https://biblehub.com/exodus/30-6.htm)–7; cf. [Heb. 9:3](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/9-3.htm)–4). The scene is richly grounded in the Jerusalem temple's *tamid* (daily) service: after the sacrificial lamb was placed on the altar of burnt offering and the blood poured at its base, the assigned priest took the golden censer, entered the Holy Place, and burned incense on the golden altar (Tamid 5:4–5; [Luke 1:8](https://biblehub.com/luke/1-8.htm)–11; cf. [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–4). During this act, the congregation kept reverential silence in the court (Tamid 7:3; cf. [Rev. 8:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-1.htm) — linking the silence of v1 to this liturgical background).

"Much incense" (*thymiamata polla*): the quantity of incense signals abundance, corresponding to the vast number of saints whose prayers are being presented. [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm) identifies the incense explicitly as "the prayers of the saints." Here those prayers are the same prayers of the martyred saints in [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm): "How long, O Lord, holy and true, wilt thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth?" The "golden altar which is before the throne" is the heavenly counterpart to the OT golden altar of incense — the ultimate locus of intercession before the very throne of God.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm); [Rev. 6:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-9.htm)–11; [Exod. 30:6](https://biblehub.com/exodus/30-6.htm)–7; [Lev. 16:12](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/16-12.htm)–13; [Luke 1:8](https://biblehub.com/luke/1-8.htm)–11; [Heb. 9:3](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/9-3.htm)–4; Tamid 5:4; 7:3.

### [Rev 8:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-4.htm) — "And the smoke of the incense..."

**Text**: "And the smoke of the incense, which came with the prayers of the saints, ascended up before God out of the angel's hand."

**Exegesis**: The ascending smoke is the visual, liturgical confirmation that the saints' prayers have been received by God. "Ascended before God" (*anebe enopion tou theou*) echoes the OT imagery in which ascending incense smoke represents prayer rising to God's presence ([Ps. 141:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/141-2.htm): "Let my prayer be set forth before thee as incense"). The phrase "out of the angel's hand" stresses that it is the angel who presents these prayers — an intercessory mediating function. The verse provides theological assurance: no prayer of the oppressed faithful falls unheard. The prayers of the martyrs beneath the altar (6:10) have reached "the throne of grace" ([Heb. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/4-16.htm)). The entire narrative of 8:3–4 is God's acknowledgment of those prayers. This moment of reception immediately precedes the judgment action of v5, establishing the causal connection: divine judgment on the persecutors flows directly from God's hearing of the saints' prayers.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 141:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/141-2.htm); [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm); [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm); [Heb. 4:16](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/4-16.htm); [Ps. 66:18](https://biblehub.com/psalms/66-18.htm)–20.

### [Rev 8:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-5.htm) — "And the angel took the censer..."

**Text**: "And the angel took the censer, and filled it with fire of the altar, and cast it into the earth: and there were voices, and thunderings, and lightnings, and an earthquake."

**Exegesis**: The scene pivots dramatically. The censer — the instrument of intercession — now becomes an instrument of judgment. The angel fills it with *fire from the altar* (the altar of burnt offering, the same altar connected to the saints' prayers) and hurls it earthward. This gesture is drawn directly from [Ezek. 10:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/10-1.htm)–2, where the man clothed in linen takes coals of fire from between the cherubim and scatters them over Jerusalem as a token of divine judgment for the abominations committed there. Jesus' own words resonate: "I have come to cast fire upon the earth" ([Luke 12:49](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-49.htm)). Robert Thomas notes that the censer, normally a vessel of incense-offering, "becomes a symbol of judgment in response to the prayers." The identity of instrument is theologically significant: the same altar that received the prayers of the saints now sends fire of judgment upon those who persecuted them. Judgment is the answer to prayer.

The accompanying theophanic phenomena — *voices, thunderings, lightnings, and earthquake* (Gr. *phonai kai brontai kai astrapai kai seismos*) — are the standard OT and Revelation signature of God's presence and self-disclosure in judgment, modeled on the Sinai theophany ([Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm)–19). This fourfold formula recurs at each structural turning point in Revelation: 4:5 (before the throne), 8:5 (before the trumpets), 11:19 (before the bowls), 16:18 (at Armageddon) — each time intensifying. Here it signals that God himself is acting in response to the saints' cry; he is "preparing to bring his righteous judgments and vengeance upon those who viciously harassed and oppressed the faithful."

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 10:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/10-1.htm)–2; [Luke 12:49](https://biblehub.com/luke/12-49.htm); [Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm)–19; [Rev. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-5.htm); 11:19; 16:18; [Exod. 3:7](https://biblehub.com/exodus/3-7.htm).

### [Rev 8:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-6.htm) — "And the seven angels which had the seven trumpets prepared themselves to sound."

**Text**: "And the seven angels which had the seven trumpets prepared themselves to sound."

**Exegesis**: This verse functions as the formal commissioning signal. The incense offering (vv3–4) and the fiery casting (v5) serve together as the trigger for the angels to sound. In the OT daily temple service, the burning of incense on the golden altar was followed immediately by the blowing of trumpets by the priests (Tamid 7:3; cf. 2 Chron. 5:12–13), marking the conclusion of the sacrificial ceremony. The heavenly liturgy in Revelation mirrors and consummates that earthly pattern. "Prepared themselves" (Gr. *hetoimasan heautous*) implies deliberate readiness — the angels stand in solemn ordered array. The structural function of v6 is transitional: it closes the introductory sanctuary scene (vv2–6) and opens the main body of trumpet plagues. The trumpets are explicitly framed as God's response to the martyrs' intercession (6:10), God's self-assertion as the deliverer of his people against "those who dwell on the earth" — language which in Revelation consistently designates the enemies of the gospel (cf. 3:10; 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 17:8).

**Cross-references**: Tamid 7:3; 2 Chron. 5:12–13; [Rev. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-10.htm); [Rev. 8:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-13.htm); [Num. 10:8](https://biblehub.com/numbers/10-8.htm)–10.

### [Rev 8:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-7.htm) — "The first angel sounded..."

**Text**: "The first angel sounded, and there followed hail and fire mingled with blood, and they were cast upon the earth: and the third part of trees was burnt up, and all green grass was burnt up."

**Exegesis**: The first trumpet unleashes *hail and fire mixed with blood* (*chalaza kai pyr memigmena en haimati*). This compound image has a dual OT source. Primary: the seventh Egyptian plague of [Exod. 9:23](https://biblehub.com/exodus/9-23.htm)–25, where hail mixed with fire devastated Egypt, destroying every plant and tree of the field — a judgment for Pharaoh's refusal to release Israel. Secondary: [Ezek. 38:22](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-22.htm)–23, God's eschatological judgment against Gog through "an overflowing rain, and great hailstones, fire, and brimstone." In OT judgment contexts, hail and fire are consistently God's weapons against those who oppose him and oppress his people ([Ps. 18:12](https://biblehub.com/psalms/18-12.htm)–14; [Isa. 10:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/10-16.htm)–19; 30:30; [Ezek. 38:22](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-22.htm)–23) — never against faithful Israel.

The effect: *a third of the earth burned, a third of the trees burned, all green grass burned.* "A third part" (*to triton*) is the proportional marker for the trumpet plagues (8:7–12; 9:15, 18), contrasting with the "fourth part" of the seal plagues (6:8). In [Ezek. 5:12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/5-12.htm)–13 and [Zech. 13:8](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/13-8.htm)–9, divine judgment against apostate Israel is described in thirds. Satan sweeps a third of the stars in [Rev. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-4.htm). The "third" signals partial but severe judgment — not total annihilation, but substantial, purposeful divine retribution on a portion of the enemy kingdom.

*Trees* (Gr. *ta dendra*): OT symbol of Israel as God's covenant people — [Ps. 1:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/1-3.htm) (righteous as a tree by water), 52:8 (olive tree), 92:12–14 (palm and cedar), [Jer. 11:15](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/11-15.htm)–17 (Judah as a green olive tree), [Isa. 61:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/61-3.htm) (trees of righteousness). *Green grass* (Gr. *chortos chloros*): [Isa. 40:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/40-6.htm)–8; 44:2–4 — grass imagery applied to Israel. John the Baptist declared that Jewish leaders who bore no fruit would be cut down and cast into fire ([Matt. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/matthew/3-10.htm); cf. 7:17–19). Jesus applied this directly: "If they do these things in the green tree, what shall be done in the dry?" ([Luke 23:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-31.htm)) — Jesus himself is the green tree; apostate Jerusalem the dry tree. The Josephan account of the Roman siege of Jerusalem in A.D. 70 (involving burning of trees, slaughter, and famine) is the primary historical referent. Judgment beginning at the house of God ([1 Pet. 4:17](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/4-17.htm); cf. Ezek. 9): the Jewish nation, as former covenant people who rejected Christ and persecuted his church, bears the first trumpet's blow.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 9:23](https://biblehub.com/exodus/9-23.htm)–25; [Ezek. 38:22](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/38-22.htm)–23; [Ps. 18:12](https://biblehub.com/psalms/18-12.htm)–14; [Isa. 10:16](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/10-16.htm)–19; [Ezek. 5:12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/5-12.htm)–13; [Zech. 13:8](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/13-8.htm)–9; [Jer. 11:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/11-16.htm); [Luke 23:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/23-31.htm); [Matt. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/matthew/3-10.htm); [1 Pet. 4:17](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/4-17.htm); [Rev. 12:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-4.htm); 16:19.

### [Rev 8:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-8.htm) — "And the second angel sounded, and as it were a great mountain burning with fire was cast into the sea..."

**Text**: "And the second angel sounded, and as it were a great mountain burning with fire was cast into the sea: and the third part of the sea became blood;"

**Exegesis**: The second trumpet vision centers on *a great mountain burning with fire* (*oros mega pyromenon*) cast into the sea. In OT prophecy, *mountain* is the standard metaphor for a kingdom or empire ([Ps. 48:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/48-1.htm)–2; [Isa. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/2-2.htm)–3; 13:4; 31:4; [Dan. 2:35](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-35.htm), 44–45; [Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm); Obad. 8–9). God's own kingdom appears as a mountain filling the earth in [Dan. 2:35](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-35.htm). Critically, Paulien observes that in OT judgment passages, "mountains as representing nations are always the object of God's judgments, never the agents" ([Isa. 41:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-15.htm); 42:15; [Ezek. 35:2](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/35-2.htm)–7; 38:20; [Zech. 4:7](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/4-7.htm)). The most decisive source text is [Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm): "'Behold, I am against you, O destroying mountain, who destroys the whole earth,' declares the LORD. 'And I will … make you a burnt out mountain.'" This is Jeremiah's oracle against Babylon. The mountain is thrown into the sea ([Jer. 51:42](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-42.htm): "The sea has come up over Babylon; she has been engulfed with its tumultuous waves") and sinks ([Jer. 51:63](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-63.htm)–64: "Just so shall Babylon sink down and not rise again"). [Rev. 18:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-21.htm) echoes the same image for spiritual Babylon: "A strong angel took up a stone like a great millstone and threw it into the sea."

By John's time, "Babylon" was the standard Jewish-Christian cipher for Rome ([1 Pet. 5:13](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/5-13.htm); [Rev. 17:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-18.htm)). Rome destroyed Jerusalem and the Temple (A.D. 70) and subsequently persecuted the church. Thus, the burning mountain of the second trumpet points to the downfall of the Roman Empire. Thiele summarizes: after Jerusalem fell, "the next scene of judgment is on a much broader and vaster scale … Like flames of fire from heaven came Genseric the Vandal, Alaric the Goth, and Attila the Hun, leaving in their wake scenes of ruin, desolation, carnage, and blood. Irresistible and destructive as a flaming mountain, the hordes of barbarians fell upon the peoples of Rome." The sea becoming blood recalls the first Egyptian plague ([Exod. 7:20](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-20.htm)–21).

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 51:24](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-24.htm)–25, 42, 63–64; [Dan. 2:35](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-35.htm), 44; [Isa. 41:15](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-15.htm); [Ezek. 35:2](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/35-2.htm)–7; [Rev. 18:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-2.htm), 8, 21; [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); [1 Pet. 5:13](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/5-13.htm); [Rev. 17:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-18.htm); [Exod. 7:20](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-20.htm)–21.

### [Rev 8:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-9.htm) — "And the third part of the creatures which were in the sea..."

**Text**: "And the third part of the creatures which were in the sea, and had life, died; and the third part of the ships were destroyed."

**Exegesis**: The burning mountain's impact on the sea triggers a two-part destruction: a third of marine life perishes, and a third of the ships are destroyed. The Egyptian plague of [Exod. 7:21](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-21.htm) — fish dying when the Nile turned to blood — stands behind the marine death. The *sea* in OT prophecy often symbolizes peoples and nations in their restlessness and hostility to God ([Isa. 17:12](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/17-12.htm)–13; 57:20; [Jer. 51:41](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-41.htm)–42; [Dan. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-2.htm)–3, 17 — the four beasts rise from the sea). *Ships* signify a nation's commercial power and pride in self-sufficiency — the economic sinew of an empire (cf. [Rev. 18:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-17.htm)–19, where merchants and shipmasters lament Babylon's fall). The destruction of a third of the sea's ships thus depicts the collapse of the Roman Empire's vast economic and social order — its trade networks, maritime dominance, and mercantile wealth — under the barbarian invasions. Together, the first two trumpets address the two powers jointly responsible for Christ's crucifixion: the Jewish nation (first trumpet) and the Roman Empire (second trumpet). Both united in opposing the gospel and persecuting the church; both experience the first waves of divine judgment.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 7:20](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-20.htm)–21; [Isa. 17:12](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/17-12.htm)–13; 57:20; [Jer. 51:41](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-41.htm)–42; [Dan. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-2.htm)–3; [Rev. 18:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-17.htm)–19.

### [Rev 8:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-10.htm) — "And the third angel sounded, and there fell a great star from heaven..."

**Text**: "And the third angel sounded, and there fell a great star from heaven, burning as it were a lamp, and it fell upon the third part of the rivers, and upon the fountains of waters;"

**Exegesis**: The third trumpet introduces *a great star* (*aster megas*) falling from heaven, burning like a torch (*lampas*), descending on the rivers and springs of water. Stars in the OT and Revelation frequently symbolize angelic beings ([Job 38:7](https://biblehub.com/job/38-7.htm); [Rev. 1:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-20.htm); 9:1; 12:4). The critical resonance is [Isa. 14:12](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/14-12.htm)–15 — *hêlēl ben-šāḥar* ("son of the morning," Lucifer), the star fallen from heaven through pride and rebellion: "How you have fallen from heaven, O star of the morning!" Jesus confirms the identification: "I was watching Satan fall from heaven like lightning" ([Luke 10:18](https://biblehub.com/luke/10-18.htm)). The "great" quality of this star indicates a being of supreme angelic stature. It is the same entity as the fallen star of [Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm), there identified as the "angel of the abyss" (9:11: Abaddon/Apollyon). That the star is already falling (perfect participle *piptonta*) indicates the fall preceded the third trumpet — it is the ongoing condition of Satan cast down.

*Rivers and springs* (Gr. *potamoi* and *pēgai*) are rich OT symbols of spiritual nourishment: the "fountain of living waters" ([Jer. 2:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/2-13.htm) — God himself), the "springs of salvation" ([Isa. 12:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/12-3.htm)), Solomon's "fountain of life" as wise teaching ([Prov. 13:14](https://biblehub.com/proverbs/13-14.htm)), [Ps. 1:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/1-3.htm) (the righteous man as a tree by streams of water). Jesus at the Feast of Tabernacles: "From his innermost being will flow rivers of living water" — the Spirit ([John 7:38](https://biblehub.com/john/7-38.htm)–39). The rivers and springs of water represent the gospel, the Word of God, and salvation — the spiritual life-sustaining sources of the church.

### [Rev 8:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-11.htm) — "And the name of the star is called Wormwood..."

**Text**: "And the name of the star is called Wormwood: and the third part of the waters became wormwood; and many men died of the waters, because they were made bitter."

**Exegesis**: *Wormwood* (Gr. *apsinthos*; the plant *Artemisia absinthium*) is a bitter herb notorious throughout the ancient Near East ([Deut. 29:17](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/29-17.htm)–18; [Lam. 3:19](https://biblehub.com/lamentations/3-19.htm)). Though not itself poisonous, poisonous associations clung to it in biblical usage ([Deut. 29:18](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/29-18.htm); [Jer. 9:15](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/9-15.htm); [Amos 6:12](https://biblehub.com/amos/6-12.htm)). The decisive OT uses are in Jeremiah: because Israel forsook God and rejected his Word ([Jer. 8:9](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/8-9.htm)), God threatened to "give them wormwood to eat and poisoned water to drink" ([Jer. 9:13](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/9-13.htm)–15; cf. 8:14). The same threat fell on the false prophets who polluted the land from Jerusalem ([Jer. 23:15](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/23-15.htm)). Wormwood is OT shorthand for divine punishment for apostasy and the suffering that follows from abandoning covenant with God.

The star-named-Wormwood poisons the rivers and springs — the spiritual waters of life — turning a third of them bitter, causing many to die. The exegetical logic: Satan pollutes the channels of spiritual truth and salvation through human religious teachers and institutions. "Wormwood is a symbol of bitterness through sin and apostasy" (Paulien). The death that follows is spiritual death — souls lost through drinking from polluted, false teaching. Thiele: "The pure church is a clear stream and a life-giving fountain. When the enemy enters that church it becomes corrupt. Henceforth it is a scourge rather than a blessing to men." Historically, following the fall of Rome (second trumpet) which Paul identified as "the restrainer" ([2 Thess. 2:6](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-6.htm)–7), the tide of medieval apostasy flooded the church. The Dark and Middle Ages witnessed the replacement of apostolic gospel with church tradition, magisterial authority, and dogma. The waters of truth were made bitter. Those who drank died spiritually; those who resisted faced persecution. Paul had warned of precisely this apostasy ([Acts 20:26](https://biblehub.com/acts/20-26.htm)–31; [1 Tim. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/4-1.htm)–2; [2 Tim. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/4-3.htm)–4; [2 Thess. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-1.htm)–12); Jesus had warned of false prophets seducing the elect ([Matt. 24:4](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-4.htm)–5, 11, 23–24).

**Cross-references**: [Deut. 29:17](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/29-17.htm)–18; [Lam. 3:19](https://biblehub.com/lamentations/3-19.htm); [Jer. 8:9](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/8-9.htm), 14; 9:13–15; 23:15; [Amos 6:12](https://biblehub.com/amos/6-12.htm); [2 Thess. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/2_thessalonians/2-1.htm)–12; [Acts 20:26](https://biblehub.com/acts/20-26.htm)–31; [1 Tim. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/1_timothy/4-1.htm)–2; [2 Tim. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/4-3.htm)–4; [Matt. 24:4](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-4.htm)–5, 11.

### [Rev 8:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-12.htm) — "And the fourth angel sounded..."

**Text**: "And the fourth angel sounded, and the third part of the sun was smitten, and the third part of the moon, and the third part of the stars; so as the third part of them was darkened, and the day shone not for a third part of it, and the night likewise."

**Exegesis**: The fourth trumpet strikes the celestial bodies — sun, moon, and stars — darkening a third of each, reducing both day and night by a third. Cosmologically, the heavenly lights were created to govern time and give light to the earth ([Gen. 1:14](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-14.htm)–18); their darkening is the reversal and undoing of creation's order.

In OT prophetic literature, the darkening of heavenly bodies is the stereotyped symbol of God's judgment-appearance. Ezekiel against Egypt: "I will cover the heavens and darken their stars; I will cover the sun with a cloud, and the moon shall not give its light … I will set darkness on your land" ([Ezek. 32:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/32-7.htm)–8). Isaiah on the Day of the LORD against Babylon: "The stars of heaven and their constellations will not flash forth their light; the sun will be dark when it rises, and the moon will not shed its light" ([Isa. 13:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/13-10.htm)). Amos: the Day of the LORD is "darkness and not light" ([Amos 5:18](https://biblehub.com/amos/5-18.htm)). Joel: "a day of darkness and gloom … the sun and moon grow dark, and the stars lose their brightness" ([Joel 2:2](https://biblehub.com/joel/2-2.htm), 10; 3:15). Jesus himself linked the darkening of celestial bodies to his Parousia ([Matt. 24:29](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-29.htm); [Mark 13:24](https://biblehub.com/mark/13-24.htm)–25). The fourth trumpet also echoes the ninth Egyptian plague — the plague of absolute darkness ([Exod. 10:21](https://biblehub.com/exodus/10-21.htm)–23).

In NT theology, the spiritual register of this darkness is explicit: light = gospel, Christ, truth; darkness = sin, apostasy, spiritual blindness ([John 1:9](https://biblehub.com/john/1-9.htm); 8:12; 12:46; [2 Cor. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/4-4.htm)–6; [Col. 1:13](https://biblehub.com/colossians/1-13.htm)–14; [1 Pet. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/2-9.htm)). "The god of this world has blinded the minds of the unbelieving, that they might not see the light of the gospel" ([2 Cor. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/4-4.htm)). Darkness is the absence of the gospel and its consequences. Judgment here is spiritual — the partial obliteration of the gospel as the source of spiritual life.

Historically, the fourth trumpet extends the apostasy of the third trumpet: after medieval apostasy, the Protestant Reformation reclaimed gospel light but its vitality was successively dimmed by (1) Protestant scholasticism — theological polemics replacing personal relationship with Christ; (2) the Age of Enlightenment/Reason (16th–18th centuries), ending Christian faith's hegemony over the Western mind; (3) the rise of rationalism, skepticism, humanism, and secularism — replacing biblical authority with human reason, robbing millions of salvific hope. The "third" of darkness is partial — merciful warning, not final extinction of the gospel — but its trajectory is toward deeper darkness in the fifth and sixth trumpets.

### [Rev 8:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-13.htm) — "And I beheld, and heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven..."

**Text**: "And I beheld, and heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven, saying with a loud voice, Woe, woe, woe, to the inhabiters of the earth by reason of the other voices of the trumpet of the three angels, which are yet to sound!"

**Exegesis**: After the first four trumpets John hears a transitional announcement — an *angel* (better: *vulture*, Gr. *aetos*) flying in *midheaven* (*mesouranēma*, the zenith visible to all). The Greek *aetos* can mean either "eagle" or "vulture"; in context (cf. [Rev. 19:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-17.htm)–18, where birds gather to eat the flesh of the slain) the vulture (*griffon vulture*) is more apt, as it is the OT symbol of impending judgment and disaster ([Deut. 28:49](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/28-49.htm); [Ezek. 32:4](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/32-4.htm); 39:17; [Hos. 8:1](https://biblehub.com/hosea/8-1.htm); [Hab. 1:8](https://biblehub.com/habakkuk/1-8.htm)). Hosea's judgment oracle is especially close: "Put the trumpet to your lips! Like an eagle [vulture] the enemy comes against the house of the LORD, because they have transgressed my covenant" ([Hos. 8:1](https://biblehub.com/hosea/8-1.htm)). Jesus: "Wherever the corpse is, there the vultures will gather" ([Matt. 24:28](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-28.htm); [Luke 17:37](https://biblehub.com/luke/17-37.htm)) — a statement in the context of eschatological judgment.

The triple *ouai, ouai, ouai* (*woe, woe, woe*) — one "woe" for each of the three remaining trumpet angels (trumpets 5, 6, 7) — is the first of seven occurrences of *ouai* in Revelation (8:13; 9:12; 11:14; 12:12; 18:10, 16, 19), deliberately counterpoising the book's seven beatitudes (*makarios*, "blessed"). The intensification is structural: the first four trumpets were divine warnings mixed with mercy, affecting the physical-historical order; the last three ("woes") escalate to demonic, spiritual torment targeting exclusively "those who dwell on the earth" (*tous katoikountas epi tēs gēs*) — Revelation's consistent designation for the wicked who are hostile to the gospel and God's people (3:10; 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8, 14; 17:8). These are those who have refused to repent and have persecuted the saints. The first four trumpets, catastrophic as they were, are reframed as warnings; what is about to come — "spiritual torment and death" resulting from demonic activity (Paulien) — is far worse.

The scene bears verbal parallels to [Rev. 14:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-6.htm)–7 (the angel flying in midheaven with the eternal gospel), suggesting structural intentionality: both are mid-heaven proclamations at pivotal transitions in Revelation's drama. The vulture's cry from the zenith of heaven ensures that no one can claim ignorance of what approaches. Flying in midheaven (*en mesouranēmati*) places the announcement at maximum visibility — as if broadcast to the entire world.

---

**Structural summary of Revelation 8**: The chapter operates on two levels simultaneously — heavenly liturgy and earthly judgment. Verses 1 links back to the seal series; vv2–6 are the indispensable interpretive key for all seven trumpets, embedding them in the priestly incense ritual and grounding their purpose in the answered prayers of the martyred saints (6:10). Verses 7–12 give the first four trumpets in two pairs: earth-judgment (fire/hail) and sea-judgment (burning mountain); then spiritual-source pollution (Wormwood) and gospel-light diminishment (darkness). The historical trajectory moves from the Jewish-Roman world of the first century (vv7–9), through the medieval apostasy (vv10–11), to the Enlightenment's secularizing darkness (v12). Verse 13 pivots to the final three, more severe woes. Throughout, the "third part" signals judgment proportional and partial — merciful warning, not final annihilation — while the underlying OT pattern is consistently the plagues of Egypt: God hearing his people's cry and judging the oppressor.
