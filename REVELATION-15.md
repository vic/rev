## Revelation 15

### [Rev 15:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-1.htm) — "And I saw another sign in heaven, great and marvellous..."

**Text**: "And I saw another sign in heaven, great and marvellous, seven angels having the seven last plagues; for in them is filled up the wrath of God."

**Exegesis**: The Greek *semeion* ("sign") echoes the same word used in 12:1 (the woman clothed with the sun) — a striking visual scene that arrests attention and demands interpretation, not merely a miracle. The qualifier "another" links this sign to that earlier cosmic sequence. "Great and marvellous" (*mega kai thaumaston*) is an OT doxological formula applied here to an act of judgment, signaling that what follows carries the full weight of divine glory.

The seven angels are most likely the same seven angels who blew the seven trumpets (chs. 8–9). In the trumpet sequence they administered preliminary, warning judgments designed to call sinners to repentance — partial, mercy-tempered judgments. Now they receive bowls. The identification as the *last* plagues is theologically decisive: the Greek *eschatas* marks these as final and terminal. The wrath of God (*thumos tou Theou*) — *thumos* denoting passionate, furious indignation (cf. 14:10) — is here said to be *completed* (*etelesthē*, aorist passive). This is not God's general displeasure but his concentrated, unmixed eschatological wrath poured out when probation has closed and mercy is withdrawn. The sins of humanity have reached their full measure. This verse serves as the thematic caption for the entire ch. 15–16 unit.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 12:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-1.htm) (semeion); [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm) (thumos); Rev. 8–9 (trumpet angels); [Rev. 6:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-16.htm)–17 (great day of wrath).

### [Rev 15:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-2.htm) — "And I saw as it were a sea of glass mingled with fire..."

**Text**: "And I saw as it were a sea of glass mingled with fire: and them that had gotten the victory over the beast, and over his image, and over his mark, and over the number of his name, stand on the sea of glass, having the harps of God."

**Exegesis**: The sea of glass first appeared in 4:6, spread before the heavenly throne, clear as crystal (*hōs hyalos homoios krystallō*). Now it is *mixed with fire* — reflecting the lightning and the seven torches of fire before the throne (4:5), heightening the dazzling splendor of the scene. The fire also signals proximity to divine judgment; the redeemed stand on the very threshold of God's presence as the plagues are about to fall.

The victors are defined by a triple conquest: over the beast (the sea beast of ch. 13), over his image, and over his mark/number of his name — the full apparatus of the Antichrist's coercive worship system. The verb "had gotten the victory" (*nikōntas*) is a present participle in the best Greek manuscripts, stressing ongoing, active overcoming — these saints conquered even as the final crisis raged. Stefanovic notes this corresponds to [Rev. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-9.htm)–15 where the redeemed from the great tribulation stand before the throne in the heavenly temple.

The *harps of God* (*kitharas tou Theou*) are instruments of liturgical praise (cf. 5:8; 14:2). The scene is a heavenly throne-room celebration that temporally precedes the outpouring of the plagues — the positive element (redemption) comes before the negative element (judgment), inverting the sequence of the Exodus typology where celebration followed deliverance.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-5.htm)–6 (sea of glass, torches); [Rev. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-9.htm)–15 (tribulation saints before throne); [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm)–18 (beast, image, mark); [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm); 14:2 (harps).

### [Rev 15:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-3.htm) — "And they sing the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb..."

**Text**: "And they sing the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb, saying, Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints."

**Exegesis**: The designation "song of Moses" (*ōdē Mōyseōs*) is a direct allusion to Exodus 15, the victory hymn sung by Israel at the Red Sea after the destruction of Pharaoh's army. Moses is here given his OT title "servant of God" (*doulos tou Theou*), marking the parallelism between OT and NT economies of salvation. The pairing as "the song of Moses and of the Lamb" implies a single unified song — not two separate compositions — celebrating the same divine deliverer across salvation history: what Moses celebrated typologically, the Lamb accomplished eschatologically.

Stefanovic observes the critical structural inversion: in Exodus the song follows the plagues and liberation; in Revelation 15, the song precedes the seven last plagues. This is deliberate — it demonstrates that the redeemed have already been sealed and secured before divine wrath falls. Their singing is a proleptic celebration of completed victory.

The song itself is an OT mosaic — almost every phrase is drawn from Hebrew Scripture:
- "Great and marvellous are your works" ← [Ps. 111:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/111-2.htm)–3
- "Righteous and true are your ways" ← [Deut. 32:4](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-4.htm); [Ps. 145:17](https://biblehub.com/psalms/145-17.htm)
- "King of the nations" (*basileus tōn ethnōn*) ← [Jer. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-6.htm)–7 (KJV: "King of saints" reflects a textual variant; best manuscripts read "King of the nations")

The title "Lord God Almighty" (*Kyrios ho Theos ho Pantokratōr*) is the covenant name of God (see Notes on 1:8), here invoked in the context of final judgment. The redeemed confess that the *ways* of God — his providential governance of history, including the apparent abandonment of his people to persecution — have been *righteous and true* all along. This is a theodicy sung by survivors.

**Cross-references**: Exod. 15 (song of Moses); [Deut. 32:4](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/32-4.htm) (righteous ways); [Ps. 111:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/111-2.htm)–3; [Ps. 145:17](https://biblehub.com/psalms/145-17.htm); [Jer. 10:6](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-6.htm)–7; [Rev. 1:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-8.htm) (Pantokratōr); [Rev. 14:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-3.htm) (only redeemed can sing this song).

### [Rev 15:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-4.htm) — "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name?..."

**Text**: "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name? for thou only art holy: for all nations shall come and worship before thee; for thy judgments are made manifest."

**Exegesis**: The song continues with a rhetorical question (*tis ou mē phobēthē*) drawn directly from [Jer. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-7.htm) — "Who would not fear you, O King of the nations?" — now adapted to the eschatological moment. The question expects no negative answer: God's judgments have been so visibly and undeniably manifested that universal reverence is the only rational response.

"For thou only art holy" (*hoti monos hosios*) — *hosios* (holy, pure, devout) here underscores God's absolute moral uniqueness. Only he possesses inherent, underived holiness. This phrase alludes to [1 Sam. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/1_samuel/2-2.htm) ("There is none holy as the LORD").

"All nations shall come and worship before thee" ← [Ps. 86:9](https://biblehub.com/psalms/86-9.htm); [Jer. 16:19](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/16-19.htm). This is an eschatological universalism of judgment, not necessarily universal salvation. The nations come to bow before God because his *righteous acts* (*dikaiōmata*) have been made openly manifest — the hidden providences of history are now transparent. Stefanovic connects this directly to 14:7 (the first angel's call to "fear God and give him glory, for the hour of his judgment has come"). The redeemed saints have internalized and embodied the eternal gospel message; their song is the fulfillment of what the three angels proclaimed. Because they accepted this message, separated from Babylon, and obeyed God's commandments, "they do not receive of her plagues" (18:4).

**Cross-references**: [Jer. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/10-7.htm) (fear and glorify); [1 Sam. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/1_samuel/2-2.htm) (only holy); [Ps. 86:9](https://biblehub.com/psalms/86-9.htm); [Jer. 16:19](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/16-19.htm) (nations worship); [Rev. 14:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-7.htm) (first angel's message); [Rev. 18:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-4.htm) (do not receive her plagues).

### [Rev 15:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-5.htm) — "And after that I looked, and, behold, the temple of the tabernacle of the testimony in heaven was opened"

**Text**: "And after that I looked, and, behold, the temple of the tabernacle of the testimony in heaven was opened:"

**Exegesis**: The scene shifts from the victorious saints to the heavenly sanctuary. The Greek *naos* is used here — not *hieron* (the outer temple precinct) but the innermost sanctuary, specifically the most holy place where God's throne is located. Stefanovic consistently identifies this as the most holy place of the heavenly temple throughout the commentary.

The compound title "temple of the tabernacle of the testimony" (*ho naos tēs skēnēs tou martyriou*) is unique and theologically loaded. It identifies the heavenly sanctuary with the Mosaic *Mishkan* (Tabernacle) and specifically with the function of the *tabernacle of the testimony* — the OT designation for the tabernacle as the dwelling place of the ark containing the law-tablets, the *eduth* (testimony/law). This phrase emphasizes the law of God as the standard of judgment: the seven angels come from the place where God's law resides, "to show that no person or nation can with impunity defy the Law of God" (Stefanovic). The opening of this sanctuary initiates the judicial phase — the outgoing of avenging commissioners from the very presence of God.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm) (naos); [Rev. 11:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-19.htm) (ark in the temple); [Exod. 25:22](https://biblehub.com/exodus/25-22.htm); 38:21 (tabernacle of testimony); [Num. 1:50](https://biblehub.com/numbers/1-50.htm) (tabernacle of testimony); [Rev. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-6.htm) (throne room).

### [Rev 15:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-6.htm) — "And the seven angels came out of the temple, having the seven plagues, clothed in pure and white linen, and having their breasts girded with golden girdles"

**Text**: "And the seven angels came out of the temple, having the seven plagues, clothed in pure and white linen, and having their breasts girded with golden girdles."

**Exegesis**: The seven plague-bearing angels emerge from the most holy place — from the immediate presence of God. They come as his avenging commissioners, executing righteous wrath in direct response to the prayers of his people (cf. 8:3–5, where the altar of incense connects the prayers of the saints to the judgment sequence).

Their vesture is priestly and royal: "pure and shiny linen" (*linon katharon lampron*) and "golden girdles" (*zōnas chrysas*) around their breasts — not the waist, but the chest. Stefanovic notes this attire precisely mirrors the description of the glorified Christ in 1:13 (*endedymenon podērē kai periezōsmenon pros tois mastois zōnēn chrysēn*). The angels' clothing is thus christological: they come with the authority of the exalted Christ who has commissioned them. They are not independent agents of destruction but delegated executors of Christ's judicial authority.

The linen (*linon*, not *byssinos*) in some manuscripts connects to priestly vestments (cf. Lev. 16 — the high priest's Day of Atonement linen garments), reinforcing the sanctuary context. The golden girdles recall both the OT priestly regalia and Christ's own appearance to John at Patmos.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-13.htm) (glorified Christ); [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–5 (prayers of saints and judgment); [Lev. 16:4](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/16-4.htm) (priestly linen); [Rev. 19:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-8.htm) (fine linen as righteousness of saints).

### [Rev 15:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-7.htm) — "And one of the four beasts gave unto the seven angels seven golden vials full of the wrath of God, who liveth for ever and ever"

**Text**: "And one of the four beasts gave unto the seven angels seven golden vials full of the wrath of God, who liveth for ever and ever."

**Exegesis**: The "four beasts" (KJV) are the four living beings (*tessara zōa*) of ch. 4 — an order of exalted angels closely associated with the divine throne, present at the opening of every seal (6:1, 3, 5, 7) and now at the commissioning of the plague-bowls. Their role throughout Revelation has been to initiate and oversee the outworking of divine judgment and providence. One of them — the text does not specify which — distributes the seven golden *phialai* (bowls, KJV: "vials").

The *phialai chrysai* ("golden bowls") are the shallow, wide libation/offering vessels used in the OT temple cultus ([1 Kings 7:40](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/7-40.htm), 45, 50; [2 Kings 12:13](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/12-13.htm); 25:15). In [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm), the same golden bowls held *incense*, identified as "the prayers of the saints." Now those same liturgical vessels — transformed from instruments of intercession to instruments of destruction — are filled with God's wrath (*thumos*). Stefanovic draws the connection explicitly: "The golden bowls of intercession become the instruments of destruction, full of the wrath of God to be poured out on the wicked. Mercy has ceased for those on whom the divine wrath will be executed. The execution of the wrath of God has been initiated by the prayers of God's faithful people."

The phrase "who liveth for ever and ever" (*tō zōnti eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn*) is a doxological formula asserting God's eternal, self-existent life — the wrath that falls is not arbitrary but the measured response of the eternal, living God of covenant.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-6.htm)–8 (four living beings); [Rev. 6:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-1.htm), 3, 5, 7 (living beings and seals); [Rev. 5:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-8.htm) (golden bowls of incense/prayers); [1 Kings 7:40](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/7-40.htm)–50 (temple phialai); [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm) (cup of wrath); [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–5 (prayers→judgment).

### [Rev 15:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-8.htm) — "And the temple was filled with smoke from the glory of God, and from his power; and no man was able to enter into the temple, till the seven plagues of the seven angels were fulfilled"

**Text**: "And the temple was filled with smoke from the glory of God, and from his power; and no man was able to enter into the temple, till the seven plagues of the seven angels were fulfilled."

**Exegesis**: This is the theological climax of chapter 15 and one of the most solemn statements in all of Revelation. The heavenly temple — the very place where intercession for humanity has been made throughout salvation history — is now inaccessible, filled with the smoke (*kapnos*) of God's glory and power.

The OT background is twofold and precise:
1. **Dedication of the Mosaic tabernacle** ([Exod. 40:34](https://biblehub.com/exodus/40-34.htm)–35): "The cloud covered the tent of meeting, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle. And Moses was not able to enter the tent of meeting because the cloud settled on it, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle."
2. **Dedication of Solomon's temple** ([1 Kings 8:10](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/8-10.htm)–11): "The cloud filled the house of the LORD, so that the priests could not stand to minister because of the cloud, for the glory of the LORD filled the house of the LORD."

On both OT occasions, the filling of the sanctuary with divine glory *suspended the services* — the priests could not function, the mediatorial office was momentarily halted. [Revelation 15:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-8.htm) applies this typology eschatologically: the suspension is now final and irrevocable for the duration of the plagues. *No one* (*oudeis*) can enter until the seven plagues are completed.

The theological meaning is unambiguous: **probation has closed**. Intercession on behalf of sinners no longer exists. The heavenly mediatorial ministry of Christ — the continuous intercession before the Father that has characterized the entire Christian era ([Heb. 7:25](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/7-25.htm); [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–5) — is now concluded for those who have rejected the gospel. What remains for the impenitent is the fullness of divine wrath "unmixed with mercy and grace" (cf. 14:10: *kekrasmenon akratou* — poured unmixed into the cup of his anger). This verse thus functions as the eschatological counterpart to the close of the Day of Atonement in Leviticus 16 — when the high priest completed his work in the most holy place and emerged, the fate of each individual was sealed.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 40:34](https://biblehub.com/exodus/40-34.htm)–35 (glory fills tabernacle); [1 Kings 8:10](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/8-10.htm)–11 (glory fills Solomon's temple); [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm) (wrath unmixed with mercy); [Heb. 7:25](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/7-25.htm) (Christ's intercession); [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–5 (incense/prayers before throne); Lev. 16 (Day of Atonement); [Rev. 22:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-11.htm) ("let him be unjust still").

## Summary of Chapter 15's Structure and Theology

Chapter 15 functions as the **vestibule** to the seven last plagues — theologically preparing the reader before a single bowl is poured. It has a two-movement structure:

**Movement 1 (vv. 1–4) — The Victors**: A proleptic vision of the redeemed already secure before the plagues fall. They stand on the crystalline-fiery sea of glass, victors over the beast-system, holding harps, singing the Exodus song now christologically reinterpreted. The song establishes: God's works are great, his ways are righteous, he alone is holy, and universal acknowledgment of his sovereignty is inevitable. This section is simultaneously the conclusion of chs. 12–14 and the springboard into 15:5–16:21.

**Movement 2 (vv. 5–8) — The Temple**: The most holy place opens; seven priestly-Christlike angels emerge as delegated executors; the four living beings distribute bowls that were once incense-holders (prayers) but are now wrath-vessels; the temple fills with glory-smoke, shutting out all intercession. The judicial machinery is set. Probation is closed.

The chapter's controlling typology is the **Exodus**: Red Sea / sea of glass; song of Moses / song of the Lamb; Egyptian plagues / seven last plagues; Pharaoh / the beast. But the anti-type exceeds the type: the song precedes rather than follows the plagues, the sea of glass is before God's own throne, and the deliverance is cosmic and final rather than national and temporal.
