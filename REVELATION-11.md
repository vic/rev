## Revelation 11

### [Rev 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm) — "And there was given me a reed like unto a rod"

**Text**: "And there was given me a reed like unto a rod: and the angel stood, saying, Rise, and measure the temple of God, and the altar, and them that worship therein."

**Exegesis**: The *kalamos* ("reed") was a hollow-stalked plant from the Jordan valley, used as a measuring rod reaching over ten feet. The passive construction "was given" (*edothē*) signals divine commissioning — this act of measuring is authorized by God. The Greek *metreō* ("measure") carries judicial overtones: in [Matt. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/7-2.htm) and [Mark 4:24](https://biblehub.com/mark/4-24.htm) it relates to divine judgment in the Last Judgment; in [2 Sam. 8:2](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/8-2.htm) David measured the Moabites to determine who would live and die. The measuring is therefore not architectural but judicial — an evaluation, a separation of those who belong to God from those who do not.

Three objects are measured: the *naos* (temple/sanctuary proper), the altar, and the worshipers. Kenneth Strand demonstrates that the only OT passage where these three elements appear together is Lev. 16 — the Day of Atonement ritual — where atonement is made for the priests, the sanctuary, the altar, and the congregation. Ezekiel's temple vision (Ezek. 40–42) provides additional background: the angel measured the temple on the tenth day of the first month (= the Day of Atonement) as a prelude to restoration. In both Ezekiel and Leviticus, the sequence runs: temple → altar → people, precisely the order in [Rev. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm).

The Greek *naos* (distinct from *hieron*, which denotes the entire temple complex including courts and property) designates only the innermost sanctuary — the Most Holy Place. John uses *naos* consistently throughout Revelation to refer to the heavenly temple (3:12; 7:15; 11:19; 14:17; 15:5–8; 16:1, 17). This is not a rebuilt Jerusalem temple (destroyed ca. A.D. 70, before Revelation was written c. A.D. 90), nor a symbol for the church (John consistently distinguishes the temple from God's people). It is the heavenly sanctuary from which God governs the universe.

The altar measured here is the golden altar of incense (not the brazen altar of burnt offering, which was in the outer court John is told not to measure). This is the same altar as [Rev. 8:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-3.htm)–6, associated with the saints' prayers ascending before God.

The measuring of the worshipers connects to the sealing of [Rev. 7:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-1.htm)–4: just as sealing preceded the seventh seal, measuring precedes the seventh trumpet. Both acts portray God's preparation of his people for the final tribulation. The Day of Atonement atmosphere is explicit: that day involved final separation — those who afflicted themselves were preserved, those who did not were cut off ([Lev. 23:28](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/23-28.htm)–29).

**Cross-references**: [Lev. 16:16](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/16-16.htm)–19, 30–31; 23:28–29; Ezek. 40–42; 43:7–11; 44:9; [2 Sam. 8:2](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/8-2.htm); [2 Cor. 10:12](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/10-12.htm); [Matt. 7:2](https://biblehub.com/matthew/7-2.htm); [Rev. 7:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-1.htm)–4; 8:3–6; 21:15–17.

### [Rev 11:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-2.htm) — "But the court which is without the temple leave out"

**Text**: "But the court which is without the temple leave out, and measure it not; for it is given unto the Gentiles: and the holy city shall they tread under foot forty and two months."

**Exegesis**: The Greek *ekballō exōthen* ("throw out," "cast out") is the same word used in [John 9:34](https://biblehub.com/john/9-34.htm)–35 for the excommunication of the blind man from the synagogue. The outer court is not merely left behind — it is actively excluded, signifying that its inhabitants have no part in God's kingdom. The outer court of Jerusalem's temple was the Court of the Gentiles: Gentiles could enter it but were barred from the inner court under penalty of death. Here it represents forces hostile to God, "those who dwell on the earth" ([Rev. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-10.htm); 6:10; 8:13; 11:10; 13:8) — the wicked who profess connection with Christianity but are apostate.

The Greek *ethnoi* ("nations" or "Gentiles") in Revelation consistently designates powers hostile to God and his people (11:2, 18; 14:8; 16:19; 18:3; 19:15; 20:3, 8). The verb *pateō* ("to trample") is used figuratively in 14:20 and 19:15 for the trampling of the wicked under divine wrath, and in the OT for enemy oppression of God's people ([Isa. 63:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/63-18.htm); [Jer. 12:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/12-10.htm)). Daniel's little horn "trampled" the holy place for "a time, times, and half a time" ([Dan. 7:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-7.htm), 19, 23, 25; 8:9–13). Jesus prophesied Jerusalem would be "trampled under foot by the Gentiles, until the times of the Gentiles be fulfilled" ([Luke 21:24](https://biblehub.com/luke/21-24.htm)) — the prototype behind [Rev. 11:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-2.htm).

The "forty-two months" appears only here and in 13:5 (for the sea beast's activities). It is the same period as the 1,260 days of v. 3 and 12:6, and "a time and times and half a time" of 12:14 — derived from [Dan. 7:24](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-24.htm)–25 and 12:7, where it exclusively designates the period of the little horn's persecution of the saints. Crucially: in Revelation, *forty-two months* is always associated with the *wicked* and their dominion; *1,260 days* is always associated with God's *faithful people* and their witness under protection. The most plausible interpretation is not a literal 3.5 years, but the prophetic period of approximately 1,260 years (the Middle Ages), during which the church endured oppression and the Bible was held in obscurity — though Stefanovic cautions against dogmatic date-fixing. The designation also has qualitative dimensions: it recalls Elijah's 3.5-year witness during Jezebel's apostasy (1 Kings 17–18; [Luke 4:25](https://biblehub.com/luke/4-25.htm); [James 5:17](https://biblehub.com/james/5-17.htm)) and typologically mirrors Jesus' 3.5-year ministry in "sackcloth" of rejection.

The "holy city" being trampled parallels the "great city" of v. 8 in antithesis: the holy city represents God's persecuted people; the great city their oppressors. The sea beast of [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm)–10 is given forty-two months (13:5) — the same period — confirming that the trampling of the holy city is identical to the beast's persecution of the saints.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 7:7](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-7.htm), 19, 23, 25; 8:9–13; 12:7; [Luke 21:24](https://biblehub.com/luke/21-24.htm); [Rev. 12:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-6.htm), 14; 13:1–10; 13:5; [Isa. 63:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/63-18.htm); [Jer. 12:10](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/12-10.htm); [Lev. 23:28](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/23-28.htm)–29; [Ezek. 44:9](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/44-9.htm).

### [Rev 11:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-3.htm) — "And I will give power unto my two witnesses"

**Text**: "And I will give power unto my two witnesses, and they shall prophesy a thousand two hundred and threescore days, clothed in sackcloth."

**Exegesis**: The literal Greek is *dōsō* ("I will give"), a Hebraic idiom meaning "I will commission." The divine "I" speaking here (parallel to 10:11) is God or Christ: the witnesses are given divine authority and power. *Martus* (Gr.) means both "witness" and "martyr," foreshadowing the witnesses' fate.

Identity of the two witnesses: Stefanovic surveys the options — Law and Prophets, Moses and Elijah, two last-days prophets — and concludes the evidence supports a twofold identification: (1) **the people of God** in their royal and priestly roles ([Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:10), bearing prophetic witness to the apostate world; and (2) **the Bible** (Old and New Testaments) as "the word of God and the testimony of Jesus" ([Rev. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-2.htm), 9). These are not mutually exclusive: it is through the church's preaching that the Word is manifested. The commission of 10:11 — to prophesy to "many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings" — is here extended to the church. Jesus sent disciples two by two as witnesses ([Mark 6:7](https://biblehub.com/mark/6-7.htm); [Luke 10:1](https://biblehub.com/luke/10-1.htm)); OT law required two witnesses for testimony to be valid ([Deut. 19:15](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/19-15.htm); [John 8:17](https://biblehub.com/john/8-17.htm); [Heb. 10:28](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/10-28.htm)).

The 1,260 days = forty-two months = time, times, and half a time. While the forty-two months is the period allotted to the wicked, 1,260 days designates the period assigned to God's people: persecuted yet under special divine protection as they bear witness. The sackcloth garb is the OT attire of prophets in their prophetic office ([Isa. 20:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/20-2.htm); [Zech. 13:4](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/13-4.htm); cf. [Matt. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/matthew/3-4.htm) — John the Baptist), signifying mourning, humiliation, and the bitterness of the prophetic calling in a hostile world (cf. [Rev. 10:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-9.htm)–10, the bittersweet scroll). The 1,260 days begins when the 42 months of Gentile trampling begins — both periods are concurrent.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-2.htm), 9; 10:11; 12:6; [Deut. 19:15](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/19-15.htm); [John 5:39](https://biblehub.com/john/5-39.htm); [Acts 1:8](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-8.htm); [Luke 4:25](https://biblehub.com/luke/4-25.htm); [James 5:17](https://biblehub.com/james/5-17.htm); [Isa. 20:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/20-2.htm); [Zech. 13:4](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/13-4.htm); [Matt. 3:4](https://biblehub.com/matthew/3-4.htm).

### [Rev 11:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-4.htm) — "These are the two olive trees, and the two candlesticks"

**Text**: "These are the two olive trees, and the two candlesticks standing before the God of the earth."

**Exegesis**: This verse is a direct allusion to Zechariah 4. Zechariah saw a lampstand with seven lamps flanked by two olive trees with two branches. The interpreting angel identified them as "the two anointed ones, who are standing by the Lord of the whole earth" (*Kuriou pasēs tēs gēs*, [Zech. 4:14](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/4-14.htm)) — Joshua the high priest and Zerubbabel the governor, the two leaders overseeing the post-exilic restoration of the temple. Their work was accomplished "not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" ([Zech. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/4-6.htm)). John takes the imagery further: while Zechariah had one lampstand and two olive trees, John's witnesses are *both* the olive trees *and* the lampstands (plural) — incorporating both images to represent both the priestly role (olive oil for anointing and lamps) and the kingly/prophetic role.

The identification of the witnesses with both olive trees and lampstands indicates their function: to supply light (testimony/gospel) continuously, empowered by the Spirit (the oil). Just as Joshua and Zerubbabel operated in priestly and royal capacities to restore the Jerusalem temple, the two witnesses bear the gospel with reference to the restoration of the heavenly sanctuary (cf. vv. 1–2). The lamp/candlestick imagery recalls [Rev. 1:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-12.htm)–20, where the seven lampstands are the seven churches — the witnessing people of God. The title "Lord of the earth" (*ho Kurios tēs gēs*) underscores that despite appearances, the earth belongs to God, not the beast.

**Cross-references**: [Zech. 4:2](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/4-2.htm)–6, 11–14; [Rev. 1:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-12.htm), 20; 5:6; 1:6; 5:10.

### [Rev 11:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-5.htm) — "And if any man will hurt them, fire proceedeth out of their mouth"

**Text**: "And if any man will hurt them, fire proceedeth out of their mouths, and devoureth their enemies: and if any man will hurt them, he must in this manner be killed."

**Exegesis**: The doubled condition ("if any man will hurt them... if any man will hurt them") intensifies the warning: any attempt to harm the witnesses triggers automatic, inescapable judgment. Fire from the mouth recalls two OT precedents: (1) Elijah calling down fire from heaven on the soldiers sent by King Ahaziah to arrest him ([2 Kings 1:9](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/1-9.htm)–14); (2) God's word becoming fire in Jeremiah's mouth devouring the rebellious people ([Jer. 5:14](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/5-14.htm)). On another level, fire from the mouth represents the Word of God — the gospel proclamation — which carries the power of condemnation upon those who reject it. In [Rev. 19:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-15.htm), 21, the sword from Christ's mouth is the instrument of judgment; the same dynamic applies here.

The inverted law of retribution operates: those who attempt to destroy the witnesses are destroyed by the same means — the Word they tried to suppress. This is divine protection during the witnesses' prophetic mission: the power that brought plagues on Egypt and shut heaven in Elijah's day now protects the church in its gospel witness. The witnesses cannot be permanently silenced until they have "finished their testimony" (v. 7).

**Cross-references**: [2 Kings 1:9](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/1-9.htm)–14; [Jer. 5:14](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/5-14.htm); [Rev. 19:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-15.htm), 21; 2:16; [Num. 16:35](https://biblehub.com/numbers/16-35.htm).

### [Rev 11:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-6.htm) — "These have power to shut heaven, that it rain not"

**Text**: "These have power to shut heaven, that it rain not in the days of their prophecy: and have power over waters to turn them to blood, and to smite the earth with all plagues, as often as they will."

**Exegesis**: The two sets of powers map directly onto the two greatest OT prophets: **Elijah** and **Moses**. (1) Elijah shut heaven so it would not rain during his three-and-a-half-year prophetic witness against Ahab's apostasy ([1 Kings 17:1](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/17-1.htm); cf. [Luke 4:25](https://biblehub.com/luke/4-25.htm); [James 5:17](https://biblehub.com/james/5-17.htm)). The drought was the divine judgment accompanying rejection of God's word. (2) Moses turned waters to blood and smote Egypt with every plague when Pharaoh refused to release Israel (Exod. 7–11). Both prophets met Jesus at the Transfiguration and discussed his coming "exodus" in Jerusalem ([Luke 9:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/9-31.htm)) — a typological convergence. Malachi predicted Elijah's return before the Day of the Lord ([Mal. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-5.htm)–6).

The authority "to strike the earth with every plague *as often as they want*" is remarkable: it is unlimited within the period of their prophesying. This represents the inherent power of the Word of God: its proclamation always brings judgment upon those who persistently reject it. This should not be understood as Moses and Elijah literally returning, but as symbolic figures whose witnessing activity is modeled on these two paradigmatic prophets. The same divine Spirit that worked through Moses, Elijah, Joshua, and Zerubbabel accompanies the two witnesses throughout the 1,260 days. The phrase "as often as they want" (*hosakis ean thelēsōsin*) underlines that the witnesses are not passive; they act with divine authority.

**Cross-references**: [1 Kings 17:1](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/17-1.htm); Exod. 7–11; [Luke 4:25](https://biblehub.com/luke/4-25.htm); 9:31; [James 5:17](https://biblehub.com/james/5-17.htm); [Mal. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/malachi/4-5.htm)–6; [Jer. 5:14](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/5-14.htm); [Zech. 4:6](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/4-6.htm).

### [Rev 11:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-7.htm) — "And when they shall have finished their testimony, the beast that ascendeth out of the bottomless pit"

**Text**: "And when they shall have finished their testimony, the beast that ascendeth out of the bottomless pit shall make war against them, and shall overcome them, and kill them."

**Exegesis**: The crucial temporal marker: *"when they shall have finished their testimony"* — the beast is *not* able to attack until the witnesses have completed the full 1,260 days. The witnesses cannot be permanently destroyed; their death only occurs after their mission is complete. This models the pattern of Jesus: he could not be taken until his "hour" had come ([John 7:30](https://biblehub.com/john/7-30.htm); 13:1).

"The beast (*thērion*) ascending from the abyss" (*abussou*) appears again in 17:8 and is related to — though not identical with — the sea beast of 13:1–10. The abyss (*abussos*) is the abode of fallen angels ([Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm); [2 Pet. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-4.htm); Jude 6) and demonic powers ([Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm)–11, the fifth trumpet locusts). This beast's demonic origin — from the abyss — signals Satanic backing. The beast "wages war" (*polemon poiēsei*) and "overcomes" (*nikēsei*) — language drawn from [Dan. 7:21](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-21.htm), where the little horn "prevailed against the saints," and echoed in [Rev. 13:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-7.htm). The sea beast of Rev. 13 similarly overcomes the saints during forty-two months; the beast from the abyss of Rev. 11 attacks *after* that period, suggesting a sequence.

SDA interpretation has identified this beast as the French Revolution — the great anti-religious, atheistic movement that attacked the Bible and Christianity in the late 18th century, which followed immediately after the prophetic 1,260 years. The death of the witnesses on this reading = the suppression of the Bible in France. Stefanovic accepts this as plausible for the witnesses-as-Bible interpretation but notes it is insufficient if the witnesses represent God's people more broadly: the books of Daniel and Revelation never restrict the beast's attack to the Bible as a book; it always extends to the saints themselves ([Dan. 7:21](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-21.htm); [Rev. 12:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-17.htm); 13:7).

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 7:21](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-21.htm); [Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm)–11; 13:1–10; 13:7; 17:8; [Luke 8:31](https://biblehub.com/luke/8-31.htm); [2 Pet. 2:4](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-4.htm); Jude 6.

### [Rev 11:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-8.htm) — "And their dead bodies shall lie in the street of the great city"

**Text**: "And their dead bodies shall lie in the street of the great city, which spiritually is called Sodom and Egypt, where also our Lord was crucified."

**Exegesis**: The Greek *to ptōma autōn* is a collective singular — "the dead body of them" — highlighting the corporate unity of the witnesses: they are one witnessing body of Christ (G. K. Beale), many scattered throughout the earth yet forming one body. The witnesses who bore one unified testimony are one in death.

Denial of burial in the ancient Near East was the ultimate disgrace — a deliberate desecration ([1 Kings 13:22](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/13-22.htm); [Ps. 79:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/79-3.htm); [Jer. 8:1](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/8-1.htm)–2; 14:16). The exposure of their corpses on the public street is a declaration of total humiliation and apparent final defeat. The world believes it has permanently silenced the voice of the witnesses.

"The great city" (*hē polis hē megalē*) is Revelation's consistent designation for the power opposing God (14:8; 16:19; 17:18; 18:10, 16, 19, 21 — identified as Babylon). It is the anti-type of the "holy city" of v. 2. It is given three symbolic designations: (1) **Sodom** — total moral degradation and all-inclusive sin ([Gen. 18:20](https://biblehub.com/genesis/18-20.htm)–21; 19:4–11); the prophets equated apostate Jerusalem with Sodom ([Isa. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-9.htm)–10; 3:9; [Jer. 23:14](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/23-14.htm); [Ezek. 16:48](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-48.htm)–58). (2) **Egypt** — arrogance, atheistic self-sufficiency, and oppression of God's people; exemplified in Pharaoh's declaration "Who is the Lord? I do not know the Lord" ([Exod. 5:2](https://biblehub.com/exodus/5-2.htm); cf. [Ezek. 32:12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/32-12.htm)). (3) **Where also our Lord was crucified** — Jerusalem, where Jesus was put to death. What happens to the witnesses recapitulates what happened to their Lord.

The great city thus synthesizes the wickedness of Sodom, the godless arrogance of Egypt, and the covenant-breaking apostasy of Jerusalem. It represents any power structure — religious or political — that persecutes God's witnesses. That it is called this "spiritually" (*pneumatikōs*) signals symbolic, not literal, interpretation.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 18:20](https://biblehub.com/genesis/18-20.htm)–21; 19:4–11; [Exod. 5:2](https://biblehub.com/exodus/5-2.htm); [Isa. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/1-9.htm)–10; 3:9; [Jer. 23:14](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/23-14.htm); [Ezek. 16:48](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/16-48.htm)–58; 32:12; [Amos 4:10](https://biblehub.com/amos/4-10.htm)–11; [1 Kings 13:22](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/13-22.htm); [Ps. 79:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/79-3.htm); [Rev. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-8.htm); 16:19; 17:18; 18.

### [Rev 11:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-9.htm) — "And they of the people and kindreds and tongues and nations shall see their dead bodies"

**Text**: "And they of the people and kindreds and tongues and nations shall see their dead bodies three days and an half, and shall not suffer their dead bodies to be put in graves."

**Exegesis**: "Peoples and tribes and tongues and nations" — the fourfold universal formula appears repeatedly in Revelation (5:9; 7:9; 10:11; 11:9; 13:7; 14:6). Here it is the same audience to whom John was commissioned to prophesy in 10:11 — now as spectators gloating over the witnesses' apparent defeat. The global scope of the formula indicates that the rejection of the witnesses' testimony is worldwide, not local.

"Three and a half days" mirrors the 3.5 years (1,260 days / 42 months) of witnessing: a day-for-a-year correspondence. Three and a half days is also the duration Jesus spent in the tomb — making the resurrection of the witnesses in v. 11 a deliberate Christological parallel. The refusal of burial (*ouk aphiousin tethēnai*) extends the humiliation: not only are the witnesses killed, they are denied the basic honor of interment. This was the most shameful treatment an enemy could inflict, confirming in the eyes of their opponents that the witnesses' cause is utterly finished.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 5:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-9.htm); 7:9; 10:11; 13:7; 14:6; cf. [Ps. 79:3](https://biblehub.com/psalms/79-3.htm); [Jer. 14:16](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/14-16.htm).

### [Rev 11:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-10.htm) — "And they that dwell upon the earth shall rejoice over them"

**Text**: "And they that dwell upon the earth shall rejoice over them, and make merry, and shall send gifts one to another; because these two prophets tormented them that dwelt on the earth."

**Exegesis**: "Those who dwell upon the earth" (*hoi katoikountes epi tēs gēs*) is Revelation's technical designation for the wicked: those whose allegiance is to the world rather than heaven ([Rev. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-10.htm); 6:10; 8:13; 13:8, 14; 17:2). The celebration is threefold: rejoicing (*chairousin*), making merry (*euphrainthēsontai*), and sending gifts (*dōra pempsousin allēlois*). The exchange of gifts recalls the Jewish festival of Purim, when Jews celebrated their deliverance by "rejoicing and sending portions of food to one another and gifts to the poor" ([Esther 9:22](https://biblehub.com/esther/9-22.htm); [Neh. 8:10](https://biblehub.com/nehemiah/8-10.htm)–12) — but here it is an ironic, inverted Purim: the world celebrates the apparent end of God's people.

The reason for the celebration: "these two prophets *tormented* (*ebasanisan*) those who dwell on the earth." *Basanizō* is normally used for torment from demonic or divine sources ([Rev. 9:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-5.htm); 11:10; 14:10; 20:10). The gospel always "torments" the consciences of those who hear it without surrendering to it. Elijah was called "the troubler of Israel" by Ahab ([1 Kings 18:17](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/18-17.htm); 21:20); Ahab hated Micah because he prophesied evil rather than good ([1 Kings 22:8](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/22-8.htm), 18). Paul's preaching on "righteousness, self-control, and judgment" terrified Felix ([Acts 26:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/26-25.htm)). The Word of God "brings torment to those who hear it without surrendering to it" (Desmond Ford). The world's celebration reveals the inverse relationship: the absence of prophetic testimony is perceived as relief.

**Cross-references**: [Esther 9:22](https://biblehub.com/esther/9-22.htm); [Neh. 8:10](https://biblehub.com/nehemiah/8-10.htm)–12; [1 Kings 18:17](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/18-17.htm); 21:20; 22:8; [Acts 26:25](https://biblehub.com/acts/26-25.htm); [Rev. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-10.htm); 6:10; 13:8; [Mark 13:13](https://biblehub.com/mark/13-13.htm).

### [Rev 11:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-11.htm) — "And after three days and an half the spirit of life from God entered into them"

**Text**: "And after three days and an half the spirit of life from God entered into them, and they stood upon their feet; and great fear fell upon them which saw them."

**Exegesis**: The phrase "spirit of life from God" (*pneuma zōēs ek tou theou*) alludes directly to [Gen. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-7.htm) — God breathing life into Adam — signaling that only God himself can bring the witnesses back to life. This resurrection is also modeled on Ezekiel's vision of the valley of dry bones ([Ezek. 37:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-1.htm)–10): God commanded the breath (*rûach*) to enter the slain; "they came to life and stood on their feet, a vast army." Ezekiel's vision was the prophecy of Israel's restoration from Babylonian exile — when the world had written off God's people as finished. The application is identical: whenever the world believes it has permanently silenced the faithful witnesses, God vindicates them.

"They stood upon their feet" — the same posture as Ezekiel's dry bones ([Ezek. 37:10](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-10.htm)): resurrection from total defeat to standing upright. The "great fear" (*phobos megas*) that falls on the spectators is not repentance but terror — the reversal of their celebration. The enemies who gloated are now confronted with the reality that God's witnesses cannot be permanently destroyed.

The three-and-a-half-day death corresponds to Jesus' time in the tomb, making the resurrection of the witnesses a direct parallel to the resurrection of Christ. As Desmond Ford writes: "The righteous may be knocked down, but they are not knocked out."

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-7.htm); [Ezek. 37:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-1.htm)–10; [Acts 1:9](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-9.htm); [Rev. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-4.htm)–5.

### [Rev 11:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-12.htm) — "And they heard a great voice from heaven saying unto them, Come up hither"

**Text**: "And they heard a great voice from heaven saying unto them, Come up hither. And they ascended up to heaven in a cloud; and their enemies beheld them."

**Exegesis**: "Come up hither" (*anaba hōde*) repeats the invitation of 4:1, where John was summoned to heaven. Here it is addressed to the resurrected witnesses — divine vindication following resurrection. The ascension "in a cloud" (*en tē nephelē*) directly recalls Jesus' ascension at [Acts 1:9](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-9.htm) — "he was taken up; and a cloud received him out of their sight" — and is witnessed by their enemies, just as the disciples watched Christ ascend. Both Moses (Jude 9) and Elijah ([2 Kings 2:11](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/2-11.htm)) were taken to heaven, providing OT precedents for the witnesses' exaltation.

The enemies' observation of the ascension (*etheōrēsan autous hoi echthroi autōn*) compounds their terror: first resurrection, now exaltation. The very powers that celebrated the witnesses' demise now watch their glorification. This vindication-before-enemies motif pervades the Psalms ([Ps. 23:5](https://biblehub.com/psalms/23-5.htm); 110:1) and recalls [Dan. 7:27](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-27.htm), where the kingdom is given to the saints after the defeat of the beast. The public nature of the ascension — seen by enemies — makes it a cosmic declaration of divine vindication.

The event parallels [Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm)–5, where the 144,000 stand on Mount Zion with the Lamb — the redeemed witnessing community exalted after tribulation.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 4:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-1.htm); [Acts 1:9](https://biblehub.com/acts/1-9.htm); [2 Kings 2:11](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/2-11.htm); Jude 9; [Dan. 7:27](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-27.htm); [Ps. 110:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/110-1.htm); [Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm)–5.

### [Rev 11:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-13.htm) — "And the same hour was there a great earthquake, and the tenth part of the city fell"

**Text**: "And the same hour was there a great earthquake, and the tenth part of the city fell, and in the earthquake were slain of men seven thousand: and the remnant were affrighted, and gave glory to the God of heaven."

**Exegesis**: The "great earthquake" (*seismos megas*) occurring at the same hour as the ascension connects to the earthquake of the sixth seal ([Rev. 6:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-12.htm)) — suggesting this is the same eschatological earthquake signaling the beginning of final events. Earthquakes throughout Revelation mark decisive divine interventions (4:5; 8:5; 11:13, 19; 16:18).

The destruction is partial: one-tenth of the city falls, 7,000 are killed. The city is "the great city" of v. 8 — spiritual Babylon. The 7,000 killed recalls the 7,000 who had not bowed to Baal in Elijah's day ([1 Kings 19:18](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/19-18.htm)): there, 7,000 were the faithful remnant preserved; here, 7,000 represents the totality of hardened unbelievers who perish. The partial destruction — only a tenth — contrasts with Revelation 18's total destruction of Babylon. This is a preliminary judgment; the final collapse comes later.

Critically: "the remnant were affrighted, and gave glory to the God of heaven." Stefanovic argues this represents genuine repentance. The phrase "give glory to God" (*edōkan doxan tō theō*) throughout Revelation signals a positive response to God ([Luke 17:18](https://biblehub.com/luke/17-18.htm); [John 9:24](https://biblehub.com/john/9-24.htm); [Acts 12:23](https://biblehub.com/acts/12-23.htm); [Rom. 4:20](https://biblehub.com/romans/4-20.htm); [Rev. 14:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-7.htm); 15:4; 19:7) — the opposite of the hardened response in [Rev. 9:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-20.htm)–21 and 16:9. The first angel's call in 14:7 — "Fear God and give him glory" — is here fulfilled by the survivors. Nebuchadnezzar's confession after divine judgment provides the OT model ([Dan. 4:34](https://biblehub.com/daniel/4-34.htm)–37). This suggests that the vindication of the witnesses (vv. 11–13) parallels the proclamation of the eternal gospel by the first angel of Rev. 14, and that the close of earth's history will see many people responding to the final proclamation of the gospel in mighty power (cf. [Rev. 18:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-1.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 6:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/6-12.htm); 9:20–21; 14:7; 16:9; 18:1; [1 Kings 19:18](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/19-18.htm); [Dan. 4:34](https://biblehub.com/daniel/4-34.htm)–37; [Luke 17:18](https://biblehub.com/luke/17-18.htm); Rev. 18 (total destruction of Babylon).

### [Rev 11:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-14.htm) — "The second woe is past; and, behold, the third woe cometh quickly"

**Text**: "The second woe is past; and, behold, the third woe cometh quickly."

**Exegesis**: The three woes correspond to the fifth ([Rev. 9:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-1.htm)–12), sixth (9:13–11:13), and seventh (11:15–19) trumpets (cf. [Rev. 8:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-13.htm)). The first woe (fifth trumpet) involved the demonic locust army from the abyss; the second woe (sixth trumpet) released the destroying cavalry from the Euphrates. The interlude of chapters 10–11:13 is part of the second woe. Now the second woe formally closes, and the third woe — the seventh trumpet — is imminent. The phrase "cometh quickly" (*erchetai tachu*) matches the urgency language throughout Revelation (2:5, 16; 3:11; 22:7, 12, 20) and recalls [Rev. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm): "in the days of the sound of the seventh angel... the mystery of God will be completed." The transition is abrupt because the seventh trumpet brings not a plague sequence but the declaration of God's eternal kingdom — the completion of the mystery of God.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 8:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/8-13.htm); 9:12; 10:7; 22:7, 12, 20.

### [Rev 11:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-15.htm) — "And the seventh angel sounded; and there were great voices in heaven"

**Text**: "And the seventh angel sounded; and there were great voices in heaven, saying, The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever."

**Exegesis**: The seventh trumpet is the climax of the trumpet sequence and the formal consummation of God's mystery ([Rev. 10:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/10-7.htm)). Unlike the previous six trumpets (which introduced specific plagues), the seventh trumpet produces no plague but a declaration — a heavenly proclamation of the *fait accompli* of divine rule. The loud voices (*phōnai megalai*) echo the universal witness of heavenly beings.

"The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ" (*Kuriou hēmōn kai tou Christou autou*). The phrase is drawn from [Ps. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-2.htm) — the nations conspire against "the Lord and his Messiah." The singular "kingdom" (*basileia*) absorbs all earthly kingdoms into one: this earth, which has been in rebellion under usurping powers, now formally reverts to its rightful owner. This fulfills [Dan. 2:44](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-44.htm) — "the God of heaven will set up a kingdom which will never be destroyed... it will itself endure forever" — and [Dan. 7:27](https://biblehub.com/daniel/7-27.htm) — "the kingdom and the dominion and the greatness of the kingdoms under the whole heaven shall be given to the people of the saints of the Most High."

The establishment of God's eternal kingdom is the central theme of the entire book of Revelation (11:17; 12:10; 19:6; 22:5). In Rev. 5, Christ formally took the sealed scroll as co-ruler with the Father; his co-rulership was to last "until He has put all His enemies under His feet" ([1 Cor. 15:25](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-25.htm)). At the seventh trumpet, that moment arrives. "He shall reign for ever and ever" (*eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn*) is absolute and unconditional: God's rule, once established, is permanent.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-2.htm); [Dan. 2:44](https://biblehub.com/daniel/2-44.htm); 7:14, 27; [1 Cor. 15:24](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-24.htm)–28; Rev. 5; 10:7; 12:10; 19:6; 22:5; [Matt. 6:10](https://biblehub.com/matthew/6-10.htm).

### [Rev 11:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-16.htm) — "And the four and twenty elders, which sat before God on their seats, fell upon their faces"

**Text**: "And the four and twenty elders, which sat before God on their seats, fell upon their faces, and worshipped God,"

**Exegesis**: The twenty-four elders appear throughout Revelation (4:4, 10; 5:8, 14; 7:11; 11:16; 14:3; 19:4) as representatives of redeemed humanity — a corporate symbol encompassing the twelve patriarchs of Israel and the twelve apostles of the New Testament church (cf. [Rev. 21:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-12.htm), 14). They "sat on thrones" (*kathēmenous epi tous thronous autōn*) — an indication of their regal dignity as those who reign with Christ ([Rev. 5:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-10.htm)).

Their response to the seventh trumpet is prostration and worship: *epeson epi ta prosōpa autōn kai prosekunēsan tō theō* — the full prostration of the highest creation before the Creator-God. This same posture occurred in 4:10 and 5:14 and will recur in 19:4. Prostration (*pesein epi prosōpon*) in the OT signals total reverence before the divine presence ([Num. 16:22](https://biblehub.com/numbers/16-22.htm); 20:6; [Josh. 5:14](https://biblehub.com/joshua/5-14.htm); [1 Kings 18:39](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/18-39.htm)). The elders' worship launches the thanksgiving hymn of vv. 17–18 — they speak on behalf of all redeemed creation.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-4.htm), 10; 5:8, 14; 7:11; 14:3; 19:4; 21:12, 14.

### [Rev 11:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-17.htm) — "Saying, We give thee thanks, O LORD God Almighty"

**Text**: "Saying, We give thee thanks, O LORD God Almighty, which art, and wast, and art to come; because thou hast taken to thee thy great power, and hast reigned."

**Exegesis**: The elders' hymn opens with *eucharistoumen soi* ("we give thanks to you") — the only thanksgiving hymn in Revelation addressed directly to God. The divine title "Lord God Almighty, who is and who was" (*ho ōn kai ho ēn*) appears throughout Revelation (1:4, 8; 4:8), but here the third element — "who is to come" (*ho erchomenos*) — is *omitted*. This is theologically decisive: God is no longer "who is coming" because he has come. His parousia is no longer future; the kingdom has been established. The absence of "who is to come" signals the eschatological completion of divine history.

*Pantokratōr* ("Almighty") — used nine times in Revelation (1:8; 4:8; 11:17; 15:3; 16:7, 14; 19:6, 15; 21:22) — designates absolute sovereign power. "Thou hast taken to thee thy great power" (*eilēphas tēn dunamin sou tēn megalēn*) uses the perfect tense (*eilēphas*): the taking of power is a completed act with present results. This echoes Rev. 5 where Christ formally received the scroll and authority, but now the full exercise of that authority is realized. "And hast reigned" (*ebasileusas*) is also an ingressive aorist — "thou hast begun to reign" — indicating the decisive inauguration of the eternal kingdom.

The hymn's structure outlines the events of Revelation 12–22: nations rage (12–13), God's wrath comes (14–18), judgment occurs (19–20), reward is given (21–22).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-4.htm), 8; 4:8; 5:7; [Ps. 93:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/93-1.htm); 97:1; 99:1; [1 Cor. 15:24](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-24.htm)–28.

### [Rev 11:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-18.htm) — "And the nations were angry, and thy wrath is come"

**Text**: "And the nations were angry, and thy wrath is come, and the time of the dead, that they should be judged, and that thou shouldest give reward unto thy servants the prophets, and to the saints, and them that fear thy name, small and great; and shouldest destroy them which destroy the earth."

**Exegesis**: The verse is a theological summary of Revelation's final events structured chiastically around judgment:

(A) **Nations enraged** (*hoi ethnoi ōrgisthēsan*): The verb *orgizō* and noun *orgē* share the same Greek stem — God's *orgē* (wrath) responds to the nations' *orgē* (rage). The nations' rage against God and his people (developed in Rev. 12–13) culminates in the battle of Armageddon. This alludes to [Ps. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-1.htm)–2, 12: nations rage against the Lord and his anointed; God laughs and responds with wrath. The nations' anger is the direct response of their opposition to the establishment of God's kingdom.

(B) **Thy wrath came** (*ēlthen hē orgē sou*): God's wrath is not irrational anger but the righteous response to unrepentant rebellion. It is poured out in the seven last plagues ([Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm); 15:1; 16:1–21).

(C) **The time of the dead, that they should be judged** (*krithēnai tous nekrous*): The judgment includes both the righteous dead and the unrighteous dead ([Rev. 20:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-4.htm)–15). This encompasses the entire spectrum of divine judgment from the investigative judgment through the millennium to the great white throne.

(D) **To give reward to servants, prophets, saints, and those who fear God, small and great** (*mikrous kai tous megalous*): The phrase "who fear the Lord, the small and the great" is borrowed from [Ps. 115:13](https://biblehub.com/psalms/115-13.htm), denoting all faithful believers regardless of socioeconomic status. The reward occurs at the Second Coming ([Rev. 19:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-1.htm)–10; chapters 21–22).

(E) **To destroy those who destroy the earth** (*diaphtheirai tous diaphtheirontas tēn gēn*): The Greek wordplay — *diaphtheirō* used for both the destroyers and their destruction — mirrors the pre-flood narrative: [Gen. 6:11](https://biblehub.com/genesis/6-11.htm)–13 (LXX) uses the same verb *kataphtheirō* for the antediluvians filling the earth with iniquity and for God's decision to destroy them. The destroyers of the earth are not primarily ecological despoilers but moral corrupters — end-time Babylon "filling the earth with iniquity" ([Rev. 18:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-5.htm)). Jeremiah identified historical Babylon as "the destroying mountain, that destroys the whole earth" ([Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm), LXX). End-time Babylon "corrupted the earth with her immorality" ([Rev. 19:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-2.htm); cf. 17:1–6). This is their judgment.

**Cross-references**: [Ps. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/2-1.htm)–2, 12; 115:13; [Gen. 6:11](https://biblehub.com/genesis/6-11.htm)–14 (LXX); [Jer. 51:25](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/51-25.htm); [Rev. 14:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-10.htm); 15:1; 16:1–21; 19:2; 20:4–15; [1 Cor. 3:17](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/3-17.htm).

### [Rev 11:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-19.htm) — "And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament"

**Text**: "And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament: and there were lightnings, and voices, and thunderings, and an earthquake, and great hail."

**Exegesis**: This verse functions as a double hinge: it closes the first half of Revelation (the seven trumpets) and opens the second half (Rev. 12–22). It is simultaneously a conclusion and a prologue.

*Naos* here — as throughout Revelation — is the innermost sanctuary, the Most Holy Place (the Greek distinguishes *naos* from *hieron*, the entire temple complex). That the innermost part is specified is confirmed by what is seen within: the ark of the covenant, which was located exclusively in the Most Holy Place. The opening of the heavenly *naos* in vv. 19 marks the transition in the heavenly sanctuary's ministry from the Holy Place to the Most Holy Place — corresponding to the Day of Atonement, the theme already flagged in v. 1. Kenneth Strand demonstrated that [Rev. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm)–2, 19 together constitute an inclusion structured around Yom Kippur.

**The ark of the covenant** (*hē kibōtos tēs diathēkēs*): In the OT, the ark was the most sacred object in Israel's sanctuary system — the footstool of God's throne, the repository of the law (Ten Commandments), covered by the mercy seat (*kappōret*) where the blood of atonement was sprinkled. Its appearance at this point serves three functions:

1. **Disclosure**: The ark was the storage place of the Book of the Covenant ([Deut. 31:26](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/31-26.htm)). Its revelation here prepares for the disclosure of the contents of the little scroll of Rev. 10 — the subject of Revelation 12–22.

2. **Covenant faithfulness**: In the OT, the ark was the symbol of God's continuing presence with his people and guarantee of his covenant promises during wilderness journey and battle. Its appearance here assures God's end-time people that despite the trials to come (Rev. 12–22), God will remain faithful to his covenant promises and destroy the enemies of his people.

3. **Judgment**: The ark housed the law — the standard of divine judgment. Its revelation at this pivotal moment, coinciding with the judgment scene of v. 18, confirms that divine judgment is grounded in the law.

The accompanying theophanic signs — *astrapai kai phōnai kai brontai kai seismos kai chalaza megalē* ("lightnings, voices, thunderings, earthquake, and great hail") — appear four times in Revelation (4:5; 8:5; 11:19; 16:18–21), each time with intensification, marking decisive divine interventions modeled on Sinai ([Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm)–19; 20:18; [Deut. 5:22](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/5-22.htm)–23). The addition of "great hail" here (and in 16:21) signals increasing eschatological severity. These phenomena simultaneously represent the manifestation of the divine presence and the beginning of the final judgments to be unfolded in Rev. 12–22.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 19:16](https://biblehub.com/exodus/19-16.htm)–19; 25:10–22; Lev. 16 (Day of Atonement); [Deut. 31:26](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/31-26.htm); [1 Kings 8:1](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/8-1.htm)–9; [Rev. 4:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-5.htm); 8:5; 16:18–21; Ezek. 40–42 (Strand parallel); Dan. 9 (week-of-years context); [Rev. 12:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/12-17.htm); 14:12 (law of God / ark connection).
