## Revelation 22

### [Rev 22:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-1.htm) — "And he shewed me a pure river of water of life"

**Text**: "And he shewed me a pure river of water of life, clear as crystal, proceeding out of the throne of God and of the Lamb."

**Exegesis**: The subject of "he shewed" is the angel who has guided John through the new Jerusalem vision since 21:9. The Greek *potamon hydatos zōēs* ("river of water of life") means literally "the water which is life" — not merely water that sustains life but water that IS life, an identification of substance with effect. The qualifier *lampron hōs krustallon* ("bright/clear as crystal") emphasizes purity and divine radiance in contrast to the murkied waters of Babylon (17:1, 15).

The image is a dense confluence of OT allusions: (1) the river flowing from Eden watering the garden and making it fruitful, with the tree of life on its bank ([Gen. 2:9-10](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-9.htm)); (2) [Ps. 46:4](https://biblehub.com/psalms/46-4.htm), "a river whose streams make glad the city of God, the holy dwelling places of the Most High"; (3) Ezekiel's visionary river flowing from the temple giving life wherever it flows, its banks bearing trees for food and healing ([Ezek. 47:1-12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-1.htm)); (4) Joel's fountain flowing from the house of the Lord ([Joel 3:18](https://biblehub.com/joel/3-18.htm)); (5) Zechariah's living waters flowing from restored Jerusalem ([Zech. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-8.htm)). Everything the prophets foreshadowed John now sees fulfilled.

Some commentators (Sweet, Beale) identify the river with the Holy Spirit, citing [Isa. 44:3-4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/44-3.htm) (pouring out water / Spirit on offspring) and [John 7:38](https://biblehub.com/john/7-38.htm)-39 where Jesus calls living water "the Spirit." The connection is reinforced at 22:17 where the Spirit calls "Come!" followed by the invitation to take the water of life freely.

A pivotal theological moment: this is the **first time in Revelation the throne is called "the throne of the Lamb."** Throughout the book, the throne has been the prerogative of God the Father. [Rev. 3:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-21.htm) anticipated Christ sitting on the Father's throne as co-ruler; now, after the millennium and the destruction of evil, Christ takes full co-rulership. The throne is the joint seat of both Father and Lamb (cf. 7:17; [1 Cor. 15:28](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-28.htm)). The place from which all judgments were issued becomes the source of eternal life and happiness.

Contrastively, this river stands against the Euphrates flowing through ancient Babylon (17:1), beside which God's exiled people sat weeping ([Ps. 137:1-6](https://biblehub.com/psalms/137-1.htm)). Babylon is past; captivity is over. The redeemed now rest beside the river of life.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 2:9-10](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-9.htm); [Ps. 46:4](https://biblehub.com/psalms/46-4.htm); [Ezek. 47:1-12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-1.htm); [Joel 3:18](https://biblehub.com/joel/3-18.htm); [Zech. 14:8](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-8.htm); [Isa. 44:3-4](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/44-3.htm); [John 7:38](https://biblehub.com/john/7-38.htm)-39; [Rev. 3:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-21.htm); 7:17; 21:6; [1 Cor. 15:28](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-28.htm).

### [Rev 22:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-2.htm) — "In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, was there the tree of life"

**Text**: "In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, was there the tree of life, which bare twelve manner of fruits, and yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations."

**Exegesis**: The Greek text lacks punctuation, creating a crux. The phrase "in the middle of its street" can either: (a) conclude verse 1 — the river flows down the city's broad street (NASB, RSV, NIV, NRSV), or (b) begin verse 2 as a new sentence (KJV, UBS, NE Greek NT). Most scholars, including David Aune, favor reading (a) because John frequently begins new sentences with *kai* or prepositional phrases (cf. 4:1; 7:9; 18:1; 19:1). In this reading, the river flows through the city's main street, with the tree of life on both its banks.

The paradox of one tree on "both sides" of the river recalls [Ezek. 47:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-7.htm), 12 (trees on both banks), but where Ezekiel has avenues of many trees, John has one — the tree of life (*to xulon tēs zōēs*), singular, yet spanning both banks. This single tree in the midst echoes [Gen. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-9.htm) where the tree of life stood in the center of Eden. To eat from it meant "to live forever" ([Gen. 3:22](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-22.htm)); after Adam and Eve's expulsion, access was barred ([Gen. 3:23-24](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-23.htm)). In the new Jerusalem the tree is no longer forbidden. All that was lost through Adam is regained through Christ.

**Twelve fruits, each month**: The perpetual, monthly cycle of fruit (from [Ezek. 47:12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-12.htm) — "their fruit will not fail") signifies the unending, superabundant provision of eternal life. Twelve is the number of completeness and universality (the twelve tribes, twelve apostles). Fruit available every month means the supply never lapses; the life-giving nourishment of God is not seasonal but constant.

**Healing of the nations**: Direct allusion to [Ezek. 47:12](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-12.htm) — "their leaves for healing." The OT background: "sitting under his vine and fig tree" signified peace and safety under Solomon ([1 Kings 4:24](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/4-24.htm)-25); exile deprived Israel of this; Micah (4:4) and Zechariah (3:10) prophesied the restored era when every man would sit under his vine and fig tree unafraid. The "healing" in Ezekiel referred to the emotional wounds of exile being healed in the restored homeland. In [Rev. 22:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-2.htm) the scope is universalized — "the healing of the **nations**" (Greek *ethnē*, Gentiles).

Critically, this does NOT mean disease prevention in eternity, since with annihilation of evil, sickness and death have been forever removed (21:4). "The healing of the nations" refers figuratively to the **removal of all national, ethnic, linguistic, and racial barriers**. God's kingdom is not confined to any one people. New Jerusalem is inhabited by people of all nations, tribes, and tongues (5:9; 7:9), including Egyptians, Assyrians, Babylonians, Romans, and Jews. The leaves of the tree of life heal the ancient breaches between peoples; nations are no longer "gentiles" but one family. Micah's vision — "nation will not lift up sword against nation" ([Mic. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/micah/4-3.htm)) — comes to fulfillment. What Zechariah anticipated (Zech. 10) — neighbors sitting together under the tree — is now reality.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 2:9](https://biblehub.com/genesis/2-9.htm); 3:22-24; [Ezek. 47:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/47-7.htm), 12; [Mic. 4:3-4](https://biblehub.com/micah/4-3.htm); [Zech. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/3-10.htm); [1 Kings 4:24](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/4-24.htm)-25; [Rev. 5:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-9.htm); 7:9; 21:24-26.

### [Rev 22:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-3.htm) — "And there shall be no more curse"

**Text**: "And there shall be no more curse: but the throne of God and of the Lamb shall be in it; and his servants shall serve him:"

**Exegesis**: Punctuation again affects exegesis. The phrase "there will be no more curse" functions best as the conclusion of the preceding thought about the tree of life (v2): the healing of the nations is complete because the curse is removed. The Greek *katathema* (curse) — the covenantal curse laid on creation after the fall — is annulled. Sin brought the curse on the earth ([Gen. 3:17-19](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-17.htm); 5:29); the most painful aspect was banishment from Eden and the tree of life ([Gen. 3:22-24](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-22.htm)). With eradication of sin, the curse ends entirely. Zechariah prophesied: "There will be no more curse, for Jerusalem will dwell in security" ([Zech. 14:11](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-11.htm)) — now fulfilled.

**The throne of God and of the Lamb**: The central object of the new earth is this joint throne (also 22:1). The political theology is significant: John wrote to Christians suffering under the imperial throne of Rome, which Satan used for persecution and corruption. The Roman throne was the instrument of oppression. In the new Jerusalem, the divine throne as the symbol of God's power signifies the **ultimate subjection of Satanic power** and God's permanent, abiding presence among the saved (cf. [Ezek. 43:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/43-7.htm)). The very symbol of terror becomes the fount of eternal life and happiness.

**His servants shall serve him** (*latreuousin autō*): The Greek *latreuō* is specifically cultic/worship service. In the restored paradise, the redeemed freely approach God as priests in unmediated worship. In the earthly sanctuary only priests served in God's presence; the common people accessed God through mediators. Now all the redeemed serve as a kingdom of priests (1:6; 5:10) in the immediate presence of God, without barrier.

**Cross-references**: [Gen. 3:17-19](https://biblehub.com/genesis/3-17.htm); [Zech. 14:11](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/14-11.htm); [Ezek. 43:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/43-7.htm); [Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:10; 21:3; 22:1.

### [Rev 22:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-4.htm) — "And they shall see his face"

**Text**: "And they shall see his face; and his name shall be in their foreheads."

**Exegesis**: **Seeing the face of God** is the supreme privilege of the redeemed. In the OT this was categorically denied to mortals: "You cannot see My face, for no man can see My face and live" ([Exod. 33:20](https://biblehub.com/exodus/33-20.htm)). Moses, the greatest prophet, was permitted only to see God's back ([Exod. 33:23](https://biblehub.com/exodus/33-23.htm)). The priests of the earthly temple could not enter the Holy of Holies; only the high priest entered once yearly, and even he could not look directly at the ark. The promise of NT believers was "we will see Him just as He is" ([1 [John 3:2](https://biblehub.com/john/3-2.htm)](https://biblehub.com/1_john/3-2.htm)) — now fulfilled.

The new Jerusalem is shaped as a perfect cube, like the Holy of Holies ([1 Kings 6:20](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-20.htm)). What the high priest alone experienced once a year — direct presence with God — is now the permanent, unmediated experience of all the redeemed throughout eternity.

**His name on their foreheads**: This echoes [Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm) where the 144,000 stand on Mount Zion with the Father's name written on their foreheads, and the promise to Philadelphian overcomers (3:12 — "I will write on him the name of My God"). It stands in deliberate contrast to the mark of the beast (13:16; 14:9) — those who refused to receive the name of the beast on their foreheads now bear the name of God. In biblical theology a name denotes **character** (cf. the renaming of Jacob/Israel, Abram/Abraham). To bear God's name is to perfectly reflect God's character. The redeemed will image the character of God throughout eternity — the restoration of the *imago Dei* ([Gen. 1:27](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-27.htm)) to its fullest expression.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 33:20](https://biblehub.com/exodus/33-20.htm), 23; [1 [John 3:2](https://biblehub.com/john/3-2.htm)](https://biblehub.com/1_john/3-2.htm); [Rev. 3:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-12.htm); 14:1; 13:16; [Gen. 1:27](https://biblehub.com/genesis/1-27.htm); [1 Kings 6:20](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-20.htm).

### [Rev 22:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-5.htm) — "And there shall be no night there"

**Text**: "And there shall be no night there; and they need no candle, neither light of the sun; for the Lord God giveth them light: and they shall reign for ever and ever."

**Exegesis**: John here deliberately recapitulates 21:23-25, forming a doxological conclusion to the vision of the new earth (21:9–22:5). The absence of night is the absence of all that night symbolizes — danger, evil, hiddenness, death. No *luchnos* (oil lamp) is needed; no sun (*hēlios*) is needed. The light source is the Lord God himself, whose glory illumines the entire city (cf. 21:23, which specifies the Lamb as its lamp). This fulfills [Isa. 60:19-20](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm): "No longer will you have the sun for light by day... but you will have the Lord for an everlasting light."

**They shall reign for ever and ever** (*basileusosin eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn*): Throughout Revelation it is repeatedly God who lives and reigns forever (4:9-10; 10:6; 11:15; 15:7). Now this attribute of eternal reign is shared with the redeemed. Christ's death made them kings and priests (1:6; 5:10); Paul affirmed "if we endure, we shall also reign with Him" ([2 Tim. 2:12](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/2-12.htm)). The great cosmic drama reaches its destination: the redeemed dwell in God's abiding presence, in intimate relationship, co-reigning with him throughout eternity. The promise given to the overcomers in all seven churches (2:26-27; 3:21) reaches its final fulfillment.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 21:23-25](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-23.htm); [Isa. 60:19-20](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm); [Rev. 4:9-10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-9.htm); 11:15; 1:6; 5:10; [2 Tim. 2:12](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/2-12.htm); [Rev. 2:26-27](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-26.htm); 3:21.

### [Rev 22:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-6.htm) — "These sayings are faithful and true"

**Text**: "And he said unto me, These sayings are faithful and true: and the Lord God of the holy prophets sent his angel to shew unto his servants the things which must shortly be done."

**Exegesis**: With 22:6, John transitions from the vision to the epilogue (22:6-21), which mirrors the prologue (1:1-8) structurally. The angel concludes the vision of new Jerusalem with a certification of reliability. The phrase "faithful and true" (*pistoi kai alēthinoi*) is the signature of Christ himself (19:11; 3:14); the same description applied to the words of 21:5. Everything recorded in the book carries the reliability of Christ himself: "Heaven and earth will pass away, but My words shall not pass away" ([Matt. 24:35](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-35.htm)).

**"The Lord God of the spirits of the prophets"** (*ho theos tōn pneumatōn tōn prophētōn*): A direct allusion to 19:10. The phrase means "the God who inspired the minds/spirits of the prophets." It establishes a chain of authority: the same God who inspired the OT prophets sent this angel to show John what "must shortly take place." The vision is not separate from OT prophecy but its continuation and fulfillment. Therefore the visions of Revelation "must be treated with equal seriousness" as OT prophecy.

**"Things which must shortly be done"** (*ha dei genesthai en tachei*): This phrase repeats 1:1 word for word, completing the frame around the entire book. The Greek *en tachei* ("in/with speed") conveys not primarily calendar nearness but the certainty and suddenness of fulfillment once events begin. Combined with "the time is near" (v. 10), it urges every generation to live in readiness, not because the end will arrive within years but because its approach is certain and its arrival will be swift.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-1.htm); 19:11; 3:14; 21:5; 19:10; [Matt. 24:35](https://biblehub.com/matthew/24-35.htm).

### [Rev 22:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-7.htm) — "Behold, I come quickly"

**Text**: "Behold, I come quickly: blessed is he that keepeth the sayings of the prophecy of this book."

**Exegesis**: The speaker shifts from angel to Christ. The futuristic present *erchomai* ("I come") stresses not primarily imminence but **certainty** — an event so sure it is described as already occurring (cf. 1:7; 22:12, 20). The Greek *tachu* ("quickly/soon") echoes throughout the epilogue (vv. 7, 12, 20) as a refrain, keeping the eschatological urgency before the reader.

**Sixth beatitude of Revelation**: "Blessed is he that keepeth the sayings of the prophecy of this book." This reiterates 1:3 ("blessed is he who reads and those who hear the words of the prophecy and keep those things written in it"), forming an inclusio with the prologue. The beatitude stresses *tērōn* (keeping, heeding, observing) — not passive acquaintance with the book's contents but active obedience. Revelation's prophecies were not given to satisfy curiosity about the future but to admonish God's people to steadfast loyalty to Christ in the face of persecution and deception, so they might be preserved through the final crisis.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-3.htm), 7; 3:11; 22:12, 20.

### [Rev 22:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-8.htm) — "And I John saw these things, and heard them"

**Text**: "And I John saw these things, and heard them. And when I had heard and seen, I fell down to worship before the feet of the angel which shewed me these things."

**Exegesis**: John reidentifies himself as eyewitness (cf. 1:9), stressing that everything recorded is what he personally heard and saw. The personal testimony underscores the reliability of the vision. Overwhelmed by the spectacular glory of the new Jerusalem — the cumulative effect of all he has witnessed — John again falls (*epesan*, aorist) to worship at the feet of the angel. This is the **second proskunēsis before an angel** (cf. 19:10), making it a deliberate pattern. The repetition is structurally significant: in both cases John is immediately corrected.

The act is understandable psychologically (sublime vision, angelic mediator of divine revelation) but theologically wrong — it displaces worship from God to a creature. The epilogue's explicit correction of this act twice is especially relevant to the end-time context John addresses: when the entire world is being deceived into worshiping the beast and the false prophet (13:12), the call to worship God alone (14:7) is the central counter-claim.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-9.htm); 19:10; 13:12; 14:7.

### [Rev 22:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-9.htm) — "See thou do it not: for I am thy fellowservant"

**Text**: "Then saith he unto me, See thou do it not: for I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren the prophets, and of them which keep the sayings of this book: worship God."

**Exegesis**: The angel's sharp refusal (*hora mē* — "see [that you do] not") is nearly identical to 19:10. The angel identifies himself as *syndoulos* ("fellow servant"), placing himself in a category shared with (a) John, (b) the prophets, and (c) those who keep the words of this book. All are servants; only God receives worship.

The threefold identification is theologically rich. The angel stands alongside the prophets — the inspired communicators of God's word. This reinforces 22:6: the same God who inspired the prophets commissioned this angel. He stands alongside the faithful keepers of Revelation's words — end-time believers. Neither prophetic office nor angelic dignity justifies receiving worship. The command *proskunēson tō theō* ("worship God") is absolute and exclusive. In the context of the book's central conflict between the worship of God (14:7) and the worship of the beast (13:4, 12), this prohibition of angel-worship provides the template: the only legitimate recipient of worship is God.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 19:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-10.htm); 14:7; 13:4, 12.

### [Rev 22:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-10.htm) — "Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book"

**Text**: "And he saith unto me, Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand."

**Exegesis**: The contrast with Daniel is deliberate and explicit. Daniel was commanded: "Conceal these words and seal up the book until the end of time" ([Dan. 8:26](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-26.htm); 12:4). Daniel's visions concerned the distant future end-time and were irrelevant to his contemporaries — hence the seal. In Revelation, the situation is reversed: "Do not seal the words of the prophecy of this book." The reason: *ho kairos engus estin* ("the time is near"). The seven-sealed scroll of Rev. 5 that Christ alone was worthy to open has now been opened and its contents revealed (12:1–22:5). Christ instructs John to leave it unsealed so it may be read, heard, and heeded.

This "unsealing" is not merely about publication but about interpretive openness. The prophecies of Revelation concern things that are already beginning to affect God's people; they must not be locked away as irrelevant to the present. Every generation of believers lives in "the time near" — the eschatological hour that has been opening since the first century and reaches its climax at the end.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 8:26](https://biblehub.com/daniel/8-26.htm); 12:4; [Rev. 5:1-9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-1.htm); 1:3.

### [Rev 22:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-11.htm) — "He that is unjust, let him be unjust still"

**Text**: "He that is unjust, let him be unjust still: and he which is filthy, let him be filthy still: and he that is righteous, let him be righteous still: and he that is holy, let him be holy still."

**Exegesis**: This verse sits structurally between the statement "the time is near" (v. 10) and "I come quickly" (v. 12). It describes the **moral polarization** that results from the opening of prophetic word at the time of the end. Daniel received the same principle: "Many will be purged, purified and refined, but the wicked will act wickedly; and none of the wicked will understand, but those who have insight will understand" ([Dan. 12:10](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-10.htm)).

The Greek aorist imperatives (*adikēsatō eti*, *rhupanthētō eti*, *dikaiosunēn poiēsatō eti*, *hagiasthētō eti*) are not divine approval of sin but a solemn decree: when the proclamation of God's word has run its course and the close of probation arrives (cf. 15:5-8), human characters are fixed. There is no further opportunity to accept the gospel or establish relationship with God. The four-fold parallelism (unjust/filthy // righteous/holy) echoes the two-way division throughout the book: those inside the city versus those outside (21:8, 27; 22:14-15).

This is not fatalism about the present moment but eschatological realism about the final moment: those who persistently resist the gospel will find their resistance confirmed; those who responded will find their righteousness confirmed. When Christ arrives, each person's work is already determined.

**Cross-references**: [Dan. 12:10](https://biblehub.com/daniel/12-10.htm); [Rev. 15:5-8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-5.htm); 21:8, 27; 22:14-15.

### [Rev 22:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-12.htm) — "And, behold, I come quickly; and my reward is with me"

**Text**: "And, behold, I come quickly; and my reward is with me, to give every man according as his work shall be."

**Exegesis**: Christ speaks directly. The pattern "I come quickly" (*erchomai tachu*) recurs from v. 7 and anticipates v. 20, giving the epilogue a rhythmic eschatological pulse. With his coming Christ brings *ho misthos mou* ("my reward") — the recompense that belongs to him to distribute. The phrase "give to each as his work is" (*apodounai ekastō hōs to ergon estin autou*) echoes [Matt. 16:27](https://biblehub.com/matthew/16-27.htm) ("he will recompense every man according to his works," KJV), and is repeated by Paul ([Rom. 2:6](https://biblehub.com/romans/2-6.htm); 14:12; [2 Cor. 5:10](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/5-10.htm)).

This does not teach salvation by works. Elsewhere in Revelation salvation is explicitly God's gift (7:10, 14). The Reformation principle: salvation is by grace; the judgment is according to works. Works are the strongest evidence of one's salvation and relationship with Christ. The character revealed through works serves as the basis of judicial recognition, not the ground of salvific merit.

The imminence of reward-at-his-coming undercuts both complacency ("it doesn't matter what I do") and despair ("my failures are final"). Waiting for Christ's return properly motivates righteous living — not idle anticipation but diligent faithfulness in every sphere.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 22:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-7.htm), 20; [Matt. 16:27](https://biblehub.com/matthew/16-27.htm); [Rom. 2:6](https://biblehub.com/romans/2-6.htm); [2 Cor. 5:10](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/5-10.htm); [Rev. 7:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-10.htm), 14.

### [Rev 22:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-13.htm) — "I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the first and the last"

**Text**: "I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the first and the last."

**Exegesis**: The threefold self-identification achieves maximum density of divine absoluteness. (1) **Alpha and Omega** (*to Alpha kai to Ō*): first and last letters of the Greek alphabet — applied to God in 1:8 and 21:6, and here to Christ. He encompasses all of history from first to final letter. (2) **The beginning and the end** (*hē archē kai to telos*): applied to God in 21:6; here to Christ. He is the origin from which all things proceed and the goal toward which all things move. (3) **The first and the last** (*ho prōtos kai ho eschatos*): applied to Christ in 1:17 and 2:8, echoing [Isa. 44:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/44-6.htm) and 48:12 where it is a title of YHWH — "I am the first and I am the last, and there is no God besides Me."

The accumulation of all three titles on Christ in a single verse is a high Christological statement: Christ holds absolute sovereignty over history, is the totality of divine authority, and is YHWH himself. He is not only the one coming to reward — he is the A-to-Z of reality itself. This grounds the certainty of his coming and the reliability of his reward: one who spans all of history cannot fail to complete what he began.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-8.htm), 17; 2:8; 21:6; [Isa. 44:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/44-6.htm); 48:12.

### [Rev 22:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-14.htm) — "Blessed are they that do his commandments"

**Text**: "Blessed are they that do his commandments, that they may have right to the tree of life, and may enter in through the gates into the city."

**Exegesis**: The **seventh and final beatitude** of Revelation (cf. 1:3; 14:13; 16:15; 19:9; 20:6; 22:7). Seven beatitudes for the seven-church audience — completeness of blessing. This beatitude describes the condition for entering the new Jerusalem.

**Textual crux**: The KJV reads "do his commandments" (*hoi poiountes tas entolas*), but the earliest and best Greek manuscripts read "wash their robes" (*hoi plunontes tas stolas*). The two phrases are visually and aurally similar when written in uncial (capital) Greek without spaces:
- HOIPOIOUNTESTASENTOLAS
- HOIPLUNONTESTASSTOLAS

A scribe reading or hearing could easily substitute one for the other. The manuscript evidence strongly favors "wash their robes." Internal evidence concurs: (a) Revelation refers to "keeping commandments" elsewhere (12:17; 14:12) and "keeping the words of the book" (1:3; 22:7, 9) — never "doing commandments" in this absolute form; (b) the washing of robes is the explicit soteriological mechanism in 7:14 ("washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb").

The robe (*stolē*) represents righteousness (19:8 — "the righteous deeds of the saints") and is identified with the garment of salvation ([Isa. 61:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/61-10.htm) — "He has wrapped me with a robe of righteousness"). Washing in the blood of the Lamb is the only means of making it white. The **present participle** (*plunontes* — "those who are washing") indicates ongoing, continual action — a sustained posture of reliance on Christ's atoning blood, not a single act.

The reward is twofold: **right/authority over the tree of life** (*exousia epi to xulon tēs zōēs*) and **entrance through the city's gates**. These recapitulate the promises of 2:7 ("I will grant to eat from the tree of life") and the invitation of 21:25 ("its gates shall never be closed"). Salvation's ground is entirely what Christ has done, not what the believer has done for himself.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-3.htm); 7:14; 12:17; 14:12; 19:8; 2:7; 21:25; [Isa. 61:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/61-10.htm); [Rev. 22:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-7.htm).

### [Rev 22:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-15.htm) — "For without are dogs, and sorcerers, and whoremongers"

**Text**: "For without are dogs, and sorcerers, and whoremongers, and murderers, and idolaters, and whosoever loveth and maketh a lie."

**Exegesis**: The exclusion list contrasts sharply with v. 14's beatitude. This is the third such list in the final section of Revelation (cf. 21:8, 27), each slightly varied. The condemned are permanently outside (*exō*) — not merely temporarily excluded but definitively placed beyond the city's walls and beyond the tree of life.

**"Dogs"** (*hoi kynes*): In antiquity dogs were negative symbols — scavengers, base creatures. In the OT "dog" denotes a male prostitute ([Deut. 23:17-18](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/23-17.htm)) and evil/contemptible persons ([2 Sam. 16:9](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/16-9.htm); [2 Kings 8:13](https://biblehub.com/2_kings/8-13.htm); [Ps. 22:16](https://biblehub.com/psalms/22-16.htm), 20; 59:6; [Isa. 56:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/56-11.htm)). In the NT it symbolizes the unholy ([Matt. 7:6](https://biblehub.com/matthew/7-6.htm)), Gentiles in a derogatory sense ([Matt. 15:26-27](https://biblehub.com/matthew/15-26.htm)), and false teachers/evil workers ([Phil. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/philippians/3-2.htm); [2 Pet. 2:22](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/2-22.htm)). In Revelation, "dogs" = morally base/unholy persons in general.

**Sorcerers** (*pharmakoi*): Those who practice magic arts and drug-assisted sorcery. Appear also in 21:8 and 9:21. Isaiah lists sorcery as characteristic of Babylon ([Isa. 47:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-9.htm), 12).

**Whoremongers/fornicators** (*pornoi*): Sexual immorality as both literal sin and the spiritual adultery of idolatrous compromise with the beast system (cf. 17:2; 18:3).

**Murderers** (*phoneis*): Cf. 21:8.

**Idolaters** (*eidōlolatroi*): The fundamental sin — misplaced worship, the core of the beast-system's demand (13:4, 12, 15).

**"Whosoever loveth and maketh a lie"** (*pas philōn kai poiōn pseudos*): The double verb is significant — both loving and practicing the lie. The "lie" (*pseudos*) throughout Revelation refers to the satanic counterfeit religion, the false prophet's deceptions (16:13; 19:20), and the beast's propaganda. The phrase encompasses all who have embraced the great deception rather than the truth of Christ. This is the product of idolatry ([Rom. 1:18-32](https://biblehub.com/romans/1-18.htm); [Rev. 21:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-8.htm); 22:15). All who love and practice what is contrary to the gospel are excluded from the heavenly city.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 21:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-8.htm), 27; [Deut. 23:17-18](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/23-17.htm); [Phil. 3:2](https://biblehub.com/philippians/3-2.htm); [Isa. 47:9](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/47-9.htm); [Rev. 9:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-21.htm); 13:4, 12, 15; 17:2.

### [Rev 22:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-16.htm) — "I Jesus have sent mine angel to testify unto you these things"

**Text**: "I Jesus have sent mine angel to testify unto you these things in the churches. I am the root and the offspring of David, and the bright and morning star."

**Exegesis**: Christ now identifies himself as the source of the entire book. The phrase "I, Jesus, have sent my angel" (*egō Iēsous epempsa ton angelon mou*) corresponds to 1:1 ("He sent and signified it by His angel to His servant John") — another epilogue/prologue inclusio. The emphasis is deliberate: the angel who spoke throughout the vision was Christ's commissioned agent; Christ himself is the ultimate author. The visions are Christ's personal testimony to his churches.

**"In the churches"** (*epi tais ekklēsiais*): The pronoun "you" is plural in Greek — this testimony is corporate, addressed to the seven churches of Asia Minor (1:11) and, through them, to the universal church. What was shown to John was not for private edification but for the corporate people of God.

**"The root and the offspring of David"** (*hē rhiza kai to genos Dauid*): Christ is simultaneously David's **root** (the divine source from which David sprang, [Isa. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/11-1.htm), 10; [Rom. 15:12](https://biblehub.com/romans/15-12.htm)) and David's **offspring** (the human descendant). This double identification — transcendent deity and incarnate Messiah — encapsulates the mystery of the Incarnation. In 5:5 he was called "the Lion from the tribe of Judah, the Root of David." He is the fulfillment of the Davidic covenant ([2 Sam. 7:12-16](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/7-12.htm)) and simultaneously its divine origin. All authority and dominion have been given to him ([Matt. 28:18](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-18.htm); Rev. 5).

**"The bright and morning star"** (*ho astēr ho lampros ho prōinos*): The morning star (Venus) appears just before dawn — the herald of a new day. In [Num. 24:17](https://biblehub.com/numbers/24-17.htm) Balaam prophesied a "star" arising from Jacob to rule Israel, widely understood messianically. [Isaiah 14:12](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/14-12.htm) used the morning star (*hēlel*, Lucifer in Latin) for the king of Babylon in his pride — Christ claims the title in its true, unhusurped form. In [Rev. 2:28](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-28.htm), the overcomer at Thyatira was promised "the morning star" — Christ himself. He is the dawning of the long-awaited new age, the inauguration of the kingdom of God on earth.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-1.htm), 11; 5:5; [Isa. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/11-1.htm), 10; [Rom. 15:12](https://biblehub.com/romans/15-12.htm); [2 Sam. 7:12-16](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/7-12.htm); [Num. 24:17](https://biblehub.com/numbers/24-17.htm); [Rev. 2:28](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-28.htm); [Matt. 28:18](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-18.htm).

### [Rev 22:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-17.htm) — "And the Spirit and the bride say, Come"

**Text**: "And the Spirit and the bride say, Come. And let him that heareth say, Come. And let him that is athirst come. And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely."

**Exegesis**: The concluding invitation of the book. The Spirit (*to pneuma*) and the bride (*hē numphē* — the church/new Jerusalem, 21:2, 9) together issue a single word: *erchou* ("Come"). The call is universal — everyone who hears the Spirit's voice through the church's witness should echo it to others, becoming a conduit of the gospel invitation. The cascade — Spirit and bride → "let him that heareth say Come → let him that is athirst come → whosoever will" — is an expanding ring of invitation, moving from corporate proclamation to individual response.

The bride here is the church in its witness-bearing function, not merely the eschatological heavenly city. The Spirit works through the church to extend salvation's invitation to the thirsty world. This alludes to Jesus at the Feast of Booths (Tabernacles): "If any man is thirsty, let him come to Me and drink" ([John 7:37](https://biblehub.com/john/7-37.htm)) — and, as the commentary at 22:1 notes, this living water is also connected to the Spirit ([John 7:38](https://biblehub.com/john/7-38.htm)-39). The connection of the Spirit to the water of life links the present work of the Spirit in the church with the ultimate eschatological reality of the river of life in the new Jerusalem.

**"Take the water of life freely"** (*labetō hydōr zōēs dōrean*): *Dōrean* = "as a gift, without cost." This is grace — salvation offered without price, without merit, without prerequisite beyond desire (*ho thelōn*, "the one who desires/wills"). It echoes 21:6 ("I will give freely to the thirsty from the fountain of the water of life") and [Isa. 55:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/55-1.htm) ("Everyone who thirsts, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat"). The gospel's final word in Revelation is not warning or judgment but free gift.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 21:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-2.htm), 9; [John 7:37](https://biblehub.com/john/7-37.htm)-39; [Rev. 21:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-6.htm); [Isa. 55:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/55-1.htm).

### [Rev 22:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-18.htm) — "If any man shall add unto these things"

**Text**: "For I testify unto every man that heareth the words of the prophecy of this book, If any man shall add unto these things, God shall add unto him the plagues that are written in this book:"

**Exegesis**: Christ adds a solemn postscript (*marturomai* — "I solemnly testify/witness"). The warning addresses "everyone who hears the words of the prophecy of this book" — the entire audience of Revelation in every generation. The threat of severe consequence is not arbitrary but reflects the sacred character of the book as divinely revealed word.

**"Add unto these things"**: The Greek *epitithēmi* (add upon/to). The punishment is precisely proportional: the plagues of the book (*tas plēgas tas gegrammentas en tō bibliō toutō*) are added to the one who adds to the book. The symmetry — addition for addition — underscores that tampering with God's word is a covenant violation of the highest order.

The echo of [Deut. 4:2](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/4-2.htm) is explicit: "You shall not add to the word which I am commanding you, nor take away from it, that you may keep the commandments of the Lord your God." Similar warnings appear in [Deut. 12:32](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/12-32.htm) and [Prov. 30:5-6](https://biblehub.com/proverbs/30-5.htm). The principle is ancient: the word of God in its totality is normative and must not be augmented by human invention.

Critically, Christ's warning is NOT merely about physically adding text to the book of Revelation, as though defending a concept of verbal mechanical inspiration. It is about **misinterpreting the teaching of the book by forcing personal ideas and views upon the text** to suit one's own purposes — distorting what Revelation actually teaches about the course of history, the nature of the final crisis, and the gospel invitation.

**Cross-references**: [Deut. 4:2](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/4-2.htm); 12:32; [Prov. 30:5-6](https://biblehub.com/proverbs/30-5.htm); [Rev. 15:1-8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/15-1.htm).

### [Rev 22:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-19.htm) — "And if any man shall take away from the words of the book"

**Text**: "And if any man shall take away from the words of the book of this prophecy, God shall take away his part out of the book of life, and out of the holy city, and from the things which are written in this book."

**Exegesis**: The second half of the warning, balancing subtraction against addition. "Take away from the words" (*aphairō*) = deletion, suppression, or minimization of what Revelation teaches. Again, punishment fits the crime precisely: God will take away (*aphairein*) his part (*to meros autou*) from the tree of life and the holy city.

Note: the best Greek manuscripts read "the tree of life" here, not "the book of life" as KJV renders it. The phrase aligns directly with 22:14 — those who wash their robes "may have right to the tree of life and may enter through the gates into the city." To tamper with the words of the book that promises these things is to forfeit precisely those promised realities. The punishment is not arbitrary divine anger but the natural consequence: one who suppresses or distorts the word that offers the tree of life and the holy city loses access to those very things.

"His part out of the holy city" — the word *meros* (part/share) suggests an allocated inheritance, a portion in the eschatological inheritance of the saints. To lose one's *meros* is to be placed with those "outside" (v. 15) — the mirror image of those who "enter through the gates" (v. 14). This framing makes the warning an inclusio around the final beatitude and exclusion list.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 22:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-14.htm); 21:27; [Deut. 4:2](https://biblehub.com/deuteronomy/4-2.htm); [Rev. 20:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-12.htm), 15.

### [Rev 22:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-20.htm) — "Surely I come quickly. Amen. Even so, come, Lord Jesus."

**Text**: "He which testifieth these things saith, Surely I come quickly. Amen. Even so, come, Lord Jesus."

**Exegesis**: The penultimate verse of the NT canon. Christ, the witness (*ho marturōn tauta* — recapitulating the role established in 1:5 — "the faithful witness"), makes his final declaration: *nai, erchomai tachu* — "Yes, I am coming quickly." The *nai* ("yes, indeed") is emphatic affirmation, stronger than mere assertion — it is a solemn *amen* from Christ himself.

John's response — *amēn, erchou kurie Iēsou* ("Amen. Come, Lord Jesus") — is the NT's rendering of the Aramaic *Maranatha* ("Our Lord, come"), which Paul cites in [1 Cor. 16:22](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-22.htm). This liturgical cry was the standard eschatological prayer of early Christianity (cf. Didache 10:6). It demonstrates that the book of Revelation functioned within the worshiping assembly — the communities for whom it was written received it as liturgy, responding to Christ's promise with longing prayer.

The exchange is the culmination of the Spirit-and-bride call of v. 17. The bride, having heard Christ's promise, echoes it back as prayer. This is the proper posture of the church awaiting the parousia: not passive waiting but active, vocal longing. "All those who come to the realization of the importance of the central message of Revelation will respond with a longing sigh: Amen. Come, Lord Jesus."

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-5.htm); 22:7, 12; [1 Cor. 16:22](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-22.htm); Didache 10:6.

### [Rev 22:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-21.htm) — "The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you all"

**Text**: "The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you all. Amen."

**Exegesis**: The book closes with an epistolary benediction, characteristic of Pauline letter endings ([Rom. 16:20](https://biblehub.com/romans/16-20.htm); [1 Cor. 16:23](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-23.htm); [2 Cor. 13:14](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/13-14.htm); [Gal. 6:18](https://biblehub.com/galatians/6-18.htm); [Phil. 4:23](https://biblehub.com/philippians/4-23.htm); [1 Thess. 5:28](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-28.htm)). This confirms what the prologue (1:4-5) established: Revelation is a letter to real churches facing real persecution. It begins with grace and peace from the triune God (1:4-5) and ends with grace from the Lord Jesus.

**"Grace"** (*charis*): The final word of comfort to people for whom the book's visions may have been terrifying as well as hopeful. Grace — unmerited divine favor — is the only foundation on which God's people can face the final events announced in the book. The future may appear frightful, but Christ's grace accompanies them ([Matt. 28:20](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-20.htm) — "I am with you always, to the end of the age"). Through grace the messages of Revelation were given; through grace its promises will be realized; through grace the faithful will reach the heavenly city.

**"With you all"** (*meta pantōn*): The benediction is for all — every hearer, every generation, every church. No one is excluded from the offer of grace. John's last word is not judgment but gift.

The entire arc of Chapter 22 — from the river of life and the tree of life, through the certification of the vision, through Christ's repeated "I come quickly," through the final warning and invitation, to the mutual exchange of promise and longing — ends on the keynote that began it all: grace. The book refuses to end with wrath; it ends with Christ's grace extended to all.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:4-5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-4.htm); [1 Cor. 16:23](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/16-23.htm); [2 Cor. 13:14](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/13-14.htm); [Matt. 28:20](https://biblehub.com/matthew/28-20.htm).
