## Revelation 21

**Overview**: Chapter 21 divides into two main sections: (1) vv. 1–8, the new heaven and earth, anchored by God's proclamation from the throne; (2) vv. 9–27, the detailed description of the New Jerusalem. The entire chapter stands as the positive counterpart to the Babylon vision of chs. 17–18: same angel, same Spirit-transportation, same vocabulary — but inverted. Where Babylon is the prostitute, Jerusalem is the bride; where Babylon destroys, Jerusalem gives life.

---

### [Rev 21:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-1.htm) — "And I saw a new heaven and a new earth"

**Text**: "And I saw a new heaven and a new earth: for the first heaven and the first earth were passed away; and there was no more sea."

**Exegesis**: The Greek word for "new" here is *kainos* (not *neos*). The distinction is critical: *neos* denotes something new in time, while *kainos* denotes new in quality or form — something that has become obsolete and is fundamentally replaced. John uses *kainos* four times in vv. 1–5, pressing the point that this is not a minor renovation of the old order but an ontological re-creation. Stefanovic: the new creation is "a re-creation of existing elements rather than a creation ex nihilo" (cf. [2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm)). Peter's language of a great conflagration in which "the heavens will pass away with a roar and the elements will be destroyed with intense heat" ([2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm), 13) precedes "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells." John stresses that the old earth, subjected to corruption through sin ([Rom. 8:19](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-19.htm)–22), is now fully replaced.

The primary OT allusion is [Isaiah 65:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/65-17.htm): "Behold, I create new heavens and a new earth; and the former things shall not be remembered or come to mind." John builds his whole vision on this prophetic promise. Peter echoes it ([2 Pet. 3:13](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-13.htm)). Sin had altered the original creation; God now liberates it from bondage to corruption ([Rom. 8:21](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-21.htm)).

**The sea is no more**: This is the first and startling observation. At the literal level, no great bodies of water will exist on the new earth. But the theological weight is metaphorical. In Jewish cosmology, the sea (*thalassa*) is the domain of chaos, evil, and the powers hostile to God (cf. [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm), where the beast rises "out of the sea"). For John, exiled on Patmos surrounded by the sea that separated him from his community ([Rev. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-9.htm)), the sea had become personally and symbolically freighted: "the metaphoric place of 'disturbed and stormy social and political conditions out of which tyrannies commonly arise'" (Stefanovic). Its absence on the new earth means the complete removal of every force that threatens, alienates, and destroys. All fears and hostilities are permanently ended.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 65:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/65-17.htm); [2 Pet. 3:10](https://biblehub.com/2_peter/3-10.htm)–13; [Rom. 8:19](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-19.htm)–22; [Rev. 13:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/13-1.htm); [Rev. 1:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-9.htm).

### [Rev 21:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-2.htm) — "And I John saw the holy city, new Jerusalem"

**Text**: "And I John saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down from God out of heaven, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband."

**Exegesis**: The identification "holy city" (*hagia polis*) deliberately echoes OT usage: the earthly Jerusalem was "the holy city" ([Isa. 52:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/52-1.htm); [Dan. 9:24](https://biblehub.com/daniel/9-24.htm); [Matt. 27:53](https://biblehub.com/matthew/27-53.htm)). The adjective *kainos* (new) signals that this city replaces the old: as the old earth is replaced by the new, so the old Jerusalem — which failed in its calling, was filled with the blood of prophets, and was condemned ([Matt. 23:37](https://biblehub.com/matthew/23-37.htm)) — is now superseded by this divine city. Badenas (cited by Stefanovic): the new Jerusalem becomes "to the new heaven and the new earth what the old Jerusalem never succeeded to be to Israel and the world."

The city "coming down from God out of heaven" (*katabaínousa ek tou ouranou apo tou theou*) is decisive: this is not a rebuilt Palestinian Jerusalem. It is the city "prepared by Christ in the heavenly places" ([John 14:1](https://biblehub.com/john/14-1.htm)–3), actualized on the new earth after the millennium (cf. [Rev. 20:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-9.htm)). The architect and builder is God himself ([Heb. 11:10](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/11-10.htm); 12:22). Its movement is downward — eschatological geography runs heaven-to-earth, not earth-to-heaven.

The bride image: the city "prepared as a bride adorned for her husband" (*hós nymphén kekosmémenen*) echoes [Isa. 52:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/52-1.htm) and [Isa. 49:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/49-18.htm) (Isaiah portrays restored Zion as a bride adorned with ornaments). In [Rev. 19:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-7.htm)–8, the bride is the church dressed in fine linen. In 21:9, the city is called "the bride, the wife of the Lamb." Saints and city are inseparable — both are arrayed as the bride. This stands in deliberate contrast to Babylon, "lavishly adorned with gold, pearls, and precious stones" ([Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm)) through self-glorification. New Jerusalem is adorned by God's glory.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 52:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/52-1.htm); 49:18; 62:5; [Dan. 9:24](https://biblehub.com/daniel/9-24.htm); [John 14:1](https://biblehub.com/john/14-1.htm)–3; [Heb. 11:10](https://biblehub.com/hebrews/11-10.htm), 16; 12:22; [Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm); 19:7–8; 20:9.

### [Rev 21:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-3.htm) — "And I heard a great voice out of heaven saying, Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men"

**Text**: "And I heard a great voice out of heaven saying, Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them, and be their God."

**Exegesis**: A heavenly voice makes the grand theological proclamation. "The tabernacle of God is with men" (*hé sképé tou theou meta tón anthrópón*) — *sképé* (tabernacle, dwelling, tent) is the key word. It echoes the Greek of [Lev. 26:11](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-11.htm)–12 and [Ezek. 37:27](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-27.htm): "My dwelling place also will be with them; and I will be their God, and they will be My people." In the OT, the tabernacle/temple was the institutional locus of God's presence among Israel ([Exod. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/exodus/25-8.htm); [Exod. 40:34](https://biblehub.com/exodus/40-34.htm)–35; [Lev. 9:23](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/9-23.htm)). When Israel was unfaithful, that presence was removed (Ezek. 10). [John 1:14](https://biblehub.com/john/1-14.htm) announces the provisional fulfillment: in the incarnate Word, God "tabernacled" (*eskénósen*) temporarily among humans, and "we beheld his glory." Now, at the consummation, God tabernacles permanently — no mediating institution, no curtain, no separation.

The plural "peoples" (*laoi*) is significant: John switches from the OT singular "people" (*laos*) to the plural, signaling the inclusion of all God's children from every nation, tribe, and tongue ([Rev. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-9.htm)). The covenant formula — "I will be their God, and they shall be my people" — inherited from [Jer. 30:22](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/30-22.htm); 31:33; [Ezek. 37:27](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-27.htm); [Lev. 26:12](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-12.htm) — now achieves its eschatological fullness, encompassing the entire redeemed humanity across all ages.

Centuries earlier, Ezekiel prophesied the new name of restored Jerusalem: "The LORD is there" (*YHWH shamma*, [Ezek. 48:35](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-35.htm)). That name now becomes the permanent reality: God himself is among his people always, without barrier.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/exodus/25-8.htm); 40:34–35; [Lev. 26:11](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/26-11.htm)–12; [Ezek. 37:27](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/37-27.htm); 43:7; 48:35; [John 1:14](https://biblehub.com/john/1-14.htm); [Rev. 7:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-9.htm); 21:16–27.

### [Rev 21:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-4.htm) — "And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes"

**Text**: "And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away."

**Exegesis**: This verse is the fullest enunciation of the negative catalog — everything eliminated in the new order. Every item is the direct consequence of the fall and of sin's corruption of creation.

"God shall wipe away all tears" (*exaleipsei pan dakryon*): God does this personally; the intimacy of the act — God wiping tears from every face — is not ceremonial but pastoral. This echoes [Isa. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/25-8.htm): "He will swallow up death for all time, and the Lord GOD will wipe tears away from all faces"; also [Isa. 35:10](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/35-10.htm); 65:19. It was already announced at [Rev. 7:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-15.htm)–17 (God spreading his tabernacle over the redeemed and wiping away tears) — now it is the enacted reality, not the promise.

"No more death" (*thanatos ouketi estai*): Stefanovic notes the Greek uses the definite article — *ho thanatos*, "the death" — personifying death as the great enemy of humanity. Paul: "The last enemy that will be abolished is the death" ([1 Cor. 15:26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-26.htm), lit.). John saw "Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire" ([Rev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-14.htm)). Now their elimination is confirmed by God's abiding presence, for "in the presence of Jesus, tears and death had no place" — as Mary and Martha knew: "Lord, if You had been here, my brother would not have died" ([John 11:21](https://biblehub.com/john/11-21.htm), 32).

"Neither sorrow, nor crying, nor pain": *penthos* (mourning/grief), *kraugé* (crying/wailing), *ponos* (pain/toil). These are the direct products of sin's curse on the earth (Gen. 3). "The former things have passed away" (*ta prota apélthan*) — the entire old order of sin, death, and corruption is definitively over. Paul's "the former things are passed away" finds its cosmic fulfillment ([1 Cor. 15:54](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-54.htm), RSV: "swallowed up in victory").

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/25-8.htm); 35:10; 65:19; Gen. 3; [1 Cor. 15:26](https://biblehub.com/1_corinthians/15-26.htm), 54; [John 11:21](https://biblehub.com/john/11-21.htm), 32; [Rev. 7:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-15.htm)–17; 20:14.

### [Rev 21:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-5.htm) — "And he that sat upon the throne said, Behold, I make all things new"

**Text**: "And he that sat upon the throne said, Behold, I make all things new. And he said unto me, Write: for these words are true and faithful."

**Exegesis**: For the first time in Revelation, God the Father speaks directly from the throne. The throne is the center of cosmic authority (Rev. 4–5); its occupant now pronounces the final creative act: *Idou kaina poió panta* — "Behold, I make all things new." The present tense (*poió*) may be ingressive-futuristic or describe the act as already underway. The word *kaina* again: qualitatively new.

The declaration fulfills [Isa. 43:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/43-18.htm)–19: "Remember not the former things, nor consider things of old. Behold, I am doing a new thing." Paul anticipated this new creation ([Rom. 8:21](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-21.htm); [2 Cor. 5:17](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/5-17.htm)); the entire created order, which "groans and suffers pain in travail" ([Rom. 8:22](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-22.htm)), will be "set free from its slavery to corruption into the freedom of the glory of the children of God" ([Rom. 8:21](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-21.htm)).

"Write: for these words are true and faithful" (*Gráyon, hoti houtoi hoi logoi pistoi kai aléthinoi eisin*): This command to write is solemn validation. The identical formula appears in [Rev. 19:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-9.htm) (the wedding supper of the Lamb) and recurs in 22:6. In Revelation, Christ is "the faithful and true" ([Rev. 19:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-11.htm); cf. 3:14). The trustworthiness of the promise is grounded in the character of the One making it. The prophetic words are as reliable as God himself.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 43:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/43-18.htm)–19; [Rom. 8:19](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-19.htm)–22; [2 Cor. 5:17](https://biblehub.com/2_corinthians/5-17.htm); [Rev. 4:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-2.htm); 19:9, 11; 22:6.

### [Rev 21:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-6.htm) — "And he said unto me, It is done. I am Alpha and Omega"

**Text**: "And he said unto me, It is done. I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end. I will give unto him that is athirst of the fountain of the water of life freely."

**Exegesis**: "It is done" (*Gegonen*): the Greek perfect tense — the act is completed with continuing results. This pronouncement echoes the "It is done" of [Rev. 16:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/16-17.htm) (the seventh bowl), signaling that the final judgment is complete and the restoration is sealed. The consummation of all things is announced from the throne.

"I am the Alpha and the Omega": *Egó eimi to Alpha kai to Ó*. This divine self-declaration frames the entire book ([Rev. 1:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-8.htm) opens it; 22:13 closes it). Alpha and Omega are the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet — God encompasses all of history and reality from beginning to end. He is also "the beginning and the end."

"The beginning" (*arché*): Greek *arché* carries multiple meanings — "beginning" in time, "ruler," "origin," "source," "first cause." Stefanovic argues the latter two apply here: God is the *source* and *origin* of all things (cf. the similar usage in [Rev. 3:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-14.htm) where Christ is "the *arché* of the creation of God"). "The end" (*telos*) likewise carries weight beyond temporal termination: it can mean "goal," "completion," or "rest." The meaning here is that God is the *goal* of all existence — "all life begins in God and ends in God" (Stefanovic). Paul: "For from him and through him and to him are all things" ([Rom. 11:36](https://biblehub.com/romans/11-36.htm)).

"I will give freely to the thirsty from the fountain of the water of life" (*dóréan*): *dóréan* means "as a gift, freely, without cost." This alludes to [Isa. 41:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-17.htm)–18 (God opening rivers on bare heights for the afflicted and needy who thirst) and [Isa. 55:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/55-1.htm) ("everyone who thirsts, come to the waters"). The Psalmist: "My soul thirsts for God, for the living God" ([Ps. 42:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/42-2.htm)). The thirst is a longing for God himself. The image anticipates the river of life in [Rev. 22:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-1.htm) and stands in deliberate contrast to "drinking from Babylon's wine of fornication" ([Rev. 17:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-2.htm)). All human thirst for salvation is quenched solely in God ([Matt. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/matthew/5-6.htm)).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 1:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-8.htm); 3:14; 16:17; 22:1, 13; [Isa. 41:17](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/41-17.htm)–18; 55:1; [Ps. 42:2](https://biblehub.com/psalms/42-2.htm); 63:1; [Rom. 11:36](https://biblehub.com/romans/11-36.htm); [Matt. 5:6](https://biblehub.com/matthew/5-6.htm).

### [Rev 21:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-7.htm) — "He that overcometh shall inherit all things"

**Text**: "He that overcometh shall inherit all things; and I will be his God, and he shall be my son."

**Exegesis**: The overcomer (*ho nikón*) — a key Revelation term appearing in every message to the seven churches (Rev. 2–3) — now receives the consolidated promise. "Shall inherit all things" (*kléronomései tauta*): "which things?" — all the *kaina* (new things) God has just declared. The inheritance is comprehensive.

The promises to overcomers in Rev. 2–3 find their totalized fulfillment here: access to the tree of life (2:7); escape from the second death (2:11); hidden manna, white stone, new name (2:17); authority over nations and the morning star (2:26–28); walk with Jesus, white garments, names retained in the book of life, acknowledged before the Father and angels (3:4–5); pillars in the temple never to leave, God's name written on them (3:12); seated with Christ on his throne (3:21). All of these — and more promised in the NT — flow together into the comprehensive inheritance.

"I will be his God, and he shall be my son" (*Egó essomai autó Theos kai autos estai moi huios*): The filial covenant formula — drawn from [2 Sam. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/7-14.htm) (the Davidic covenant: "I will be his father, and he shall be my son") and [Ps. 89:26](https://biblehub.com/psalms/89-26.htm)–27 — is now applied universally to every overcomer. The overcomers are children of God with full rights of heirs. This is the pinnacle of the covenant relationship.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 2:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/2-7.htm), 11, 17, 26–28; 3:4–5, 12, 21; [2 Sam. 7:14](https://biblehub.com/2_samuel/7-14.htm); [Ps. 89:26](https://biblehub.com/psalms/89-26.htm)–27; [Rom. 8:16](https://biblehub.com/romans/8-16.htm)–17.

### [Rev 21:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-8.htm) — "But the fearful, and unbelieving, and the abominable"

**Text**: "But the fearful, and unbelieving, and the abominable, and murderers, and whoremongers, and sorcerers, and idolaters, and all liars, shall have their part in the lake which burneth with fire and brimstone: which is the second death."

**Exegesis**: The contrast to v. 7 is stark. Those who followed the satanic trinity (Rev. 13; 16–19) are excluded from the family of God. Stefanovic arranges this list in light of the whole book:

**The cowardly** (*hoi deiloi*): Greek *deilía* is not natural timidity but "a lack of genuine commitment." These are those who, to escape persecution or inconvenience in the day of trial, denied Christ and chose personal safety over faithfulness (cf. [Matt. 13:21](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-21.htm); [John 12:42](https://biblehub.com/john/12-42.htm)–43). In the context of Rev.'s final crisis, they capitulate to the satanic trinity rather than endure with the saints ([Rev. 14:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-12.htm): "the endurance of the saints").

**The unfaithful** (*apistoi*): those who, in the final crisis, slipped away from faith in Christ — antithetical to the saints who "keep the faith of Jesus" ([Rev. 14:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-12.htm)).

**The abominable** (*ebdelygmenoi*): those steeped in the filth of Babylon's cup ([Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm)–5).

**Murderers, whoremongers/fornicators, sorcerers, idolaters, liars**: These are the commandment-breakers standing in direct contrast to the saints who "keep the commandments of God" ([Rev. 14:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-12.htm)). In [Rev. 9:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-20.htm)–21, in the scene of the sixth trumpet (preparation for the final crisis), the wicked refuse to repent of their sorceries, murders, fornication, and thefts, and continue worshiping demons. Their sin-hardened hearts seal their exclusion.

"Their part will be in the lake which burns with fire and sulphur, which is the second death": *hé meros autón en té limné té kaiomené pyri kai theió* — the second death (*ho thanatos ho deuteros*, [Rev. 20:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-14.htm); cf. 20:6). While God's people have "a part in the first resurrection" ([Rev. 20:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-6.htm)), the wicked are consigned to the second death. Contrast is total: the overcomers *inherit*; the wicked *receive their part* — in destruction.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 9:20](https://biblehub.com/revelation/9-20.htm)–21; 13; 14:12; 17:4–5; 20:6, 14–15; [Matt. 13:21](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-21.htm); [John 12:42](https://biblehub.com/john/12-42.htm)–43.

### [Rev 21:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-9.htm) — "And there came unto me one of the seven angels"

**Text**: "And there came unto me one of the seven angels which had the seven vials full of the seven last plagues, and talked with me, saying, Come hither, I will shew thee the bride, the Lamb's wife."

**Exegesis**: The same formula opens the Babylon vision ([Rev. 17:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-1.htm): "one of the seven angels who had the seven bowls came and spoke with me"). It is almost certainly the same angel, now commissioned for the antithetical vision. The deliberate parallelism is structural: in [Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm), John was carried to the wilderness to see the prostitute Babylon; in 21:10, he is carried to a great and high mountain to see the bride Jerusalem. The two visions are designed as a diptych — each illuminating the other by contrast.

The angel's invitation: "Come, I will show you the bride, the wife of the Lamb" (*tén gynaika tou Arniou*). In [Rev. 19:7](https://biblehub.com/revelation/19-7.htm)–8, the "bride" is the church arrayed in fine linen. Here church and city merge — the community of the redeemed and the city they inhabit are inseparably identified. The OT background: [Isa. 62:5](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/62-5.htm) ("as the bridegroom rejoices over the bride, so your God will rejoice over you"); [Isa. 49:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/49-18.htm) (restored Jerusalem adorned as a bride).

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 17:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-1.htm), 3; 19:7–8; [Isa. 49:18](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/49-18.htm); 62:5.

### [Rev 21:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-10.htm) — "And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain"

**Text**: "And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem, descending out of heaven from God,"

**Exegesis**: "Carried away in the Spirit" (*apénegken me en pneumati*) — identical phrase to [Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm) (Babylon vision). John is in a visionary state. The *great and high mountain* (*oros mega kai hypsélón*): Greek *oros* can mean mountain, mount, or hill, and even a desert place (cf. [Matt. 18:12](https://biblehub.com/matthew/18-12.htm)). Stefanovic makes a distinctive historical argument: this is likely a large artificial mound (*tell*) formed from the ruins and debris of destroyed end-time Babylon. The practice of building cities on the rubble of destroyed predecessors was well-known ([Josh. 11:13](https://biblehub.com/joshua/11-13.htm); [Jer. 30:18](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/30-18.htm): the city "rebuilt on its ruin [tell]"). The parallel with Ezekiel is exact: God carried Ezekiel to "a very high mount" (*[Ezek. 40:2](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/40-2.htm)*) to show him the restored Jerusalem built on the ruins of the destroyed city.

The theological message: "It is over the ruins of 'the proud, evil, and corrupted Babylon' that New Jerusalem rises 'pure and radiant with the glory of God'" (Stefanovic). Babylon's judgment ([Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm) — wilderness) and Jerusalem's establishment ([Rev. 21:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-10.htm) — mountain) may share the same location. Evil's ruin becomes the foundation on which God's city is established.

The city "descending out of heaven from God" reaffirms v. 2: this is not an earthly construction but a divine gift, the fulfillment of [John 14:1](https://biblehub.com/john/14-1.htm)–3.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 17:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-3.htm); [Ezek. 40:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/40-1.htm)–2; [Josh. 11:13](https://biblehub.com/joshua/11-13.htm); [Jer. 30:18](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/30-18.htm); [John 14:1](https://biblehub.com/john/14-1.htm)–3.

### [Rev 21:11](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-11.htm) — "Having the glory of God: and her light was like unto a stone most precious"

**Text**: "Having the glory of God: and her light was like unto a stone most precious, even like a jasper stone, clear as crystal;"

**Exegesis**: The *glory of God* (*doxa tou theou*) is the defining characteristic of the city. In the OT, the *kabod YHWH* (glory of the LORD) was the visible manifestation of God's presence — in the tabernacle ([Exod. 40:34](https://biblehub.com/exodus/40-34.htm)–35), in the temple ([Lev. 9:23](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/9-23.htm)), and in Ezekiel's vision of the restored temple ([Ezek. 43:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/43-1.htm)–5). The departure of that glory was the sign of divine abandonment (Ezek. 10); its return to the new Jerusalem (Ezek. 43) is now finally, permanently realized.

"Her radiance" (*phóstér*) — Greek for "luminary" or "radiance," something from which light is radiated. Human language is inadequate to convey it; John reaches for the best analog: like a *jasper stone* (*lithe iaspidi*) *clear as crystal* (*krystalló*). The jasper appears also in [Rev. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-3.htm) to describe the one on the throne. The same stone dominates the wall in v. 18. The clarity of crystal adds the quality of transparency — this glory does not hide but radiates outward.

The glory of God in the new Jerusalem makes the sun and moon unnecessary (v. 23; 22:5) — a direct fulfillment of [Isa. 60:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm)–20.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 40:34](https://biblehub.com/exodus/40-34.htm)–35; [Lev. 9:23](https://biblehub.com/leviticus/9-23.htm); [Ezek. 43:1](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/43-1.htm)–5; [Rev. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-3.htm); 21:23; 22:5; [Isa. 60:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm)–20.

### [Rev 21:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-12.htm) — "And had a wall great and high, and had twelve gates"

**Text**: "And had a wall great and high, and had twelve gates, and at the gates twelve angels, and names written thereon, which are the names of the twelve tribes of the children of Israel:"

**Exegesis**: The city's description is now modeled on ancient walled cities, but all motifs are drawn from the OT and specifically from Ezekiel's restored temple vision (Ezek. 40–48).

**The great and high wall**: Ezekiel's restored temple was surrounded by a high wall ([Ezek. 40:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/40-5.htm)). The wall of new Jerusalem is not for military defense — all enemies have been destroyed. It is a symbol of security and safety. Zechariah's word to restored Jerusalem: "'For I,' declares the Lord, 'will be a wall of fire around her, and I will be the glory in her midst'" ([Zech. 2:5](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/2-5.htm)). God himself is the ultimate security.

**Twelve gates**: Patterned directly on Ezekiel's vision of twelve gates in the restored Jerusalem ([Ezek. 48:31](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-31.htm)–35). The twelve gates symbolize universality: Jesus foresaw that "many will come from east and west, and from north and south, and will recline at the table in the kingdom of God" ([Luke 13:29](https://biblehub.com/luke/13-29.htm)). The new Jerusalem is a universal city; everyone has unlimited, unhindered access to God.

**Twelve angels at the gates**: Watchers (*phylakes*) stationed at each gate. Ref. to [Isa. 62:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/62-6.htm)–7: "On your walls, O Jerusalem, I have appointed watchmen; all day and all night they will never keep silent." Nothing unclean or abominable will enter (cf. v. 27). Beale: "the entire people of God redeemed throughout the ages will experience consummate security in the inviolable new creation because of God's permanent and consummate presence there."

**Names of the twelve tribes of Israel**: Following [Ezek. 48:31](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-31.htm)–35 where each gate bore a tribal name. The twelve tribes represent the OT people of God.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 40:5](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/40-5.htm); 48:31–35; [Zech. 2:5](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/2-5.htm); [Isa. 62:6](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/62-6.htm)–7; [Luke 13:29](https://biblehub.com/luke/13-29.htm); [Rev. 21:27](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-27.htm).

### [Rev 21:13](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-13.htm) — "On the east three gates; on the north three gates; on the south three gates; and on the west three gates"

**Text**: "On the east three gates; on the north three gates; on the south three gates; and on the west three gates."

**Exegesis**: The fourfold arrangement — three gates on each of the four compass points — is directly drawn from [Ezek. 48:31](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-31.htm)–34, where the twelve gates of restored Jerusalem are similarly arranged, three per side. The four directions comprehensively represent the whole world: north, south, east, west — universal geography, universal access. Jesus' prophecy ([Luke 13:29](https://biblehub.com/luke/13-29.htm)) of people coming "from east and west, from north and south" to recline at the table in God's kingdom is here architecturally instantiated.

The arrangement also evokes the encampment of Israel in the wilderness around the tabernacle, where the tribes camped on all four sides (Num. 2). The new Jerusalem is the eschatological tabernacle (v. 3), now opened universally rather than restricted to one nation.

**Cross-references**: [Ezek. 48:31](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-31.htm)–34; Num. 2; [Luke 13:29](https://biblehub.com/luke/13-29.htm).

### [Rev 21:14](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-14.htm) — "And the wall of the city had twelve foundations, and in them the names of the twelve apostles of the Lamb"

**Text**: "And the wall of the city had twelve foundations, and in them the names of the twelve apostles of the Lamb."

**Exegesis**: The twelve foundations (*themelíoi dódeka*) bearing the names of the twelve apostles represent the NT people of God. Paul: the church is "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Jesus himself as the cornerstone" ([Eph. 2:20](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-20.htm)). The apostles are not merely historical figures but the foundational witnesses whose testimony constitutes the church.

The juxtaposition is theologically rich: twelve gates with the names of the twelve tribes of Israel (OT covenant people) + twelve foundations with the names of the twelve apostles (NT covenant community). John is portraying new Jerusalem as the city of God's *total* covenant people — OT and NT together. This same symbolism appears in the twenty-four elders of [Rev. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-4.htm) (12 + 12) as "symbolic representatives of all the redeemed and faithful people of God," and in the 144,000 of [Rev. 7:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-4.htm)–8.

**Cross-references**: [Eph. 2:20](https://biblehub.com/ephesians/2-20.htm); [Rev. 4:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-4.htm); 7:4–8; [Matt. 16:18](https://biblehub.com/matthew/16-18.htm).

### [Rev 21:15](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-15.htm) — "And he that talked with me had a golden reed to measure the city"

**Text**: "And he that talked with me had a golden reed to measure the city, and the gates thereof, and the wall thereof."

**Exegesis**: The golden measuring rod (*kalamos chrysous*) recalls Ezekiel's vision of the measuring figure with a rod (Ezek. 40–42), and John's own earlier act of measuring the temple in [Rev. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm)–2. These parallels reinforce the identification of new Jerusalem as the eschatological temple — the place where God dwells with his people.

The act of measuring in apocalyptic literature signals divine ownership, protection, and the certainty of God's promise. Stefanovic: "the act of measuring here 'and the stating of measurements are doubtless to give assurance of the adequacy and sufficiency of the heavenly home.'" The measurements are not architectural specs but theological assurances: the city is precisely what God says it is — sufficient, complete, secure.

**Cross-references**: Ezek. 40–42; [Rev. 11:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-1.htm)–2; [Zech. 2:1](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/2-1.htm)–2.

### [Rev 21:16](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-16.htm) — "And the city lieth foursquare, and the length is as large as the breadth"

**Text**: "And the city lieth foursquare, and the length is as large as the breadth: and he measured the city with the reed, twelve thousand furlongs. The length and the breadth and the height of it are equal."

**Exegesis**: The city is a perfect *cube* — length, breadth, and height all equal at 12,000 stadia. A *stadion* (furlong) was approximately 606 feet or 185 meters, making 12,000 stadia roughly 1,400 miles or 2,200 kilometers per side.

The *cube* is the crucial geometric symbolism: the Holy of Holies in Solomon's temple was a perfect cube — 20 × 20 × 20 cubits ([1 Kings 6:20](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-20.htm)). The new Jerusalem *is* the Holy of Holies — the very inner sanctum of divine presence, now expanded to cosmic scale. In the ancient temple, only the high priest entered the Holy of Holies, and only once a year. In the new Jerusalem, "the privilege of being in the immediate presence of God is now granted to all of God's people" (cf. [Rev. 22:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-3.htm)–4).

The number 12,000: each of the twelve edges of the cube = 12,000 stadia. Total edge length = 144,000 stadia (12 × 12,000). Stefanovic: 144,000 is "the number of totality of God's people" ([Rev. 7:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-4.htm)), pointing to the universality of the city as the home of both OT and NT redeemed. The city's dimensions encode the full number of God's people.

The city is also *foursquare*: Schüssler Fiorenza noted that Herodotus described ancient Babylon as "an exact square, a hundred and twenty furlongs in length each way." John may be modeling new Jerusalem's description after Babylon — consciously presenting it as the *anti-image* of Babylon (the anti-city, the true city), recognizable to readers familiar with Herodotus.

**Cross-references**: [1 Kings 6:20](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-20.htm); [Rev. 7:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-4.htm); 14:1–5; 22:3–4; [Ezek. 48:20](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/48-20.htm); Herodotus (Babylon description).

### [Rev 21:17](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-17.htm) — "And he measured the wall thereof, an hundred and forty and four cubits"

**Text**: "And he measured the wall thereof, an hundred and forty and four cubits, according to the measure of a man, that is, of the angel."

**Exegesis**: 144 cubits (*pécheís*) for the wall. A cubit was approximately half a yard or 44.5 cm, making 144 cubits roughly 72 yards or 64 meters. This is either the height or the thickness of the wall. Since John already described the wall as "great and high" (v. 12) and the city height as 12,000 stadia, Stefanovic argues 144 cubits refers to the wall's *thickness* — an immense thickness signifying impenetrable security, not structural necessity (since there are no enemies).

The number 144 = 12 × 12, a compound of the number of God's people multiplied by itself — the totality of totalities. Symbolically, the wall thickness = the full number of God's redeemed, who together constitute the impregnable security of the city.

"According to a human measurement, which is an angelic measurement": The statement is deliberately obscure. It may indicate the measurements are real by both human and angelic standards simultaneously — or that the angel uses ordinary human units of measurement to make the vision comprehensible, stressing both the literal and the symbolic dimensions of the city's grandeur.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 7:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/7-4.htm) (144,000); [Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm).

### [Rev 21:18](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-18.htm) — "And the building of the wall of it was of jasper: and the city was pure gold, like unto clear glass"

**Text**: "And the building of the wall of it was of jasper: and the city was pure gold, like unto clear glass."

**Exegesis**: The materials of the city radiate the glory of God. The wall is jasper (*iaspis*) — the same stone associated with God's glory in 4:3 and the city's radiance in v. 11. The entire wall glows with divine glory.

The city itself (*hé polis hé chrysé katharé hyalos*): pure gold (*chrysion katharon*) like clear glass (*hyalos*). The paradox — gold transparent as glass — defies natural categories. This is not ordinary gold; it is gold transformed by divine glory into something luminous and translucent. Josephus described Herod's temple as covered "all over with plates of gold of great weight" which at sunrise "reflected back a very fiery splendor" so intense that observers had to turn their eyes away. John's vision far surpasses even that: the gold here is not just reflective but transparent — it does not merely reflect light, it transmits it.

The street of pure gold (*v. 21*) directly contrasts "the street of the great city" where the bodies of the two witnesses were displayed ([Rev. 11:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-8.htm)) — the street of oppression becomes the street of glory; God's persecuted people are vindicated.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-3.htm); 11:8; [1 Kings 6:30](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-30.htm) (Solomon's temple floor overlaid with gold); Josephus, *Jewish War* 5.222.

### [Rev 21:19](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-19.htm)–20 — "And the foundations of the wall of the city were garnished with all manner of precious stones"

**Text**: "And the foundations of the wall of the city were garnished with all manner of precious stones. The first foundation was jasper; the second, sapphire; the third, a chalcedony; the fourth, an emerald; The fifth, sardonyx; the sixth, sardius; the seventh, chrysolyte; the eighth, beryl; the ninth, a topaz; the tenth, a chrysoprasus; the eleventh, a jacinth; the twelfth, an amethyst."

**Exegesis**: The twelve foundation stones correspond to the twelve precious stones on the breastplate (*chóshen*) of the OT high priest ([Exod. 28:17](https://biblehub.com/exodus/28-17.htm)–20; 39:10–13), upon which the names of the twelve tribes of Israel were engraved. Only eight of the twelve stones appear in both lists, leading scholars to suggest John may have made his own translation of stone names from Hebrew into Greek (explaining variations in identification).

The breastplate connection is theologically significant: the high priest bore the names of all Israel close to his heart when he entered the presence of God in the tabernacle. The new Jerusalem's foundations bear the names of the twelve apostles (v. 14) — the stones of the apostolic witness are the gemstones upon which the entire city rests. The priestly imagery is transferred: where once the high priest alone entered God's presence bearing Israel's names, now the entire city — bearing the apostolic foundation — dwells permanently in God's presence.

Structurally, this passage also fulfills [Isa. 54:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/54-11.htm)–12: "Behold, I will set your stones in antimony, and your foundations I will lay in sapphires. Moreover, I will make your battlements of rubies, and your gates of crystal, and your entire wall of precious stones." The apocryphal book of Tobit (13:16–17) uses nearly identical imagery: "For Jerusalem will be built with sapphires and emeralds, her walls with precious stones... The streets of Jerusalem will be paved with beryl and ruby and stones of Ophir." John's vision fulfills the full spectrum of Jewish eschatological hopes about the glorified Jerusalem.

**Cross-references**: [Exod. 28:17](https://biblehub.com/exodus/28-17.htm)–20; 39:10–13; [Isa. 54:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/54-11.htm)–12; Tobit 13:16–17.

### [Rev 21:21](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-21.htm) — "And the twelve gates were twelve pearls"

**Text**: "And the twelve gates were twelve pearls: every several gate was of one pearl: and the street of the city was pure gold, as it were transparent glass."

**Exegesis**: Each gate is a single pearl (*margarités*) of enormous size — each gate is *one pearl*. Pearls were among the most costly commodities in the ancient world ([Matt. 13:45](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-45.htm)–46: the kingdom of heaven is like "one pearl of great price"). Each of the twelve gates into the city — representing universal access from all directions (v. 13) — is an individual pearl of incomprehensible size. This hyperbolically conveys that access to God's city is of infinite, incalculable value, and yet it is freely given (v. 6).

"The street of the city was pure gold, as it were transparent glass" (*ho plateia tés poleós chrysion katharon hós hyalos diaugés*): the singular *plateia* (street/broad way) suggests the main street. It is pure gold of absolute transparency. As noted above, this contrasts with the street of [Rev. 11:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-8.htm) where the two witnesses' corpses were exposed. The floor of Solomon's temple was overlaid with gold ([1 Kings 6:30](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-30.htm)) — another temple typology.

The cumulative effect of vv. 18–21 is overwhelming: jasper walls, gold city, jeweled foundations, pearl gates, gold street — an indescribable radiance that goes beyond material wealth into the realm of divine glory. The city is the manifested presence of God, not a display of luxury.

**Cross-references**: [Rev. 11:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/11-8.htm); [1 Kings 6:30](https://biblehub.com/1_kings/6-30.htm); [Matt. 13:45](https://biblehub.com/matthew/13-45.htm)–46; [Isa. 54:12](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/54-12.htm) ("your gates of crystal").

### [Rev 21:22](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-22.htm) — "And I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it"

**Text**: "And I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it."

**Exegesis**: This is one of the most theologically pregnant statements in the entire chapter. After describing the city's temple-characteristics at length — the cubic shape of the Holy of Holies, the wall of God's glory, the twelve precious stones of the high priest's breastplate — John announces: there is *no temple*.

The reason: *ho gar Kyrios ho Theos ho Pantokrátor naos autés estin kai to Arnion* — "For the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb are its temple." The temple was always a mediating structure — a place where God's presence could be *encountered* at defined points (the tabernacle, the mercy seat, the Holy of Holies). The temple existed because of the separation between God and sinful humanity. Now that separation is ended. The entire city is the temple because God's presence fills it without mediation, without curtain, without restriction.

"The Lord God the Almighty" (*Pantokrátor*): the divine title used throughout Revelation for the God of ultimate sovereign power. The pairing of "the Almighty" and "the Lamb" — sovereign might and sacrificial love — together constitute the temple. God's power and Christ's redemption are the ground on which the redeemed dwell in God's presence forever.

The statement also fulfills the implication of [John 2:19](https://biblehub.com/john/2-19.htm)–21 where Jesus identified his body as the temple. In the new Jerusalem, the divine presence — incarnate in the risen, glorified Christ — is the city's temple.

**Cross-references**: [John 2:19](https://biblehub.com/john/2-19.htm)–21; [Rev. 4:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/4-8.htm) (*Pantokrátor*); 21:3; [Exod. 25:8](https://biblehub.com/exodus/25-8.htm); [Ezek. 43:7](https://biblehub.com/ezekiel/43-7.htm).

### [Rev 21:23](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-23.htm) — "And the city had no need of the sun, neither of the moon, to shine in it"

**Text**: "And the city had no need of the sun, neither of the moon, to shine in it: for the glory of God did lighten it, and the Lamb is the light thereof."

**Exegesis**: The fulfillment of [Isa. 60:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm)–20: "No longer will you have the sun for light by day, nor for brightness will the moon give you light; but you will have the Lord for an everlasting light, and your God for your glory. Your sun will set no more, neither will your moon wane; for you will have the Lord for an everlasting light, and the days of your mourning will be finished."

The sun and moon are not destroyed — they are simply unnecessary. God's glory (*hé doxa tou theou ephótisen autén*) — the *kabod* radiating from his presence — outshines every created light source. "The Lamb is its lamp" (*to Arnion ho lychnos autés*): Christ is the *lamp* through which divine glory shines. The imagery points back to [Rev. 1:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-12.htm)–16 (Christ among the lampstands, his face shining like the sun in full strength) and forward to [Rev. 22:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/22-5.htm) (no need for lamp or sun because the Lord God illumines them).

This is more than a geographical or astronomical fact. In biblical theology, light is life, presence, and righteousness ([Ps. 36:9](https://biblehub.com/psalms/36-9.htm); [John 1:4](https://biblehub.com/john/1-4.htm)–5; 8:12). God's light in the city means the permanent, unmediated presence of divine life among the redeemed. There is no spiritual darkness, no ignorance, no absence of God.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 60:19](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-19.htm)–20; [Rev. 1:12](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-12.htm)–16; 22:5; [Ps. 36:9](https://biblehub.com/psalms/36-9.htm); [John 1:4](https://biblehub.com/john/1-4.htm)–5; 8:12.

### [Rev 21:24](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-24.htm) — "And the nations of them which are saved shall walk in the light of it"

**Text**: "And the nations of them which are saved shall walk in the light of it: and the kings of the earth do bring their glory and honour into it."

**Exegesis**: "The nations" (*ta ethné*) and "the kings of the earth" (*hoi basileis tés gés*): throughout Revelation, these terms have largely carried negative connotations — the nations deceived by Babylon ([Rev. 18:3](https://biblehub.com/revelation/18-3.htm)), the kings allied with the beast ([Rev. 17:2](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-2.htm); 19:19). Their redemptive presence here is striking.

Stefanovic argues these are "most likely the redeemed of the entire world" (cf. [Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:9; 7:9 — where the Lamb has redeemed people "from every tribe and tongue and people and nation" to be "kings and priests"). The OT background: [Isa. 60:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-3.htm) ("Nations will come to your light, and kings to the brightness of your rising"); [Isa. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/2-2.htm)–4; 56:6–7; [Jer. 3:17](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-17.htm); [Zech. 8:21](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/8-21.htm)–23 — all spoke of nations and kings streaming to Jerusalem to worship. The apocryphal Tobit: "Many nations will come from afar to the name of the Lord God, bearing gifts in their hands, gifts for the King of heaven."

The kings "bring their glory into it": *tén doxan autón eisoísousin eis autén* — the glory and honor of redeemed humanity is consecrated to God. This is total lordship: every achievement, every dignity, every human glory is brought into the city and offered to God. The eschatological Jerusalem fulfills all OT prophetic dreams about the earthly Jerusalem as the center of world worship.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 2:2](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/2-2.htm)–4; 60:3; 56:6–7; [Jer. 3:17](https://biblehub.com/jeremiah/3-17.htm); [Zech. 8:21](https://biblehub.com/zechariah/8-21.htm)–23; [Rev. 1:6](https://biblehub.com/revelation/1-6.htm); 5:9; 7:9; 17:2; 19:19.

### [Rev 21:25](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-25.htm) — "And the gates of it shall not be shut at all by day: for there shall be no night there"

**Text**: "And the gates of it shall not be shut at all by day: for there shall be no night there."

**Exegesis**: City gates in antiquity were shut at night for protection against enemies and at day when threatened. The gates of new Jerusalem are *never shut*. The reason is twofold:

1. There are no enemies — all opposing powers have been destroyed ([Rev. 20:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-10.htm), 14–15).
2. There is no night (*nyx ouk estai ekei*) — and therefore no temporal marker for gate-shutting.

The primary allusion is [Isa. 60:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-11.htm): "Your gates will be open continually, they will not be closed day or night, so that men may bring to you the wealth of the nations, with their kings led in procession." The open gates symbolize permanent, unhindered access to God's presence. Stefanovic: the open gates make possible for all people "immediate and unhindered access to the presence of God."

The absence of night is both literal (no darkness because God's glory illumines — v. 23) and symbolic (no evil, no death, no absence of God). Night in OT and NT is associated with danger, evil, spiritual darkness ([John 9:4](https://biblehub.com/john/9-4.htm); [1 Thess. 5:5](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-5.htm)–7). Its absence confirms the total elimination of every form of opposition to God and life.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 60:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-11.htm); [Rev. 20:10](https://biblehub.com/revelation/20-10.htm), 14–15; 22:5; [John 9:4](https://biblehub.com/john/9-4.htm); [1 Thess. 5:5](https://biblehub.com/1_thessalonians/5-5.htm)–7.

### [Rev 21:26](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-26.htm) — "And they shall bring the glory and honour of the nations into it"

**Text**: "And they shall bring the glory and honour of the nations into it."

**Exegesis**: This verse repeats and amplifies v. 24. The repetition is deliberate emphasis. "The glory and the honor of the nations" (*tén doxan kai tén timén tón ethnón*) — all that is noble, excellent, and dignified among the peoples of the redeemed earth is consecrated to the city. This is not tribute exacted under coercion but the free, joyful offering of redeemed humanity's best to God.

Isaiah envisioned: "so that men may bring to you the wealth of the nations... to beautify the place of My sanctuary; and I shall make the place of My feet glorious" ([Isa. 60:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-11.htm)b–13). What in Isaiah was the ideal of restored earthly Jerusalem is now the permanent reality of the new Jerusalem. The repetition of this theme — nations and kings bringing their glory in — underscores the universal character of the city: it is not the exclusive possession of any ethnic or national group but the gathering place of all redeemed humanity.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 60:11](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/60-11.htm)–13; v. 24; [Rev. 5:9](https://biblehub.com/revelation/5-9.htm); 7:9.

### [Rev 21:27](https://biblehub.com/revelation/21-27.htm) — "And there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth"

**Text**: "And there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth, neither whatsoever worketh abomination, or maketh a lie: but they which are written in the Lamb's book of life."

**Exegesis**: The chapter concludes with a final exclusion list, balancing v. 8's list and forming an inclusio on the theme of holiness and exclusion. The double negative in Greek (*ou mé eiselthé*: "shall in no wise enter") is emphatic: absolute exclusion.

Three categories excluded:
1. *Pan koinón* — anything unclean/defiling. OT purity law forbade the unclean from entering the temple (Lev. 21–22; [Num. 5:3](https://biblehub.com/numbers/5-3.htm)). Since new Jerusalem *is* the temple of God (v. 22), the same principle applies universally.
2. *Ho poión bdelygma* — those practicing abomination (*bdelygma*): the same word used for the "abominations" in Babylon's cup ([Rev. 17:4](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-4.htm)–5). These are those who "drank from Babylon's golden cup filled with abominations" — the end-time deceived.
3. *Pseudos* — those who practice lying. Standing in contrast to God's "faithful and true" words (v. 5). Satan is "the father of lies" ([John 8:44](https://biblehub.com/john/8-44.htm)); those conformed to Satan's character have no place in the city of truth.

Isaiah's prophecy: "For the uncircumcised and the unclean will no more come into you" ([Isa. 52:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/52-1.htm)). And [Isa. 4:3](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/4-3.htm): "everyone who is recorded for life in Jerusalem will be called holy." The criterion for inclusion is singular: *written in the Lamb's book of life* (*en tó bibliō tés zóés tou Arniou*). This book appears throughout Revelation (3:5; 13:8; 17:8; 20:12, 15) as the register of those who belong to Christ — those who refused the beast's mark and bore God's name ([Rev. 14:1](https://biblehub.com/revelation/14-1.htm); 15:2). Loyalty to the Lamb is the sole condition of entrance.

The contrast with Babylon is explicit: Babylon's inhabitants are those "whose names are not written in the book of life of the Lamb" ([Rev. 17:8](https://biblehub.com/revelation/17-8.htm)); Jerusalem's inhabitants are those whose names *are* written there (21:27). Total inversion.

**Cross-references**: [Isa. 52:1](https://biblehub.com/isaiah/52-1.htm); 4:3; Lev. 21–22; [Num. 5:3](https://biblehub.com/numbers/5-3.htm); [John 8:44](https://biblehub.com/john/8-44.htm); [Rev. 3:5](https://biblehub.com/revelation/3-5.htm); 13:8; 14:1; 17:4–5, 8; 20:12, 15.

## Summary of Theological Architecture of Chapter 21

Chapter 21 presents the eschatological telos of all Revelation's narrative: the elimination of the old order (sea, death, sorrow, pain), the permanent arrival of God's dwelling with humanity, and the consummation of all OT covenant promises. The New Jerusalem is simultaneously:
- The restored Garden of Eden (river, tree of life — ch. 22; no curse)
- The fulfilled Ezekielian temple (measuring rod, cubic shape = Holy of Holies, high wall, twelve gates, foundations, God's glory)
- The anti-Babylon (same angel, Spirit-transport, precious stones, nations and kings — but inverted: bride not prostitute, life-water not wine of fornication, God's presence not demons)
- The fulfillment of Isaiah's new Jerusalem oracles (chs. 40, 43, 52, 54, 60, 65)
- The home of the totality of God's redeemed — OT (twelve tribes on gates) + NT (twelve apostles on foundations) — the 144,000, the innumerable multitude, "every nation and tribe and tongue"

The chapter's movement is from cosmic re-creation (vv. 1–2) through divine proclamation (vv. 3–8) to visionary description (vv. 9–27), ending with the absolute holiness criterion: only those written in the Lamb's book of life dwell there. The entire chapter is the answer to every tear, every death, every persecution, every exile that Revelation's first readers experienced — God's final, permanent, glorious "Yes."
